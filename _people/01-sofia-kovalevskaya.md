<p>Kovalevskaya was a nineteenth-century Russian mathematician, known mainly for her achievements in mechanics and the theory of PDEs. She gained qualifications and held a number of posts which were traditionally reserved only for men and was amongst the first women to work as an editor of a scientific journal<a href="#fn2" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref2" role="doc-noteref"><sup>2</sup></a>. Probably her most substantial mathematical contribution was the Cauchy-Kovalevskaya theorem, which belongs to PDE theory. PDEs (partial differential equations) are equations which model the rate of change of a quantity with respect to two more other quantities which are also changing, whereas ODEs (ordinary differential equations) model the rate of change with respect to one quantity only (usually a time variable). All of science, engineering and applied mathematics depends on differential equations. PDEs were originally brought into being as tools to describe physical phenomena such as wave motion and heat flow (a role which they still play), but over time PDE theory has also developed in another direction into a rich branch of mathematical theory in its own right (without completely losing sight of the physical origin of the equations).</p>
<p>A famous and well-studied example is the one-dimensional wave equation:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cfrac%7B%5Cpartial%5E%7B2%7Df%7D%7B%5Cpartial%20t%5E%7B2%7D%7D%20%3D%20c%5E%7B2%7D%5Cfrac%7B%5Cpartial%5E%7B2%7Df%7D%7B%5Cpartial%20x%5E%7B2%7D%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>One sees that there are no mixed derivatives in this expression (ie. derivatives with respect to more than one variable), but it should also be pointed out that mixed derivatives can in fact occur in equations which model physical processes: there are versions of the diffusion equation which contain mixed derivative terms, for example. A very interesting development in the twentieth century was the notion that one might be able to ‘weaken’ the notion of a solution to obtain weak solutions which exist where our traditional idea of a solution does not exist: this is rather surprising from the viewpoint of classical PDE theory, since physical intuition would seem to tell us that the notion of a solution has to be a concrete one which cannot be weakened in this way.</p>
<p>Essentially, we take a PDE and rewrite it so that it is in ‘weak’ form: a solution to this equation is known as a weak solution to the original PDE. A strong solution is automatically a weak solution, but it is unfortunately not always obvious that a weak solution should also be strong. I should point out that the act of rewriting an equation is not necessarily by itself a profound one. The notion of having an equation or solution with particular types of operator in it and rewriting it so those operators do not appear is fairly common and trivial. As an example, one can obtain a fundamental solution to a particular system of equations modelling dilute gas flow, where the solution represents the physical pressure due to a gas:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p%20=%20-%20%5Cmathbf%7Bf%7D.%5Cnabla%5Cphi%20+%20%5Cmathbf%7BG%7D%5Cleft(%20%5Cnabla%5Cnabla%5Cphi%20%5Cright)%20-%20%5Cfrac%7B8%5Cgamma_%7B2%7D%5E%7B2%7D%5Ctext%7BKn%7Dg%7D%7B15%7D%5Cmu_%7B%5Cgamma_%7B2%7D%7D%20+%20%5Cmathbf%7BG%7D%5Cleft(%20%5Cnabla%5Cnabla%5Cmu_%7B%5Cgamma_%7B2%7D%7D%20%5Cright),"><br></p></center>
<p>where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbf%7BG%7D"> is some arbitrary trace-free symmetric 2-tensor, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cphi"> is the fundamental solution to the PDE known as Laplace’s equation (derived essentially by considering the symmetries which the solution must have), <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmu_%7B%5Cgamma_%7B2%7D%7D"> is the fundamental solution to the Helmholtz equation (similar to the Laplace equation) and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BKn%7D"> is a dimensionless parameter similar to the Reynolds number<a href="#fn3" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref3" role="doc-noteref"><sup>3</sup></a>. One notes the repeated appearance of the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cnabla"> operator, known as ‘nabla’ or ‘the gradient’. Using the definition of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cnabla"> as a differential operator, it is a trivial operation to rewrite the pressure term so that it no longer includes nabla:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p%20%3D%20%5Cmathbf%7Bf%7D.%5Cleft%28%20-%20%5Cfrac%7B%5Cmathbf%7Br%7D%7D%7B4%5Cpi%5Cleft%7C%20%5Cmathbf%7Br%7D%20%5Cright%7C%5E%7B3%7D%5C%20%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%2B%20G_%7B%5Ctext%7Bij%7D%7Dr_%7Bi%7Dr_%7Bj%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Cfrac%7B3%7D%7B4%5Cpi%5Cleft%7C%20%5Cmathbf%7Br%7D%20%5Cright%7C%5E%7B5%7D%5C%20%7D%20%5Cright%29%20-%20%5Cfrac%7B%5Cfrac%7B40%7D%7B6%5Ctext%7BKn%7D%7Dg%7D%7B15%7D%5Cfrac%7B1%7D%7B4%5Cpi%5Cleft%7C%20%5Cmathbf%7Br%7D%20%5Cright%7C%7De%5E%7B-%20%5Cgamma_%7B2%7Dr%7D%20%2B%20G_%7B%5Ctext%7Bij%7D%7Dr_%7Bi%7Dr_%7Bj%7D%5Cfrac%7Be%5E%7B-%20%5Cgamma_%7B2%7Dr%7D%5Cleft%28%205%5Cleft%7C%20%5Cmathbf%7Br%7D%20%5Cright%7C%20%2B%203%5Ctext%7BKn%7D%5Cleft%28%206Kn%20%2B%20%5Csqrt%7B30%7D%5Cleft%7C%20%5Cmathbf%7Br%7D%20%5Cright%7C%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%29%7D%7B24%5Ctext%7BKn%7D%5E%7B2%7D%5Cpi%5Cleft%7C%20%5Cmathbf%7Br%7D%20%5Cright%7C%5E%7B5%7D%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>This looks a bit messier, but the differential operator no longer appears and so it might be faster to process the solution with a computer. However, in this case, there is something more powerful going on, since the reformulation we propose allows us to keep a notion of a solution to the equation, even if it cannot be differentiated a sufficient number of times to be counted as a classical solution!</p>
<p>At the bottom of things, a variational technique is being employed to find existence of solutions. As the simplest possible example in what is called the ‘elliptic’ theory, one takes a standard second-order elliptic PDE such as the Poisson equation defined for a boundary value problem on a three-dimensional ball <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> and then rewrites it as:</p>

<p>where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cphi"> belongs to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D%5Cleft%28%20B%20%5Cright%29"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?u"> solves the original PDE. Similarly, in a different variational argument, one can find a function <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Coverline%7Bu%7D"> which minimises an energy functional similar to the Dirichlet energy over the space of admissible functions:</p>

<p>and then deduce from the following result (the weak form of Laplace’s equation)</p>

<p>and the fundamental lemma of calculus of variations that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Coverline%7Bu%7D"> satisfies the Laplace equation<a href="#fn4" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref4" role="doc-noteref"><sup>4</sup></a>.</p>
<p>The point of this reformulation is that it makes sense even if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?u"> is not in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5E%7B2%7D"> and cannot be differentiated twice, whereas the classical theory tells us that we must be able to differentiate the solution twice by definition. One then defines a linear functional <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L"> which maps <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C_%7B0%7D%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D%5Cleft%28%20B%20%5Cright%29"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D"> as follows:</p>

<p>One can see that this is a bounded linear functional using a standard inequality (the Friedrichs-Poincaré inequality). A linear operator <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"> between two normed spaces <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y"> is said to be bounded if the norm of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29"> is less than or equal to a non-negative constant multiplied by the norm of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x">.</p>

<p>In our weak reformulation, we have now changed the problem of solving the Poisson equation to the problem of solving</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5Cleft%28%20%5Cphi%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cleft%28%20u%2C%5Cphi%20%5Cright%29_%7BH_%7B0%7D%5E%7B1%7D%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>One would like to apply the Riesz representation theorem to our bounded linear functionals, but they are bounded operators on the space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20C_%7B0%7D%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D%5Cleft%28%20B%20%5Cright%29%2C%5C%20%5Cleft%28%20%5Cbullet%20%2C%20%5Cbullet%20%5Cright%29_%7BH_%7B0%7D%5E%7B1%7D%7D%20%5Cright%29">, which is not complete (ie. you can find a Cauchy sequence of functions in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C_%7B0%7D%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D%5Cleft%28%20B%20%5Cright%29"> which converges to a function which is not in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C_%7B0%7D%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D%5Cleft%28%20B%20%5Cright%29">).</p>
<p>The Riesz theorem for representation of functionals on Hilbert spaces states that every bounded linear functional <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> on a Hilbert space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H"> can be represented in a simple way via the inner product:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5Clangle%20%5Cleft.%20%5C%20x%2Cz%20%5Cright%5Crangle%20%5Cright.%5C%20%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?z"> depends on and is determined uniquely by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> and has the norm</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20f%20%5Cright%5C%7C%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20z%20%5Cright%5C%7C."><br></p></center>
<p>This is a fundamental theorem and more details can be found in any book or set of lecture notes on functional analysis<a href="#fn5" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref5" role="doc-noteref"><sup>5</sup></a>. A more useful way of putting it is that for a Hilbert space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H"> and a linear functional in the dual space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H%5E%7B%2A%7D">, there exists a unique element <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?u"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H"> such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5Cleft%28%20%5Cphi%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cleft%28%20u%2C%5Cphi%20%5Cright%29"><br></p></center>
<p>for any <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cphi"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H">. <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L"> is then a linear isometric isomorphism from the dual space to the original Hilbert space.</p>
<p>One example of an application of the theorem would be to gradient flows. Imagine that we start with a linear functional <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> which maps a space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D">. If we complete that space to a new space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cwidehat%7BX%7D"> by equipping it with an inner product and taking the closure, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> lives in the dual space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20%5Cwidehat%7BX%7D%20%5Cright%29%5E%7B%2A%7D">. Recall that Hilbert spaces are complete by definition. Riesz representation tells us that</p>

<p>for any <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cwidehat%7BX%7D"> and any <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cnabla%20f"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20%5Cwidehat%7BX%7D%20%5Cright%29%5E%7B%2A%7D">. Obviously, that inner product could be the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5E%7B2%7D"> inner product, depending on the Hilbert space. <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> can always be written as <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5Clangle%20%5Cnabla%20f%20%5Cright.%5C%20%2C%5Cleft.%20%5C%20x%20%5Cright%5Crangle"> and f having gradient just means that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cnabla%20f%20%5Cin%20X">. An <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5E%7B2%7D"> gradient flow is then a flow associated with the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5E%7B2%7D"> gradient vector field of a functional (it is important to be clear that the gradient flow depends on the functional). For an example from differential geometry, one can take a surjective continuous map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi"> from a topological space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> to a compact orientated manifold <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M">. In this case, the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5E%7B2%7D"> inner product has to take <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSym%7D%5E%7B2%7D%5Cleft%28%20T%5E%7B%2A%7DM%20%5Cright%29">, so it takes the form</p>

<p>As we hinted at above, one can always complete a space, so the way out is to take the completion of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20C_%7B0%7D%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D%5Cleft%28%20B%20%5Cright%29%2C%5C%20%5Cleft%28%20%5Cbullet%20%2C%20%5Cbullet%20%5Cright%29_%7BH_%7B0%7D%5E%7B1%7D%7D%20%5Cright%29"> and view it as a subspace of the well-understood space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5E%7B2%7D">. <p>where the inner product can be defined in terms of weak derivatives and assuming that we can prove that the weak solution <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?u"> is also smooth (which is possible).</p>
<p>Also note that the exact definition for a weak solution might vary depending on the type of equation and how one formulates the problem. For example, in rigorous fluid dynamics, a vector field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?v"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5E%7B2%7D%5Cleft%28%20T%5E%7B2%7D%20%5Cright%29"> is defined to be a weak solution of the incompressible Euler equations in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%5E%7B3%7D%20%5Ctimes%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%200%2CT%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"></p>

<p>if for any <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cphi"> and any <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpsi"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C_%7Bc%7D%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%200%2CT%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%20%5Ctimes%20T%5E%7B2%7D%20%5Cright%29"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7Bdiv%5C%20%7D%5Cpsi%20%3D%200">, we have</p>

<p>where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%5E%7B2%7D"> is the 2-torus and the subscript <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?c"> for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D"> refers to the fact that the functions have compact support<a href="#fn6" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref6" role="doc-noteref"><sup>6</sup></a>. <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C_%7Bc%7D%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D%5Cleft%28%20U%20%5Cright%29"> is a particularly nice function space to work with because all the derivatives of the functions living in this space have to vanish within a neighbourhood of the boundary of the set <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U"> and they must also vanish in some sense as one gets closer and closer to infinity. These weak solutions we have defined are quite different from the corresponding classical solutions which hold pointwise <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5E%7B1%7D%5Cleft%28%20T%5E%7B3%7D%20%5Ctimes%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%200%2CT%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%20%5Cright%29">.</p>
<p>Although not as famous as their cousins, the Navier-Stokes equations which govern viscous fluid flow, there are still many deep and interesting mathematical questions regarding the Euler equations. One of these (proved recently) is Onsager’s conjecture. This a technical conjecture which states that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?v"> is a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5E%7B%5Cbeta%7D"> weak solution of the Euler equations such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cbeta%20%5Cin%20%5Cleft%28%200%2C1%20%5Cright%29">, then when <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cbeta%20%3E%201%2F3"> we must have that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E%5Cleft%28%20t%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20c"> for any <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?t%20%5Cin%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%200%2CT%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">, where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> denotes the Euler equations and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?c"> is a constant. If, on the other hand, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cbeta%20%3C%201%2F3">, then there must exist <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?v"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> is non-increasing and not a constant. So, in some sense, the numerical value <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1%2F3"> in the exponent offers a kind of turning point. In more physical terms, Hölder continuous solutions to the incompressible Euler equations with exponent greater than <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1%2F3"> must conserve the total kinetic energy, whereas Hölder continuous solutions with exponent less than <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1%2F3"> must dissipate the total kinetic energy.</p>
<p>If you are not familiar, in analysis there are various notions of continuity, some of them stronger than others. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U"> is a subset of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cbeta%20%5Cin%20%5Cleft%28%200%2C1%20%5Cright%29">, then a function <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> mapping <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D"> is uniformly Hölder continuous with exponent <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cbeta"> if there exists a constant <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?c"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%7C%20f%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20-%20f%5Cleft%28%20y%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%7C%20%5Cleq%20%7Bc%5Cleft%7C%20x%20-%20y%20%5Cright%7C%5C%20%7D%5E%7B%5Cbeta%7D"> for any <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U">. One can also define a Hölder continuous function with exponent <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cbeta"> as a function which returns a finite value when we act on it with the semi-norm defined for the Hölder space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5E%7B0%2C%5Cbeta%7D">.</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?\left&space;\|&space;f&space;\right&space;\|_{C^{0,&space;\beta}(U)}:=&space;\left&space;\|&space;f&space;\right&space;\|_\infty&plus;sup\frac{|f(x)-f(y)|}{|x-y|^\beta&space;}"><br></p></center>
<p>In the special case where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cbeta%20%3D%201">, the function is Lipschitz continuous<a href="#fn7" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref7" role="doc-noteref"><sup>7</sup></a>. A function <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> is locally Hölder continuous if it is uniformly Hölder continuous on every compact subset of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U">.</p>
<p>The conjecture is based on Kolmogorov’s theory of turbulence, so a proof of the conjecture gives strong indirect evidence in favour of that theory. This is probably the most famous theory of isotropic turbulence and the details should be in a good book on turbulence. In words, a turbulent flow is formed of eddies: the energy flux cascades down from the larger eddies to the smaller ones, where it is dissipated by viscosity<a href="#fn8" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref8" role="doc-noteref"><sup>8</sup></a>. As with many difficult conjectures, the proof of the Onsager conjecture is spread over several decades and many papers and intermediate lemmas. Some progress was made using mollification and commutator estimates, and it was found that convex integration could be used to produce an infinite number of bounded weak dissipative solutions with non-constant energy. Convex integration was a technique originally used by John Nash for his famous embedding theorem, which states that every strictly short embedding of a Riemannian manifold into some Euclidean space can be uniformly approximated by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5E%7B1%7D"> isometric embeddings. An eventual proof was finalised recently using convex integration once more and building on the work of many other mathematicians who had contributed to the problem<a href="#fn9" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref9" role="doc-noteref"><sup>9</sup></a>. The overall strategy is to start with what is roughly speaking a ‘subsolution’ of the problem ie. a solution of a relaxed version of the problem which is nicer and easier to handle. One then iteratively adds perturbations to the subsolution to obtain at each step another subsolution which is closer to being an actual solution. Careful estimates are required which imply convergence to a solution in the required topology. The convex integration parts of the proof rely on ‘Mikado flows’ (so-called because the components of the flow look like straws and so resemble the Japanese ‘pocky’ biscuits which are sold under the name Mikado in Europe). The proof also uses a technique of ‘gluing approximation’ at certain points: for example, to approximate Reynolds stresses with disjoint time steps when mollifying with the integration kernel.</p>
<p>Within the area of PDE theory, the Cauchy-Kovalevskaya theorem relates to a particular type of problem called a Cauchy problem. The Cauchy problem starts with initial data along a smooth curve in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B2%7D"> and looks for a solution of a PDE which assumes the initial data we have specified on that curve. If the PDE is of order <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">, then the Cauchy data are the values which the dependent variable can take along with all of its possible PDEs up to and including <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n%20-%201"> on the curve. If the PDE, the smooth curve and the Cauchy data can be written in terms of functions which can all be represented locally in terms of convergent power series, then the Cauchy-Kovalevskaya theorem guarantees the existence of a unique solution to the Cauchy problem close to a point on the smooth curve, and that solution can also be represented locally in terms of convergent power series (ie. it is analytic). If you have studied complex analysis, you might recall that the word ‘analytic’ is generally replaced with holomorphic when working over the complex numbers. This theorem is a rigorous version of our intuitive feeling that there should be a unique solution to a PDE which achieves the values specified by the initial data along the smooth curve and it was proved in the general form by Kovalevskaya. If you not sure what to imagine by a convergent power series, you could think of a Taylor series, but generalise it and remember that a Taylor series need not converge. Although we have stated things for a curve, in the general setting for PDEs of more than 2 variables, this would be replaced with a hypersurface of the appropriate dimension<a href="#fn10" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref10" role="doc-noteref"><sup>10</sup></a>.</p>
<p>The method of proving the Cauchy-Kovalevskaya theorem demonstrates that Kovalevskaya’s work blends into analysis, since it relies on the method of convergent power series. If you have studied ODEs, you will be familiar with the idea of obtaining solutions to differential equations in terms of power series. In a similar way, one can find the functions involved in the general solution of a PDE by expanding the solution as a power series and making substitutions to find the coefficients which are involved. As mentioned before, this would obviously restrict us to analytic PDEs and analytic solutions which happen to have representations as convergent power series. The strategy which one takes for such an ‘analytic’ problem with analytic initial conditions should also be familiar if you have done some study of basic analysis. One calculates all the possible partial derivatives of the solution to the PDE at the origin and uses these to find the Taylor series expansion. Whether or not this series is a reasonable solution depends on whether or not it converges.</p>
<p>At this stage, it is useful to take the PDE (which might be second or higher order) and convert it to a canonical system of first order. I will skip the details here, but essentially one writes the PDE as a function of particular variables, and then views these variables in the argument as new unknown functions of two new independent variables. After manipulations with integrals and derivatives, one then ends up with a canonical system of first-order quasi-linear PDEs for new unknowns <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?u_%7Bk%7D">.</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cfrac%7B%5Cpartial%20u_%7Bj%7D%7D%7B%5Cpartial%5Cxi%7D%20%3D%20%5Csum_%7Bk%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7Bm%7D%7Ba_%7B%5Ctext%7Bjk%7D%7D%5Cleft%28%20u_%7B1%7D%2C%5Cldots%2Cu_%7Bm%7D%20%5Cright%29%5Cfrac%7B%5Cpartial%20u_%7Bk%7D%7D%7B%5Cpartial%5Ceta%7D%7D%2C%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20j%20%3D%201%2C%5Cldots%2Cm"><br></p></center>
<p>A linear differential equation is one which is linear (ie. of degree 1) in the dependent variable and its derivatives, whereas ‘quasi-linear’ merely means that the partial derivatives appear linearly. It is described as being ‘canonical’ because we have found <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpartial%20u_%7Bk%7D%2F%5Cpartial%5Cxi"> in terms of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?u_%7Bk%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpartial%20u_%7Bk%7D%2F%5Cpartial%5Ceta">. The coefficients in this system have convergent power series representations in a neighbourhood of the origin<a href="#fn11" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref11" role="doc-noteref"><sup>11</sup></a>.</p>
<p>In fact, one can prove the Cauchy-Kovalevskaya theorem without switching to a canonical system, but if we assume that the canonical system is sufficient for our purposes, we must now find Taylor series expansions about the origin for the unknown functions <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?u_%7Bk%7D">. The coefficients in such a series are found by mechanically computing the partial derivatives of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?u_%7Bk%7D"> at the origin. To do this, one has to find recursion formulae for partial derivatives with respect to the variable <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cxi"> (again, you might already be familiar with recursion formulae for finding coefficients in power series solutions to ODEs). The PDEs themselves are already recursion formulas of this type and one can find the derivatives of the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?u_%7Bk%7D"> with respect to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ceta"> by differentiating the initial conditions. After some more manipulations, we find all the partial derivatives of the functions <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?u_%7Bk%7D"> and realise that they are always positive. This eventually allows us to prove the convergence of the series expansion</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?u_k(\xi,\eta)=\sum^{\infty}_{\mu,v=0}\frac{1}{\mu!v!}\frac{\partial^{\mu&plus;v}u_k(0,0)}{\partial\xi^\mu\partial\eta^v}\xi^\mu\eta^v"><br></p></center>
<p>This proves that the canonical problem has a unique analytic solution within a neighbourhood of the origin and leads us to the Cauchy-Kovalevskaya theorem, which can be stated in the following way: in the neighbourhood of a point at which the coefficients <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?a_%7B%5Ctext%7Bjk%7D%7D"> and the functions <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?h_%7Bj%7D"> have power series representations, one can find a unique vector with components <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?u_%7Bk%7D"> which solves the following initial value problem<a href="#fn12" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref12" role="doc-noteref"><sup>12</sup></a>:</p>

<p>The functions <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?h_%7Bj%7D"> are analytic data defined as</p>

<p>where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> is the upper bound on the absolute value of each term in the Taylor series and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Crho"> is a sufficiently small positive number. This theorem is more limited than it appears (even if we assume that we are working with problems where all the functions are analytic), but it does show that if are willing to focus on analytic functions, the number of functions needed for a general solution of the PDE is always the same as the order of the PDE and that the PDE has one more variable in comparison to the number of variables in the arguments of the arbitrary functions.</p>
<p>The Cauchy problem for the gravitational field plays a key role in general relativity. One starts with a spacelike hypersurface <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M%5E%7B3%7D"> equipped with a metric <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g_%7B%5Ctext%7Bij%7D%7D"> and a symmetric 2-tensor <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k_%7B%5Ctext%7Bij%7D%7D"> known as the extrinsic curvature. The word ‘extrinsic’ refers to the fact that we are studying how an immersion of a space into a smaller space bends and distorts the larger space inside the smaller one. Intrinsic geometry studies operations on a manifold which do not depend on how we immerse it in another manifold<a href="#fn13" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref13" role="doc-noteref"><sup>13</sup></a>. As an example, Gauss’s <em>Theorema Egregium</em> states that the Gaussian curvature of a 2-dimensional submanifold <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20M%2Cg%20%5Cright%29"> of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B3%7D"> (where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> is the metric ‘induced’ or inherited from the ambient space into which the manifold is embedded) is a local isometry invariant of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20M%2Cg%20%5Cright%29."> In different words, a surface in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B3%7D"> has a type of curvature called Gaussian curvature which is defined by the fact that the surface is embedded into <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B3%7D">, but that definition coincides with the definition given by the Riemann tensor, which only depends on the metric with which the surface is equipped, and the metric does not care about the ambient space into which the surface is embedded and only measures intrinsic bending. Therefore, the Gaussian curvature does not actually depend on the embedding of the surface into <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B3%7D">, and is an intrinsic invariant. This is a rather beautiful and remarkable result of classical intrinsic geometry<a href="#fn14" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref14" role="doc-noteref"><sup>14</sup></a>.</p>
<p>In GR, the 3-manifold along with the metric and the 2-tensor is known as an ‘initial data’ for the Einstein equations (similar to the idea of initial data which you might already be familiar with from elementary PDE theory, where you would need to know the initial temperature distribution of the body before being able to deduce its final temperature from the heat equation). For the Cauchy problem, one starts with a 3-manifold <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?N"> with some more complicated initial data, and must then find a 4-manifold <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M">, plus an embedding <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> which maps <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?N"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> and a metric on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> which satisfies the Einstein equations. The metric must also agree with the initial values of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E%5Cleft%28%20N%20%5Cright%29"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E%5Cleft%28%20N%20%5Cright%29"> must be a Cauchy surface for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M">, where a Cauchy surface is a spacelike hypersurface such that every curve in the surface which is not spacelike intersects only once. The final manifold is said to be a development of the initial manifold with its initial data and there always exist developments of the initial 3-manifold as long as the initial data satisfy well-defined constraint equations (we will talk about this in more depth later when we discuss the work of Choquet-Bruhat)<a href="#fn15" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref15" role="doc-noteref"><sup>15</sup></a>.</p>
<p>Another area where Kovalevskaya made a key contribution is that of classical mechanics. I have discussed some of the basics of classical mechanics in another work, so I will not go over these again here<a href="#fn16" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref16" role="doc-noteref"><sup>16</sup></a>. In essence, Kovalevskaya described a case of a rigid spinning top, whose motion due to free precession under gravity is simple enough to be solvable analytically. This is not possible generally due to the complexity of precession. To get some insight about the Kovalevskaya top, it might be helpful to consider the case of a symmetric top with one point fixed in space. The top is then acted on by a uniform gravitational field ie. the centre of mass of the top is acted upon by a constant vertical force. The fixed point is a point on the symmetry axis of the object (recall that we are assuming the spinning top to be a symmetric rigid body). This axis can be chosen to be the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?z">-axis of the coordinate frame which is embedded in the body and which spins with it. As there is a base point which is fixed, we can specify the motion of the top with the Euler angles (again, I have discussed these elsewhere).</p>
<p>The rates of change of the Euler angles give us three motions which have nice physical interpretations. <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cdot%7B%5Cpsi%7D"> is the ordinary rotation of the top or gyroscope about the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?z">-axis inside the top, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cdot%7B%5Cphi%7D"> is the rotation of this axis about the ‘regular’ <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?z">-axis which is existing outside the top (known as ‘precession’) and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cdot%7B%5Ctheta%7D"> is the bobbing of the embedded axis relative to the regular vertical axis (think of a float bobbing up and down, but it is the axis itself which is doing the motion in this case). This bobbing motion is known as nutation. In many cases, the size of the rate of the change of the precession is much larger than the rate of change for the bobbing motion, and the rate of change for the ordinary rotation is much larger than either of them. In this case, and given that we are studying a symmetric top where the two of the moments of inertia are the same by definition, the Euler equations simplify to<a href="#fn17" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref17" role="doc-noteref"><sup>17</sup></a></p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I_%7B1%7D%7B%5Cdot%7B%5Comega%7D%7D_%7B1%7D%20%2B%20%5Comega_%7B2%7D%5Comega_%7B3%7D%5Cleft%28%20I_%7B3%7D%20-%20I_%7B2%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20N_%7B1%7D%2C"><br></p></center>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I_%7B2%7D%7B%5Cdot%7B%5Comega%7D%7D_%7B2%7D%20%2B%20%5Comega_%7B1%7D%5Comega_%7B3%7D%5Cleft%28%20I_%7B1%7D%20-%20I_%7B3%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20N_%7B2%7D%2C"><br></p></center>

The Euler equations look appealing, but they are not particularly useful to solve in this particular situation, so it is instead easier to use the Lagrangian formulation. You might see elsewhere the Hamiltonian formulation for tops. The kinetic energy of the top is</p>

<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%20%3D%20T%20-%20V%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> is the potential energy. In this case, we are working with a uniform, constant gravitational field acting on the centre of mass of the top, so the potential energy is fairly easy to write down in terms of the Euler angles. The kinetic energy can also be rewritten in terms of these angles, so we end up with</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%20%3D%20%5Cfrac%7BI_%7B1%7D%7D%7B2%7D%28%7B%5Cdot%7B%5Ctheta%7D%7D%5E%7B2%7D%20%2B%20%7B%5Cdot%7B%5Cphi%7D%7D%5E%7B2%7D%5Csin%5E%7B2%7D%7B%5Ctheta%29%20%2B%20%5Cfrac%7BI_%7B3%7D%7D%7B2%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Cdot%7B%5Cpsi%7D%20%2B%20%5Cdot%7B%5Cphi%7D%5Ccos%5Ctheta%20%5Cright%29%5E%7B2%7D%5C%20%7D%20-%20Mgl%5Ccos%5Ctheta."><br></p></center>
<p>For this system, the components of the angular momentum along the spatial vertical axis and along the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?z">- axis embedded in the top are constant, so we have two constants of the motion of the top.</p>

<p>The system does not lose energy over time, so the kinetic energy added to the potential energy must give us a third constant of the motion:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E%20%3D%20%5Cfrac%7BI_%7B1%7D%7D%7B2%7D%28%7B%5Cdot%7B%5Ctheta%7D%7D%5E%7B2%7D%20%2B%20%7B%5Cdot%7B%5Cphi%7D%7D%5E%7B2%7D%5Csin%5E%7B2%7D%7B%5Ctheta%29%20%2B%20%5Cfrac%7BI_%7B3%7D%7D%7B2%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Cdot%7B%5Cpsi%7D%20%2B%20%5Cdot%7B%5Cphi%7D%5Ccos%5Ctheta%20%5Cright%29%5E%7B2%7D%5C%20%7D%20%2B%20Mgl%5Ccos%5Ctheta."><br></p></center>
<p>Some manipulations with these first integrals of the motion give us differential equations for the rates of change of the three Euler angles, which effectively tells us everything we need to know about the motion after some more work. For example, we can obtain the frequency of the nutation and the angular velocity of the precession. The average precession frequency of the top is found to be</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Coverline%7B%5Cdot%7B%5Cphi%7D%7D%20%3D%20%5Cfrac%7B%5Ctext%7BMgl%7D%7D%7BI_%7B3%7D%7D%5Comega_%7B3%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>Hence the rate of precession decreases as one increase the initial rotational velocity of the top<a href="#fn18" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref18" role="doc-noteref"><sup>18</sup></a>.</p>
<p>Another related example is the Kovalevskaya top<a href="#fn19" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref19" role="doc-noteref"><sup>19</sup></a>. This top is also symmetric, but one now assumes that there is a ratio amongst the principal moments of inertia such that two of the moments are equal and double the size of the remaining moment.</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I_%7B1%7D%20%3D%20I_%7B2%7D%20%3D%202I_%7B3%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>In our previous example, the centre of gravity was located on the symmetry axis, but for the Kovalevskaya top it is located on a plane which is perpendicular to the symmetry axis. One can write down the Hamiltonian for this top and find that as before the first integrals of motion include the energy (energy will always be a constant of the motion as long as the system is conservative) and the component of the angular momentum in the direction of the embedded <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?z">-axis, but that there is also a new conserved quantity called the Kovalevskaya invariant. It is quite surprising that a mathematician of the nineteenth century was able to find another classical top whose description is sufficiently simple as to allow for an analytic solution for the motion of the precession. One might have thought naively that Euler and Lagrange had already dealt with all the cases which are simple enough that finding their precession is an integrable problem. In fact, another example of a solvable top was found in the twentieth century, but it is very difficult to integrate and there was a long period of time between its introduction and its eventual integration.</p>
