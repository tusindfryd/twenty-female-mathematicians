<p>Voisin has received many awards for her mathematical achievements and is known for her work in algebraic geometry, especially relating to Hodge theory. This body of theory has many applications. For example, given the moduli space of rank <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> stable holomorphic vector bundles over a complex Riemann surface, one knows via Hodge theory that the moduli space is equipped with a canonical Kähler metric. The Riemann-Roch theorem gives us a formula for the dimension of this moduli space, but as mentioned earlier, it might not be possible to actually use that formula in practice<a href="#fn225" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref225" role="doc-noteref"><sup>225</sup></a>. A Kähler metric is a Riemannian metric on a complex manifold ie. one has a Kähler manifold with a Hermitian metric</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?h%20%3D%20g%20-%20i%5Comega%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega"> is a Kähler form defined by</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g%5Cleft%28%20X%2CY%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Comega%5Cleft%28%20X%2CJY%20%5Cright%29."><br></p></center>
<p><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?J"> is an almost-complex structure on the manifold. A structure of this kind is a smooth choice of linear maps on each tangent space</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?J_%7BX%7D%3A%5C%20T_%7BX%7DM%20%5Crightarrow%20T_%7BX%7DM"><br></p></center>
<p>such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?J_%7BX%7D%5E%7B2%7D%20%3D%20%5Ctext%7BId.%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>In other words, the tangent spaces have the structure of complex vector spaces where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?J"> acts as multiplication by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?i">. It can be shown that if a manifold <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> has an almost-complex structure, then it must have a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSpin%7D%5E%7Bc%7D"> structure. Similar constructions are possible when one works with almost-quaternionic structures, where the tangent spaces have the structure of quaternionic vector spaces.</p>
<p>For <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?r%20%5Cgeq%202">, fix an orthonormal frame <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?e_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20e_%7Br%7D%20%5Cin%20%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Br%7D">. The Clifford algebra <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BCl%7D_%7Br%7D"> is the algebra generated by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?e_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20e_%7Br%7D"> subject to the following relations</p>

<p>The concept of an almost-complex structure can be generalised by saying that a rank <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?r"> almost-even Clifford structure on a manifold <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> is a smooth choice of algebra representations:</p>

<p>The endomorphisms in this case are skew symmetric. A natural question to ask is whether <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> having a rank <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?r"> almost-even Clifford structure implies that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> must have a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSpin%7D%5E%7Br%7D%5Cleft%28%20n%20%5Cright%29"> structure. It can be shown that a manifold having this type of structure plus a large automorphism group implies that the manifold is always a spin manifold when <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?r%20%3D%205%2C%5C%207"> and that around half of all manifolds are spin when <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?r%20%3D%203%2C%5C%204%2C%5C%206%2C%5C%208">, though the proof is technical<a href="#fn226" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref226" role="doc-noteref"><sup>226</sup></a>. A <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSpin%7D%5E%7Br%7D"> structure is just an appropriate generalisation of a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSpin%7D%5E%7Bc%7D"> structure, given that not every manifold will have a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSpin%7D%5E%7Bc%7D"> structure and so the group needs to be enlarged. This can be done by considering the twisted spin group:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSpin%7D%5E%7Br%7D%5Cleft%28%20n%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cfrac%7B%5Ctext%7BSpin%7D%5Cleft%28%20n%20%5Cright%29%20%5Ctimes%20%5Ctext%7BSpin%7D%5Cleft%28%20r%20%5Cright%29%7D%7B%5Cleft%5Clangle%20%5Cleft.%20%5C%20%5Cleft%28%20-%201%2C%20-%201%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5Crangle%20%5Cright.%5C%20%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>The corresponding theorems are analogous: just replace <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSO%7D%5Cleft%28%202%20%5Cright%29"> with <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSO%7D%5Cleft%28%20r%20%5Cright%29">, and so on. Every orientable Riemannian manifold has one of these structures.</p>
<p>Hodge theory is a way of studying cohomology groups on smooth manifolds. If one wishes to summarise it briefly, one can see it is a theory of harmonic forms. Like many things, the theory actually originated in mathematical physics. The de Rham theorem in smooth manifold theory states if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> is a smooth manifold and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p"> a non-negative integer, then the de Rham cohomology group is isomorphic to the corresponding singular cohomology group with coefficients in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D"> (see Bredon for a nice proof)<a href="#fn227" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref227" role="doc-noteref"><sup>227</sup></a>.</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H_%7B%5Ctext%7BdR%7D%7D%5E%7Bp%7D%20%5Ccong%20H%5E%7Bp%7D%5Cleft%28%20M%5Cmathbb%7B%2CR%7D%20%5Cright%29."><br></p></center>
<p>This map is given by</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega%20%5Clongmapsto%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20%5Csigma%20%5Clongmapsto%20%5Cint_%7B%5Csigma%7D%5E%7B%5C%20%7D%5Comega%20%5Cright%5Crbrack."><br></p></center>
<p>If you do not know what a de Rham cohomology group is, take a form <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cvarphi"> which vanishes when the exterior derivative is applied to it: such a form is said to be closed. Consider a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p">-form <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cvarphi"> which is the exterior derivative of a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20p%20-%201%20%5Cright%29">-form <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ceta"></p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cvarphi%20%3D%20d%5Ctext%7B%CE%B7.%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>It can be proved that the square of the exterior derivative is always zero. Obviously it is equivalent to show that the result is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0"> when acting on an arbitrary <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p">-form, so put everything in local coordinates.</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%7Bd%5Cvarphi%20%3D%20%5Cfrac%7B1%7D%7Bp%21%7D%5Cleft%28%20d%5Cvarphi_%7Bi_%7B1%7D%5Cldots%5C%20i_%7Bp%7D%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cland%20%7Bdx%7D%5E%7Bi_%7B1%7D%7D%20%5Cland%20%5Cldots%5C%20%20%5Cland%20%7Bdx%7D%5E%7Bi_%7Bp%7D%7D%2C%0A%7D%7B%3D%20%5Cfrac%7B1%7D%7Bp%21%7D%5Cfrac%7B%5Cpartial%5Cvarphi_%7Bi_%7B1%7D%5Cldots%5C%20i_%7Bp%7D%7D%7D%7B%5Cpartial%20x%5E%7Bj%7D%7Ddx%5E%7Bj%7D%20%5Cland%20%7Bdx%7D%5E%7Bi_%7B1%7D%7D%20%5Cland%20%5Cldots%5C%20%20%5Cland%20%7Bdx%7D%5E%7Bi_%7Bp%7D%7D.%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>Act on the resulting form with the exterior derivative operator:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7Bdd%7D%5Cvarphi%20%3D%20%5Cfrac%7B1%7D%7Bp%21%7D%5Cfrac%7B%5Cpartial%5E%7B2%7D%5Cvarphi_%7Bi_%7B1%7D%5Cldots%5C%20i_%7Bp%7D%7D%7D%7B%5Cpartial%20x%5E%7Bj%7D%5Cpartial%20x%5E%7Bk%7D%7Ddx%5E%7Bk%7D%20%5Cland%20dx%5E%7Bj%7D%20%5Cland%20%7Bdx%7D%5E%7Bi_%7B1%7D%7D%20%5Cland%20%5Cldots%5C%20%20%5Cland%20%7Bdx%7D%5E%7Bi_%7Bp%7D%7D."><br></p></center>

<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cfrac%7B%5Cpartial%5E%7B2%7D%5Cvarphi_%7Bi_%7B1%7D%5Cldots%5C%20i_%7Bp%7D%7D%7D%7B%5Cpartial%20x%5E%7Bj%7D%5Cpartial%20x%5E%7Bk%7D%7D%20%3D%20%5Cfrac%7B%5Cpartial%5E%7B2%7D%5Cvarphi_%7Bi_%7B1%7D%5Cldots%5C%20i_%7Bp%7D%7D%7D%7B%5Cpartial%20x%5E%7Bk%7D%5Cpartial%20x%5E%7Bj%7D%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>However, the exterior product anti-commutes:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?dx%5E%7Bk%7D%20%5Cland%20dx%5E%7Bj%7D%20%3D%20-%20dx%5E%7Bj%7D%20%5Cland%20dx%5E%7Bk%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>This means that the contraction over the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?j"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> indices will vanish, but that implies that the whole thing vanishes.</p>

<p>This in turn implies that every exact form is closed. Quotient the set of closed <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p">-forms on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> through by the set of exact <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p">-forms and you have the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p">th de Rham cohomology group. For a closed form <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega"> on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M">, the equivalence class of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega"> in this quotient space is called the cohomology class of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega">.</p>
<p>In words, every real cohomology class on a smooth manifold can be represented by a closed form on that manifold, and two closed forms represent the same cohomology class if they differ only by an exact form. This is still considered to be a remarkable result many years after it was proved. We mentioned earlier that the Maxwell equations can be written in terms of a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2">-form. In the homogeneous case, the equations can actually be written as</p>

<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?d%2AF%20%3D%200."><br></p></center>
<p>The relation <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F"> and its Hodge operator is invariant under the group of Lorentz transformations. It was by considering these equations that Hodge first defined the Hodge duality operator and came upon the Hodge theorem. As you probably know, an equivalence class can have many representatives, and there is a question as to whether there is some canonical choice for the form <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega"> which represents a cohomology class. We might imagine the set <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7B%20%5Comega%20%2B%20d%5Ctau%20%5Cright%5C%7D"> for some exact form <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?d%5Ctau"> and consider the ‘smallest’ member of this set as <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctau"> varies over all <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p">-forms. This set could be defined pointwise and integrated over the entire manifold<a href="#fn228" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref228" role="doc-noteref"><sup>228</sup></a>.</p>

<p>In essence, we are looking to show that a sequence of smooth forms in a de Rham equivalence class whose sizes converge to the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cinf"> of the sizes of all the forms in the class also converges to a smooth form. This can be done with some elliptic PDE theory, and one arrives at a central result known as the Hodge theorem. This states that for a compact Riemannian manifold <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> with orientation, the canonical map which sends a harmonic <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p">-form to the corresponding de Rham equivalence class is an isomorphism of vector spaces.</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BH%7D%5E%7Bp%7D%5Cleft%28%20M%20%5Cright%29%20%5Crightarrow%20H_%7B%5Ctext%7BdR%7D%7D%5E%7Bp%7D%5Cleft%28%20M%20%5Cright%29."><br></p></center>
<p>This implies that every de Rham cohomology class has a unique harmonic form as a representative. This theorem can lead to some powerful geometric results, especially for Kähler manifolds. A related corollary is the Hodge decomposition theorem that there exists an isomorphism as follows:</p>

<p>One of the Millennium Prize problems is a problem in Hodge theory, known as the Hodge conjecture. This conjecture states that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is a non-singular complex projective manifold (ie. a smooth projective algebraic varietiy over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D">), then every Hodge class (or Hodge cycle) is a linear combination of classes of algebraic cycles with coefficients in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D">. By a theorem of Chow, algebraic cycles on a complex projective variety are equivalent to closed analytic subspaces. There are also more generalized versions of the Hodge conjecture: for example, there is a version of the conjecture which takes <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> to be a complex Kähler variety and states that every Hodge cycle on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> should be a rational linear combination of Chern classes of vector bundles on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">. A different version is exactly the same, but with vector bundles replaced by coherent sheaves. Voisin proved this to be false by constructing a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?4">-dimensional complex torus with a non-trivial Hodge cycle of degree <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?4"> such that any analytic coherent sheaf <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BF%7D"> on the variety has a trivial second Chern class. In the course of finding this counterexample, Voisin found a non-trivial criterion for the vanishing of the second Chern class of an analytic coherent sheaf on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is a compact Kähler variety of dimension <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n%20%5Cgeq%203"> such that the Néron-Severi group generated by the first Chern classes of holomorphic line bundles on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is the zero group, if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> does not contain any proper closed analytic subset of positive dimension, and if for Kähler equivalence class <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20%5Comega%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%20%5Cin%20H%5E%7B2%7D%5Cleft%28%20X%5Cmathbb%7B%2CR%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%5Ccap%20H%5E%7B1%2C1%7D%5Cleft%28%20X%20%5Cright%29">, the set of Hodge cycles <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BH%5C%20d%7Dg%5E%7B4%7D"> is perpendicular to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20%5Comega%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%5E%7Bn%20-%202%7D"> for the intersection pairing</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H%5E%7B4%7D%5Cleft%28%20X%5Cmathbb%7B%2CR%7D%20%5Cright%29%5C%20%5Cbigotimes%5Ctext%7B%5C%20H%7D%5E%7B2n%20-%204%7D%5Cleft%28%20X%5Cmathbb%7B%2CR%7D%20%5Cright%29%5Cmathbb%7B%5Crightarrow%20R%2C%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>then any analytic coherent sheaf <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BF%7D"> on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> has a trivial second Chern class<a href="#fn231" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref231" role="doc-noteref"><sup>231</sup></a>. Hodge originally proposed a stronger version of the conjecture, but Grothendieck realised that this version was automatically false for trivial reasons<a href="#fn232" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref232" role="doc-noteref"><sup>232</sup></a>.</p>

<p>Most of these isolated results (and many others) can be tied up to prove that the rational cohomology of a compact polarised complex manifold can be decomposed into a direct sum of polarised Hodge structures. We can also define integral and rational Hodge structures via the Hodge decomposition of the cohomology. Lefschetz decomposition and the Hodge index theorem allow for a direct sum expression of the cohomology of a compact Kähler manifold, where the elements of the sum are primitive components which are compatible with the Hodge decomposition. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Calpha"> is a real closed form of type <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%201%2C1%20%5Cright%29"> on a compact Kähler manifold with integral cohomology class, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Calpha"> is the Chern form for a holomorphic line bundle endowed with a Hermitian metric. <p>This talk of categories may seem strange if you are unaccustomed to category theory, but it is by this point embedded in the mathematical landscape. Homology, for example, is a functor from the category of topological spaces to the category of sequences of abelian groups, and does not necessarily make much sense if you try to view it any other way. Start for a class of objects and for every ordered pair of objects in the class, specify a set of morphisms which take object <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> to object <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> (denoted by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20A%2CB%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">). For every triple of objects <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A%2C%5C%20B%2C%5C%20C">, specify a rule which assigns to every pair of morphisms another morphism which belongs to set of morphisms from object <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> to object <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C">, called a composition of morphisms. The class of objects along with the morphisms and the composition maps forms a category if composition of morphisms is associative (if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20%5Ctext%7Bhg%7D%20%5Cright%29f%20%3D%20h%5Cleft%28%20%5Ctext%7Bgf%7D%20%5Cright%29"> for any triple of morphisms, where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%20%5Cin%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20A%2CB%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g%20%5Cin%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20B%2CC%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?h%20%5Cin%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20C%2CD%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">) and if for any object <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> there is an identity morphism taking <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> such that for any two morphisms <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%20%5Cin%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20A%2CB%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> and , <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g%20%5Cin%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20B%2CC%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">, one has the following equalities:</p>

<p>There are many examples of categories. Take the category of all sets and their maps, for example. Observe that we never talk about taking the set of all sets, or we get involved in Russell’s paradox. The most common layperson’s statement of this paradox asks that one imagine a barber in a village who shaves everyone that does not shave themselves and no-one else. The question is whether the barber will have to shave himself, or not: this is the type of paradox which is being described, since you are ending up with something which is and is not a member of itself. In formal terms, the paradox is</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BIf%5C%20%7DR%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5C%7B%20%5Ctext%7Bx%5C%20%7D%5Ctext%7Bsuch%5C%20that%5C%20%7Dx%20%5Cnotin%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7D%2C%5C%20then%5C%20R%20%5Cin%20R%5C%20%20%5CLongleftrightarrow%20R%20%5Cnotin%20R."><br></p></center>
<p>Obviously this is complete nonsense.</p>
<p>The objects of the category are all sets and the set of morphisms is composed of all the possible maps from one set into another. The next most obvious example is the category of groups and group homomorphisms, where the objects are the groups and the morphism are the homomorphisms. The category of topological spaces along with the set of continuous maps is another example. One can also take the category formed from all topological spaces and all classes of homotopic maps: in this case, the morphisms are classes of a particular type of map, and not maps themselves. In general, morphisms do not need to be maps, although we may denote them as if they were. These definitions also absorb the notion of an isomorphism in a more general way. Two objects in a category are isomorphic if there exist two morphisms <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%20%5Cin%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20A%2CB%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%20%5Cin%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20B%2CA%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?fg%20%3D%20%5Ctext%7BId%7D_%7BB%7D%2C%5C%20%5C%20gf%20%3D%20%5Ctext%7BId%7D_%7BA%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>An isomorphism in the category of sets is a bijection, an isomorphism in the category of topological spaces is a homeomorphism, and so on.</p>

Also, for a morphism <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%20%5Cin%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20A%2CB%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> in the set of morphisms of the first category, assign a morphism <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f_%7B%2A%7D%20%5Cin%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20F%5Cleft%28%20A%20%5Cright%29%2CF%5Cleft%28%20B%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> in the set of morphisms of the second category. For an example of a functor, take the category of finite-dimensional vector spaces <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> with linear isomorphisms as morphisms. One can define a functor <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BF%3A%5C%20%7D%5Ctext%7B%5C%20V%7D%5C%20%20%5Crightarrow%20%5Ctext%7B%5C%20V%7D"> by requiring that</p>

<p>It can be shown that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BF%7D"> is a smooth covariant functor (ie. it is smooth for every <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V">) and that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BF%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Ctext%7BTM%7D%20%5Cright%29"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%5E%7B%2A%7DM"> are smoothly isomorphic vector bundles via a canonical choice of bundle isomorphism<a href="#fn236" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref236" role="doc-noteref"><sup>236</sup></a>. Also, the image of the Hodge structure on the cohomology of Kähler manifolds relative to the holomorphic maps between any two Kähler manifolds is functorial. It should be clear to you that this kind of theory is very useful for proving things, and that it can be extended to multiple settings in a flexible way. However, as always you have to think about what you are working with can be computed, and then make restrictions if it cannot. For example, a functor for a homology theory will take the category of all topological spaces to the category of all <em>sequences</em> of abelian groups, which is going to make practical computation of homology groups extremely difficult unless restrictions are made to the type of space we are studying. In fact, it is rare that you can consider what you might call the completely ‘arbitrary’ case in pure mathematics, as there is too much going on: one usually has to make a restriction or assumption of some kind before any progress can be made.</p>

<p>Another important result was the proof that in dimension <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cgeq%204"> there exist compact Kähler manifolds which are not homotopic to projective complex manifolds. This is interesting, because it was already known that a compact Kähler surface is deformation equivalent to a complex projective surface, but this result says that the same is not true in higher dimensions. Two deformation equivalent complex manifolds are homeomorphic and homotopic. The problem of whether the result for a surface can be extended to higher dimension is known as the Kodaira problem. There is also a more general Kodaira theorem which states that a compact complex manifold is projective if and only if it admits a Kähler form whose cohomology class is integral<a href="#fn238" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref238" role="doc-noteref"><sup>238</sup></a>.</p>
