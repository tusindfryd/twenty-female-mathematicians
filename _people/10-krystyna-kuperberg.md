<p>Kuperberg has researched topology and dynamical systems, including work related to the Seifert conjecture. The Seifert conjecture is a statement regarding the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3">-sphere. The <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-sphere is the most intensively studied of all manifolds, from the basic level to the advanced. A standard exercise with the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-sphere is to compute the transition map given by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Calpha_%7B1%7D%20%5Ccirc%20%5Calpha_%7B2%7D%5E%7B-%201%7D"> where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Calpha_%7B1%7D"> is stereographic projection from the south pole of the sphere and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Calpha_%7B2%7D"> is stereographic projection from the north pole. As I say, this is a problem which you will likely see in a homework sheet or an exam if you take a course on smooth manifolds. I will outline some of the solution if it is helpful, but try it yourself and discuss it with someone before looking up the answer. I will study the case of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B2%7D">, but all the arguments can be generalised.</p>

<p>When seeking to find relationships between curvature and topology, one usually focusses on the effect of positive or negative Ricci curvature, sectional curvature, or scalar curvature. Other curvature assumptions are possible, however. One can assume positive curvature on totally isotropic two-planes. Under this assumption, and using the Sacks-Uhlenbeck theory of minimal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2">-spheres, Micallef and Moore showed that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> is a compact simply connected Riemannian <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-manifold with <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n%20%5Cgeq%204"> and positive curvature on totally isotropic two-planes, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> is homeomorphic to a sphere. The curvature of a Riemannian manifold on totally isotropic two-planes is said to be positive if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K%5Cleft%28%20%5Csigma%20%5Cright%29%20%3E%200"> whenever <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma%20%5Csubseteq%20T_%7Bp%7DM%5Cbigotimes_%7B%7D%5E%7B%7D%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D"> is a totally isotropic two-plane situated at any point in the manifold<a href="#fn129" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref129" role="doc-noteref"><sup>129</sup></a>. Homeomorphisms to a sphere also show up in one of the most famous conjectures in mathematics, the Poincaré conjecture. This conjecture states that every closed, smooth, simply connected <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3">-manifold is diffeomorphic to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B3%7D">. There is no need to specify that the manifold is homeomorphic to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B3%7D">, as classifications of topological <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3">-manifolds up to diffeomorphism or up to homeomorphism are equivalent. The conjecture can be written very simply in equation form:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi_%7B1%7D%5Cleft%28%20M%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%200%20%5CLongrightarrow%20M%20%5Ccong%20S%5E%7B3%7D."><br></p></center>

<p>Interestingly, what is called an outermost minimal surface is always a sphere, so you may actually seem them referred to as outermost minimal spheres. (It could be a sphere or a torus technically, but a stability argument removes the latter possibility). Outermost minimal just means a minimal surface which is not contained completely inside another minimal surface. Define <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BS%7D"> to be the collection of surfaces which are smooth compact boundaries of open sets <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M%5E%7B3%7D">, where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U"> contains all the points at infinity <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7B%20%5Cinfty_%7Bk%7D%20%5Cright%5C%7D"> which are added to compactify each of the ends <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M_%7Bk%7D">. Given a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CSigma%5Cmathcal%7B%5Cin%20S%7D">, define <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cwidetilde%7B%5CSigma%7D%5Cmathcal%7B%5Cin%20S%7D"> to be the outermost minimal area enclosure of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CSigma">. <p>The Seifert conjecture (now known to be false) states that every nonsingular continuous vector field on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B3%7D"> has a fixed point or a closed orbit. A counterexample was found to this conjecture fairly early on, but that was not the end of the story, as one would like to improve the differentiability (smoothness) of these examples. Seifert was led to this conjecture when he showed that a flow-generating, nonsingular, continuous vector field on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B3%7D"> whose vectors are close to vectors which are tangent to the fibres of the Hopf fibration has a closed integral curve. Schweitzer gave a counterexample in dimension <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3"> by exhibiting an example of an aperiodic <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5E%7B1%7D"> vector field on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B3%7D">. Kuperberg significant improved the differentiability of this example by finding a nonsingular <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D"> vector field with no closed circular orbits on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B3%7D">: this was done independently of Schweitzer’s example. The starting point of Kuperberg’s construction is a mirror-image, Wilson-type plug which is modified by an operation called self-insertion<a href="#fn132" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref132" role="doc-noteref"><sup>132</sup></a>.</p>
<p>Inspired by this real analytic counterexample, Greg Kuperberg modified the Schweitzer counterexample and proved that every <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3">-manifold admits a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5E%7B1%7D"> volume-preserving flow without fixed points or closed orbits. The main construction is a volume-preserving version of Schweitzer’s plug (specifically, a Dehn-twisted Wilson-type plug). He also proved that every <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3">-manifold admits a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D"> volume-preserving flow with no fixed points or closed orbits, along with a transversely measured <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BPL%7D"> foliation with discrete closed leaves. The proof of the theorem constructs a plug and inserts it into the flow, but there are technical obstructions due to the fact that a volume-preserving plug cannot plug up an open set. These are overcome by using Dehn-twisted plugs: these plugs can change the topology of a manifold during insertion. One important aspect of a volume-preserving flow in dimension <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3"> with no fixed points is that the parallel <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">-dimensional foliation is transversely symplectic. Such a volume-preserving flow on a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3">-manifold can be understood as a Hamiltonian flow coming from a symplectic structure on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M%5Cmathbb%7B%5Ctimes%20R%7D">. This links back to the Weinstein conjecture which we mentioned in the context of symplectic topology. In that case, the result is that the flow on a closed <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%202n%20%2B%201%20%5Cright%29">-dimensional contact manifold which is transversely symplectic has a closed orbit if</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H%5E%7B1%7D%5Cleft%28%20M%5Cmathbb%7B%2CZ%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%200."><br></p></center>
<p>The Weinstein conjecture has been shown to be true for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B3%7D">, so the volume-preserving flow we are discussing here cannot be contact<a href="#fn133" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref133" role="doc-noteref"><sup>133</sup></a>. Recall that in slightly more old-fashioned terminology, to say that a manifold is closed implies that it is compact and that it has no boundary ie. the boundary is the empty set.</p>
<p>The Kuperbergs collaborated to construct generalized counterexamples to the conjecture. Using the theory of plugs and the self-insertion construction due to Krystyna, they proved that a foliation of any codimension of any manifold can always be modified in a real analytic or piecewise-linear way so that all minimal sets have codimension <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">. A corollary of this theorem is that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B3%7D"> has an analytic dynamical system such that all limit sets are <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2">-dimensional. In particular, it has no circular trajectories. The generalized counterexamples are based on construction of aperiodic plugs. An insertible, untwisted, attachable plug is an orientated, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">-dimensional foliation <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BF%7D"> of a manifold with boundary given by the Cartesian product <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F%20%5Ctimes%20I"> of an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20n%20-%201%20%5Cright%29">-dimensional manifold and the interval <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I">. The foliation agrees with the trivial foliation in the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I"> direction on a neighbourhood of the boundary of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F%20%5Ctimes%20I">, if a leaf of the foliation connecting <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20p%2C0%20%5Cright%29"> with <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20q%2C1%20%5Cright%29"> implies that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p%20%3D%20q"> and if there is a non-compact leaf containing <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20p%2C0%20%5Cright%29">. An important result in foliation theory is the global Frobenius theorem, what states that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D"> is an involutive distribution on a smooth manifold <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M">, then the collection of all maximal connected integral manifolds of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D"> forms a foliation of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M">.</p>
<p>A distribution on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> is a choice of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">-dimensional linear subspace of the tangent space at every point <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p%20%5Cin%20M">. A distribution is said to be smooth if the union of all of these linear subspaces forms a smooth subbundle</p>

<p>The local frame criterion for subbundles immediately provides with a criterion for smoothness of a distribution. Essentially, every point of a manifold has a neighbourhood on which there are vector fields such that one can form a basis for the corresponding <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">-dimensional subspace in the distribution. In this case, one can say that the distribution is spanned locally by those vector fields. Now if we start with a distribution, an immersed submanifold <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?N"> contained in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> is called an integral manifold if we have the following</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T_%7Bp%7DN%20%3D%20D_%7Bp%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>at every point <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?N">. As an example, if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> is a smooth nowhere-vanishing vector field on a manifold, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> trivially spans a smooth <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">-dimensional distribution on that manifold. The image of any integral curve of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> is an integral manifold of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D">.</p>
<p>Integral manifolds might fail to exist, however. Take <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D"> a smooth tangent distribution on a manifold. <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D"> is said to be an involutive distribution if for any pair of smooth local sections of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D"> (essentially smooth vector fields defined on the appropriate open subset), the Lie bracket is also a local section of the distribution. <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D"> is said to be integrable if each point of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> is contained in an integral manifold of the distribution. It can be proved that every integrable distribution is involutive. Involutivity can also be described in terms of differential forms: in general, tangent distributions can be described locally by smooth <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">-forms, as well as by vector fields. <p>Involutivity can be expressed by saying that a smooth distribution is involutive if and only if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BI%7D%5Cleft%28%20D%20%5Cright%29"> is a differential ideal. <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BI%7D%5Cleft%28%20D%20%5Cright%29"> is defined as</p>

A central result of smooth manifold theory which comes from all this is that every involutive distribution is completely integrable, where a distribution is completely integrable if there always exists a flat chart for the distribution in a neighbourhood of every point in the manifold. This is known as the Frobenius theorem. The global Frobenius theorem follows once we prove that for an involutive distribution <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7B%20N_%7B%5Calpha%7D%20%5Cright%5C%7D_%7B%5Calpha%20%5Cin%20A%7D"> a collection of connected integral manifolds of the distribution with a point in common, the following union</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?N%20%3D%20%5Cbigcup_%7B%5Calpha%7D%5E%7B%5C%20%7DN_%7B%5Calpha%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>has a unique smooth structure as a smooth manifold which makes it into a connected integral manifold of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D"> in which each of the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?N_%7B%5Calpha%7D"> is an open submanifold<a href="#fn134" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref134" role="doc-noteref"><sup>134</sup></a>.</p>
<p>To return to our original foliation, the base <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F"> is an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20n%20-%201%20%5Cright%29">-manifold which admits a bridge immersion in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%20-%201%7D">, an immersion which lifts to an embedding <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%7D">. A plug is said to be aperiodic if it has no closed leaves. The idea which Schweitzer proposed is to use plugs to modify the foliation and break circular leaves, and he observed that the base of a plug only needs to admit a bridge immersion, and not an embedding. The theorem which we stated on the foliation of any codimension directly implies that the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3">-sphere admits an analytic dynamic system such that all limit sets are <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2">-dimesional. The Schweitzer-Harrison plug <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5E%7B2%7D"> aperiodic plug has two <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">-dimesional minimal sets. In particular, it can be proved that a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">-foliation of a manifold of dimension at least <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3"> can be modified in the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BPL%7D"> category so that are no closed leaves but all minimal sets are <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">-dimensional. If the manifold is closed, then there is an aperiodic <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BPL%7D"> modification with only one minimal set, and that minimal set is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">-dimensional.</p>
<p>As we have said, the main purpose of a plug is to be able to carry out a geometric operation called insertion. The idea is to start with a foliation <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cchi"> of an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-manifold <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> and an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-dimensional plug <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?P"> with a base <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F">. We then look for a leaf-preserving embedding of the trivially foliated <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F%20%5Ctimes%20I"> in the manifold, and see if it can be replaced with the plug. The technical obstructions involved when one tries to do this are insertibility, attachability and twistedness. An insertion map for a plug into a foliation is defined as an embedding</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma%3AF%20%5Clongrightarrow%20X"><br></p></center>
<p>of the base of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?P"> which is transverse to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cchi">. This insertion map can be extended to the product to get an embedding</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma%3AF%20%5Ctimes%20I%20%5Clongrightarrow%20X"><br></p></center>
<p>which takes the fibre foliation of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F%20%5Ctimes%20I"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cchi">. An <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-dimensional plug <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?P"> is said to be insertible if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F"> admits an embedding in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%7D"> which is transverse to vertical lines. An embedding of this kind is equivalent to a bridge immersion of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%20-%201%7D">. To be clear, it is equivalent to an immersion which lifts to an embedding of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F%20%5Ctimes%20I"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%7D">. As an example, if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F"> is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2">-dimensional, orientable, and with a boundary which is not equal to the empty set, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F"> must have a bridge immersion.</p>

If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> is a manifold with boundary, take <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?N_%7BM%7D"> to be an open neighbourhood of the boundary of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M">. <p>All of the leaves in this parallel boundary have two endpoints: a lemma then shows that there is a leaf-preserving homeomorphism <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?N_%7BG%7D%20%5Clongrightarrow%20N_%7BF%7D%20%5Ctimes%20I">. By this point, you are starting to see how sophisticated the geometry is which is going into this: in this case, just the fact that someone found one particular counterexample to the conjecture was not the end of the story at all.</p>
<p>Aside from work on the Seifert conjecture, Kuperberg has also done other work on the application of topology to dynamical systems theory. In particular, with Reed, she studied trajectories of dynamical systems on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B3%7D">. She constructed an example of a rest point free dynamical system on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B3%7D"> with uniformly bounded trajectories and no circular trajectories. The construction was based on an example of Schweitzer’s<img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?">. She also solved a problem related to the question of whether or not a homogeneous space has to be bihomogeneous. A counterexample to this conjecture was found many years ago, but whether or not it was true for continua remained an open problem for some time, until Kuperberg found a counterexample in the continuum case. A continuum is a topological space, often taken to be a nonempty compact connected metric space<a href="#fn136" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref136" role="doc-noteref"><sup>136</sup></a>. Textbook examples of continua which you might already have come across in topology are the Warsaw circle and the topologist’s sine curve.</p>
