<p>Uhlenbeck is known for her fundamental work in differential geometry, gauge theory and theory of PDEs. In particular, she contributed to the founding of modern geometric analysis (a field where one studies problems or properties of objects in differential geometry using tools and ideas from the theory of differential equations). Uhlenbeck is one of the most celebrated living female mathematicians and recently became the first woman to win the prestigious Abel Prize. Besides her academic achievements, she has also done work to widen minority and female participation in mathematics and co-founded the Women and Mathematics Program at the Institute for Advanced Study.</p>
<p>The physical origin of a gauge theory is that it is a theory whose Langrangian is invariant under a particular Lie group of local gauge transformations known as the gauge group. (You should think of the word ‘gauge’ as implying change of scale or ‘locality’). The simplest possible example of a gauge theory is classical electrodynamics, where the Lagrangian is invariant under local <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U%5Cleft%28%201%20%5Cright%29">-transformations. <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U%5Cleft%28%201%20%5Cright%29"> is a Lie group known as the circle group, the most basic example of a unitary group of degree <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">. A gauge theory requires a gauge field, which is the electromagnetic four-potential for the case of electromagnetism. Things become inherently geometric at this point, since the gauge field is a connection. One also has a definition for curvature which is constructed from the connection. In order to be able to discuss electrodynamics as a gauge theory, we must express the usual objects of electromagnetism as differential forms on a spacetime (Minkowski spacetime in our case). General electromagnetic fields can be combined into two <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2">-forms <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">.</p>

<p>Given that electromagnetism is described successfully by an abelian gauge group, the question arises as to whether it might be possible to describe other interactions in Nature using non-abelian gauge theories. This turns out to be the case, and such a theory is known as a Yang-Mills theory. In the Yang-Mills case, the gauge group must be compact and the definition of curvature is modified by adding an extra term:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F%20%3D%20dA%20%2B%20A%20%5Cland%20A."><br></p></center>

<p>Gauge theories are the most important examples of quantum field theories. The Standard Model is a gauge theory, for example, but the Lie group in this case is a direct product of several other Lie groups due to the complexity of the theory and the number of gauge bosons which must be accounted for. However, it is fairly common in mathematics for an area to be initially inspired or created from discoveries in theoretical physics which then develops into its own branch to the point where the physical origin becomes quite distant. This is the case for gauge theory in mathematics, which is a branch of geometry, although physical references might still appear every now and then. Essentially, one is usually studying the theory of connections when one studies gauge theory from the mathematical point of view.</p>
<p>Start with a smooth principal bundle <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi%3AP%20%5Crightarrow%20X"> with a Lie group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">. A principal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">-bundle over a smooth manifold <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is a manifold with a smooth right <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">-action and an orbit space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?P%2FG%20%3D%20X">. In general, if a Lie group acts on a manifold, one can define an equivalence relation <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p%5C%20%5Csim%5C%20q"> if there exists a group element <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g%20%5Cbullet%20p%20%3D%20q">. The equivalence classes for this relation are the orbits of the Lie group in the manifold, where the set of orbits is denoted by the quotient <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M%2FG">: if we equip the resulting space with the quotient toplogy, it is known as the orbit space of the action. It can be proved that if the Lie group acts smoothly, freely and properly, the orbit space is a topological manifold of dimension <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cdim%20M%20-%20%5Cdim%20G"> equipped with a unique smooth structure such that the quotient map from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M%2FG"> is a smooth submersion. If the action is locally equivalent to the trivial action on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U%20%5Ctimes%20G">, then we say that the bundle <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?P"> has a structure group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">. There are several ways of defining a connection on this type of bundle: for example, as a Lie algebra-valued <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">-form called the connection <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">-form (ie. as a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">-form on the bundle which takes its values in the Lie algebra <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bg%7D"> of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">). As hinted at above, the curvature of the connection is a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bg%7D">-valued <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2">-form <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5COmega"> given by the equation</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5COmega%20%3D%20d%5Comega%20%2B%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20%5Comega%2C%5Comega%20%5Cright%5Crbrack."><br></p></center>
<p>Another possibility is to define it as a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">-invariant field of tangent <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-planes <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctau"> on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?P"> such that the linear map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi_%7B%2A%7D%3A%5C%20%5Ctau_%7Bp%7D%20%5Crightarrow%20T_%7B%5Ctext%7B%CF%80p%7D%7D%5Cleft%28%20X%20%5Cright%29"> is an isomorphism for all <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p%20%5Cin%20P">. Probably the most common way of defining connections is to work with a vector bundle <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> and make it into a principal bundle. You might notice the proliferation of linear maps in differential geometry, hence why many people say the subject is just advanced linear algebra. Bundle automorphisms are gauge transformations whose local representations are matrix-valued functions. You will recognise the idea of a connection if you have studied differential geometry. In that setting, one studies connections on the tangent bundle of a manifold (usually the Levi-Civita connection). The novelty in general connection theory (known as Yang-Mills theory) is that one studies connections on auxiliary bundles. This is slightly different to what a physicist means when they are talking about Yang-Mills theory, but they <em>are</em> linked. Historically, it was realised that the correct mathematical formulation for electromagnetism came by considering a connection on a principal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U%5Cleft%28%201%20%5Cright%29">-bundle, and this gave the impetus for physical Yang-Mills theories, which then provided fertile ground for new geometric ideas<a href="#fn118" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref118" role="doc-noteref"><sup>118</sup></a>.</p>

The continuity idea comes into play here, as one takes the set of times in the unit interval for which such a gauge transformation exists and shows that the set is both open and closed, and that it contains the points <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?t%20%3D%200"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?t%20%3D%201">, which proves the proposition<a href="#fn119" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref119" role="doc-noteref"><sup>119</sup></a>.</p>
<p>To explain the terminology of a classical Coulomb gauge, one needs to explain the idea of ‘fixing the gauge’. <p>A self-dual or ASD connection can also be called an instanton or an anti-instanton. Generally speaking, you could say that an instanton is a special type of solution to the field equations for gauge theories. More specifically, an instanton is a classical solution in a classical Euclidean field theory with finite non-zero action. The name is due to the fact that they happen for an 'instant' (a point) of Euclidean time and so they are important as critical points in the path-integral formulation of a theory which uses Euclidean signature. The instanton solution of the Euclidean Yang-Mills equation leads to an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSU%7D%5Cleft%28%202%20%5Cright%29"> bundle over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B4%7D">, but it can also be proved that any finite action solution of the Euclidean Yang-Mills equations leads to a fibre bundle over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B4%7D"> (another theorem of Uhlenbeck)<a href="#fn124" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref124" role="doc-noteref"><sup>124</sup></a>.</p>
<p>If we consider the best-known example, we take a pure Yang-Mills theory with symmetry group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSU%7D%5Cleft%28%202%20%5Cright%29"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B4%7D"> with Euclidean signature. The equations of motion (ie. the Yang-Mills equations) are <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D%2AF%20%3D%200"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?DF%20%3D%200">. When we introduce the ASD condition, these equations reduce to ODEs for the gauge potential <em>A</em>. If we make an ansatz for the solution to the ASD equation which only differs from a pure gauge by a function of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?r"> at infinite radius, we guarantee that our solutions have finite, non-zero action. Our ansatz for the gauge field is such that it becomes a pure gauge as <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?r"> tends to infinity and the associated field strength disappears, meaning that the action is finite. If we choose the appropriate gauge transformation and use this to evaluate the field strength, we then obtain an equation whose full solution is the 'instanton potential' which is regular on all of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B4%7D">. The action is equal to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?-%208%5Cpi%5E%7B2%7D%2Fg%5E%7B2%7D">, so it is obviously finite. One can also consider related bundle structures such as the Dirac monopole: the bundle structure of the Dirac monopole is extremely similar to that of the Yang-Mills instanton bundle. Following this logic we can might attempt to construct a 'gravitational instanton': we look for a metric with Euclidean signature described locally by an orthonormal frame and solve the Einstein equations without matter. We end up with a solution such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> tend to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1"> as <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?r"> tends to infinity. This is similar to the Yang-Mills case, where the Yang-Mills instanton potential becomes a pure gauge as <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?r"> tends to infinity. Gravity ends up being 'analogous' to other gauge theories, rather than directly comparable, since gravity does not quantize well and gauge theories are quantum field theories.</p>

<p>In general, Uhlenbeck’s work fits in with that of a small group of differential geometers who contributed to making geometric analysis a leading subject. Probably the most obvious examples of equations which fit into geometric analysis are geometric flows (evolution equations for a functional on a manifold with geometric meaning). A famous example is the Ricci flow, used by Perelman to prove Thurston’s geometrization conjecture and by implication the Poincaré conjecture. In this flow, the metric involves according to the Ricci curvature.</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cfrac%7B%5Cpartial%20g%7D%7B%5Cpartial%20t%7D%20%3D%20-%202%5Ctext%7BRic%7D%5Cleft%28%20g%20%5Cright%29."><br></p></center>

A geometric flow is a gradient flow associated to functional on a manifold: mean curvature flow can be thought of as the gradient flow of area with respect to the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5E%7B2%7D"> norm on the surface.</p>
<p>A trick of Uhlenbeck is often used in this area when one is considering the evolution of geometric quantities such as the curvature tensor under the flow. We might for example have an evolution equation for the curvature and wish to apply some kind of maximum principle to find curvature conditions preserved under Ricci flow. If we have an evolution equation for the Einstein tensor, we need to see this tensor not only as a section of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%5E%7B%2A%7DM%5Cbigotimes%20TM">, but also as a section of the sub-bundle of sections which are symmetric with respect to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g%5Cleft%28%20t%20%5Cright%29">. This sub-bundle inherits the time dependence of the metric, which needs to be dealt with somehow<a href="#fn126" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref126" role="doc-noteref"><sup>126</sup></a>. The trick is to take an arbitrary vector bundle <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> which is isomorphic to the tangent bundle via a bundle isomorphism and fix a pullback metric <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?h"> on the fibres. We choose a bundle isometry <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?u_%7B0%7D"> at time <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0"> between <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BTM%7D"> and allow a one-parameter family of bundle endomorphisms to evolve under the equation</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cfrac%7B%5Cpartial%20u%5Cleft%28%20v%20%5Cright%29%7D%7B%5Cpartial%20t%7D%20%3D%20%5Ctext%7BRic%7D_%7Bg%5Cleft%28%20t%20%5Cright%29%7D%5Cleft%28%20u%5Cleft%28%20v%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%29."><br></p></center>
<p>In this case the pullback metric <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?h%20%3D%20u_%7B0%7D%5E%7B%2A%7D%5Cleft%28%20g_%7B0%7D%20%5Cright%29"> is constant in time as it has a zero time derivative, and the evolution equation for the metric can be used to show that</p>
<p><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?h%20%3D%20u_%7Bt%7D%5E%7B%2A%7D%5Cleft%28%20g%5Cleft%28%20t%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%29"> for all <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?t">, hence <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?u"> remains a bundle isometry between the time-dependent metric on the tangent bundle and the fixed metric on the arbitrary bundle <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V">.
