<p>Schneps is a mathematician working on various aspects of analytic number theory and Galois theory. Galois theory is a body of theory which allows us to say that the following polynomial equation</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%5E%7B5%7D%20-%206x%20%2B%203%20%3D%200%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>cannot be solved with radicals, a statement which is not immediately obvious. Given a polynomial <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> with coefficients in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D">, Galois theory provides a splitting field of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> which is the smallest possible field which contains all the zeroes of the polynomial. The splitting field has an associated finite Galois group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">. Galois theory connects field theory and group theory in a profound way, and translates the problem of solubility of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> into the problem of solubility of the associated Galois group, and as most of us know, there are many techniques in group theory which would enable us to answer this question with relative ease.</p>
<p>This raises the question as to whether all finite groups are in fact Galois groups of an extension of the radicals: this is known as the inverse Galois problem. Noether proposed that mathematicians could make progress on the inverse problem by embedding <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> in the permutation group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S_%7Bn%7D">, one obtains a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">-action on the field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D%5Cleft%28%20X_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20X_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29">. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> is the fixed field under this action, then it follows that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D%5Cleft%28%20X_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20X_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29"> is a Galois extension of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> with Galois group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">. Geometrically, the Galois extension of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> corresponds to a projection of varieties</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi%3A%5Cmathbb%7BA%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%20%5Clongrightarrow%20%5Cfrac%7B%5Cmathbb%7BA%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%7D%7BG%7D%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>for an affine <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-space over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D">. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?P"> is a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D">-rational point of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BA%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%2FG"> which is lifted to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Q%20%5Cin%20%5Cmathbb%7BA%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Coverline%7B%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D%7D%20%5Cright%29">, then the conjugates of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D"> under the action of the absolute Galois group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BGal%7D%28%5Coverline%7B%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D%7D%5Cmathbb%7B%2FQ%29%7D"> are the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BsQ%7D"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?s%20%5Cin%20H_%7BQ%7D%20%5Csubset%20G">, and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H_%7BQ%7D"> is the decomposition group at <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Q">. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H_%7BQ%7D"> is equal to the group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Q"> generates a field extension of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D"> where the Galois group is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">. A variety is rational over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D"> if it is birationally isomorphic over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BA%7D%5E%7Bn%7D">, or if the function field is isomorphic to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D%5Cleft%28%20T_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20T_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29">. It is a theorem of Hilbert’s that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BA%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%2FG"> is rational over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D">, then there is an infinite number of points <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?P"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H_%7BQ%7D%20%3D%20G">. This is a corollary of Hilbert’s irreducibility theorem.</p>

<p>We earlier mentioned the definition of a field extension in terms of it being a homomorphism (a monomorphism, to be more specific). For example, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%2FQ%7D"> is a field extension where the homomorphism is just the trivial inclusion map. <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%2FR%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%2FQ%7D"> are also field extensions with the inclusion map as the defining homomorphism. For example, the subfield of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D"> generated by the single element <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7B%201%20%5Cright%5C%7D"> is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D"> and the subfield of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D"> generated by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7B%20i%20%5Cright%5C%7D"> is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D%5Cleft%28%20i%20%5Cright%29">. <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D%5Cleft%28%20i%20%5Cright%29"> denotes an adjoining of a root. <p>We should also define the notion of the degree of a field extension. Start by noting that for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%2FK"> a field extension, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L"> is a vector space over the field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> (recall that in linear algebra, vector spaces are always defined over fields). You might wish to show to yourself that all the usual axioms for a vector space are satisfied. This kind of strange fusion between linear algebra and field theory is typical of Galois theory. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%2FK"> is an extension, then the degree of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%2FK"> (written as <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20L%5C%20%3AK%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">) is defined to be the dimension of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L"> as a vector space over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K">. The extension is said to be finite if the degree is finite, which obviously implies that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L"> is a finite-dimensional <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K">-vector space. If not, then the extension must be infinite. For example, if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%2FK"> is an extension such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20L%5C%20%3AK%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%20%3D%20p"> for some prime <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p">, then the extension is simple if and only if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%20%3D%20K%5Cleft%28%20%5Calpha%20%5Cright%29"> for some <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Calpha%20%5Cin%20L">. To prove this, start by considering the degree. We already know that</p>

<p>which implies that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%20%5Cneq%20K">, so there must exist an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Calpha"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5Cbackslash%20K">. <p>For some more definitions, start once more with a field extension <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%2FK"> with <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Calpha%20%5Cin%20L">. <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Calpha"> is said to be algebraic over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> if there is some non-zero polynomial <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> such that</p>

<p><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Calpha"> is transcendental over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> if it is not algebraic. Specifically, a complex number is said to be an algebraic number if it is algebraic over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D">. In other words, it is the zero of some polynomial with rational coefficients. A complex number is called a transcendental number if it is not an algebraic number. Although it can be very hard to prove that a number is transcendental (think about <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi">, for example), the complex plane is almost entirely composed of transcendental numbers, and it is relatively rare for a number to be algebraic. If you do not believe this, you could try looking up some pictures which provide a visualisation of the algebraic numbers in the complex plane compared to the transcendental ones. Note that this is distinct from saying that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi"> is irrational. The proof in either case is very long, but Cartwright noticed that Hermite’s proof for the irrationality of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi"> can be greatly simplified. The first example of an algebraic number which will probably occur to you is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csqrt%7B2%7D">. Again, we emphasise that this is separate from rationality or irrationality, as <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csqrt%7B2%7D"> is certainly not a rational number. This should also make it immediately clear to you that the real line also contains algebraic numbers.</p>

If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Calpha%20%5Cin%20L"> is algebraic over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K">, then the minimal polynomial exists and is unique. <p>The proof is essentially linear algebra. There is much more to say about Galois theory, but hopefully this has been good motivation. You can also go much further with the inverse Galois problem and link it to some of the other areas which we have discussed in the book (moduli spaces and mapping class groups, for example)<a href="#fn220" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref220" role="doc-noteref"><sup>220</sup></a>.</p>
<p>Schneps has also researched Lie algebras, especially an elliptic version of a Lie algebra called the Kashiwara-Vergne Lie algebra<a href="#fn221" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref221" role="doc-noteref"><sup>221</sup></a>. <p>As with any mathematical object, one is usually interested in some special types which have nice properties. For example, a Lie algebra is irreducible if the only ideals in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bg%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7B%200%20%5Cright%5C%7D">. <p>As we have already said, a Lie algebra can always be associated with a Lie group, and vice versa. A good starting point for this would be to consider the Lie algebra associated to a matrix Lie group. It is fairly typical to focus on the matrix Lie groups when beginning the study of Lie theory, as these groups are much simpler to get a handle on. A problem in Lie group theory can be transferred to the corresponding Lie algebra, and as we have emphasised, Lie algebras can be studied with elementary linear algebra techniques which we all know. For example, the Lie algebra of Euclidean space is just Euclidean space again, because it turns out to be abelian and is isomorphic to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%7D"> with the trivial Lie bracket.
