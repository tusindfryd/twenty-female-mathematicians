<p>Kirwan mostly works in algebraic and symplectic geometry. She was the second-youngest person to be elected as President of the London Mathematical Society and the first female mathematician to be elected to the historic post of Savilian Professor of Geometry. We have discussed algebraic geometry earlier in the book, but did not mention algebraic curves. These curves form possibly the richest part of algebraic geometry and have a unique status in terms of their beauty, classical prestige and importance in current mathematical research. An algebraic curve is an algebraic variety of dimension <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">. If we take an affine or projective subset (a subset of affine or projective space) together with its Zariski topology, one can define a corresponding sheaf of functions <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BO%7D_%7BX%7D"> with the appropriate restriction. The triple of a subset with the topology and the sheaf is known as an affine or a projective variety, respectively (this definition absorbs the one which we suggested earlier). One can have an affine or a projective curve accordingly, where a projective curve is a zero set of a polynomial in a projective plane.</p>
<p>A very important family of algebraic curves are the elliptic curves: these were used by Wiles in his famous proof of Fermat’s Last Theorem. An elliptic curve is by definition smooth and non-singular. It has no self-intersections or cusps (it cannot loop around on itself or curve up and back down in a way that you have a spike). Especially in applications and more computational settings, it is common to write an explicit equation for the curve known as the Weierstrass form.</p>

It can be shown that addition of points on an elliptic curve satisfies the group axioms and hence the points of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> form an abelian group where the formal point at infinity <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cinfty"> is the identity. Obviously, over a finite field there will only be a finite number of points with coordinates drawn from that field, so the group will be finite as well as abelian. In the important case of an elliptic curve defined over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D">, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E%5Cleft%28%20%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D%20%5Cright%29"> is a finitely generated abelian group. This a key result known as the Mordell-Weil theorem and for such a group we have</p>

<p>for some finite group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F">. Another key theorem is the Lutz-Nagell theorem, which says that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> is an elliptic curve in Weierstrass form with <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BZ%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?P"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E%5Cleft%28%20%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D%20%5Cright%29">, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y"> are also in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BZ%7D">, and if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y"> is non-zero, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y%5E%7B2%7D"> divides <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?4A%5E%7B3%7D%20%2B%2027B%5E%7B2%7D">. This theorem can often be used to find possible torsion points for an elliptic curve defined over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D">, where a torsion point is a point of finite order (ie. a member of the torsion group). All points in an elliptic curve over a finite field are torsion<a href="#fn203" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref203" role="doc-noteref"><sup>203</sup></a>.</p>
<p>Kirwan has worked in particular on moduli spaces of complex algebraic curves. As you might expect, a complex algebraic curve in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%5E%7B2%7D"> is a subset of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%5E%7B2%7D"> of the form</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5C%7B%20%5Cleft%28%20x%2Cy%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cin%20%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%5E%7B2%7D%5C%20%3AP%5Cleft%28%20x%2Cy%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%200%20%5Cright%5C%7D%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?P"> is a polynomial in two variables with coefficients which are complex numbers. Obviously, one can take real coefficients for the polynomial to obtain a corresponding real algebraic curve. This type of curve has been studied intensively since ancient times (the cubic curves which Newton studied and classified were real). However, complex algebraic curves can often be easier to use than their real versions. The same polynomial might factorise completely when the coefficients are complex compared to the version with real coefficients, for example. You have probably realised that a complex algebraic curve cannot be drawn in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%5E%7B2%7D"> because it has four real dimensions (the complex plane <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D"> already has two real dimensions and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%5E%7B2%7D%20%3D%20%5Cmathbb%7BC%20%5Ctimes%20C%7D">). If we add the point at infinity, we can have pictures of curves which give us a good idea of what is happening topologically, but in this case when the point <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cinfty"> is added to a complex line it becomes a sphere in topological terms. This is effectively what happens with the Riemann sphere, which can be considered as the complex projective line. This all links back in with complex analysis and the theory of Riemann surfaces, since the Riemann sphere (and every compact Riemann surface in general) is a projective algebraic curve. The symbol for a sheaf <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BO%7D_%7BX%7D"> actually originates in the complex analysis setting (from the Italian word ‘olomorfico’, meaning ‘holomorphic’).</p>
<p>We will mention some of the topological properties of complex algebraic curves. A non-singular projective curve of genus <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> in the projective plane <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BP%7D%5E%7B2%7D"> is topologically speaking a sphere with <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> handles (for a handle, imagine that you have cut through a torus to disconnect it and make it into a cylinder and then sewn both sides onto the sphere). The genus of this curve always satisfies the degree-genus formula:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g%20%3D%20%5Cfrac%7B1%7D%7B2%7D%5Cleft%28%20d%20-%201%20%5Cright%29%5Cleft%28%20d%20-%202%20%5Cright%29."><br></p></center>
<p>If the curve is an irreducible projective curve with a set of singular points <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p_%7Bi%7D">, one can assign an integer <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cdelta"> to each singularity such that Noether’s formula holds:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g%20%3D%20%5Cfrac%7B1%7D%7B2%7D%5Cleft%28%20d%20-%201%20%5Cright%29%5Cleft%28%20d%20-%202%20%5Cright%29%20-%20%5Csum_%7Bj%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7Br%7D%7B%5Cdelta%5Cleft%28%20p_%7Bi%7D%20%5Cright%29%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>One can prove the degree-genus formula by using the fact that every non-singular projective curve in the projective plane can be viewed as a branched cover of the projective line. Every non-singular projective curve in the plane (ie. every compact Riemann surface) can be triangulated and the Euler characteristic of a curve does not depend on this choice of triangulation. For the case of a compact orientable surface with no boundary and genus <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g">, the Euler characteristic is simply <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2%20-%202g">. One can prove this by starting with a surface of genus zero (a sphere). This has Euler characteristic <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2"> and one inducts on this surface<a href="#fn204" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref204" role="doc-noteref"><sup>204</sup></a>.</p>
<p>A very significant result in this area is the Riemann-Roch theorem: this theorem directly relates the complex analysis of a Riemann surface to the genus (a topological invariant). The theorem says that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D"> is a divisor on an algebraic curve <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">, then</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cdim%7BL%5Cleft%28%20D%20%5Cright%29%20-%20%5Cdim%7BH%5E%7B1%7D%5Cleft%28%20D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cdeg%20D%7D%7D%20%2B%201%20-%20%5Cdim%5Ctext%7B%5C%20H%7D%5E%7B1%7D%5Cleft%28%200%20%5Cright%29%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5Cleft%28%20D%20%5Cright%29"> is the space of meromorphic functions on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> with poles bounded by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D">. A problem where one attempts to compute the dimension of this space is known as a Riemann-Roch problem. Recall from complex analysis that a function is meromorphic on a domain if it is holomorphic (infinitely differentiable and locally equal to its own Taylor series within a neighbourhood of a point) everywhere except for a set of points. If a holomorphic function of a complex variable <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%5Cleft%28%20z%20%5Cright%29"> can be written in the form</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%5Cleft%28%20z%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cleft%28%20z%20-%20z_%7B0%7D%20%5Cright%29%5E%7Bn%7Dg%5Cleft%28%20z%20%5Cright%29"><br></p></center>
<p>for integer <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">, the function has a zero of multiplicity<img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7B%5C%20n%7D"> at <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?z_%7B0%7D">. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n%20%3D%201">, we say that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?z_%7B0%7D"> is a simple zero. A point where a meromorphic function is no longer holomorphic is called a singularity. If we can expand a function about a point <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?a"> via a Laurent series (like a Taylor series, but terms of negative degree are allowed),</p>

<p>then that point is a singularity. If there is only a finite number of terms containing negative powers of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?z%20-%20a">, then the point is a pole<a href="#fn205" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref205" role="doc-noteref"><sup>205</sup></a>.</p>
<p>If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is a Riemann surface, a divisor on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is a function <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D%5C%20%3AX%5Cmathbb%7B%5Crightarrow%20Z%7D"> such that set of points in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D%5Cleft%28%20p%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cneq%200"> is a discrete subset of that curve. The divisors of the Riemann surface form a group when equipped with pointwise addition as a group operation. In the case of a compact Riemann surface, the degree of a divisor is just the sum of the values of that divisor:</p>

<p>The set of canonical divisors form a coset of the principal divisors of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">, which themselves form a subgroup of the divisors. As it is, our statement of the Riemann-Roch theorem is not very helpful from a computational point of view, as it turns the problem of computing the dimension of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5Cleft%28%20D%20%5Cright%29"> into a problem of computing first cohomology groups, which must be identified. This can be done using the Serre duality theorem, which states that for any divisor <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D"> on an algebraic curve <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> (a compact Riemann surface if we are working over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D">), the residue map</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Res%5C%20%3A%5C%20L%5E%7B%5Cleft%28%201%20%5Cright%29%7D%5Cleft%28%20-%20D%20%5Cright%29%20%5Crightarrow%20%7B%7B%28H%7D%5E%7B1%7D%28D%29%29%5C%20%7D%5E%7B%2A%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>is an isomorphism between complex vector spaces. For a canonical divisor <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> and a general divisor <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D">, one has</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cdim%7BH%5E%7B1%7D%5Cleft%28%20D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cdim%7BL%5E%7B%5Cleft%28%201%20%5Cright%29%7D%5Cleft%28%20-%20D%20%5Cright%29%7D%7D%20%3D%20%5Cdim%7BL%5Cleft%28%20K%20-%20D%20%5Cright%29.%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>This eventually leads to the improved version of the Riemann-Roch theorem. Start with an algebraic curve <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> of genus <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g">. For any general divisor <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D"> and any canonical divisor <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K">, one has the beautiful result</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cdim%7BL%5Cleft%28%20D%20%5Cright%29%20-%20%5Cdim%7BL%5Cleft%28%20K%20-%20D%20%5Cright%29%7D%7D%20%3D%20%5Cdeg%5Cleft%28%20D%20%5Cright%29%20%2B%201%20-%20g."><br></p></center>
<p>The theorem is especially useful for computing the degree of large divisors<a href="#fn206" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref206" role="doc-noteref"><sup>206</sup></a>.</p>
<p>A moduli space of algebraic curves is a space whose points can be viewed as isomorphism classes of algebraic curves. In other words, it is a quotient space such that the equivalence relation on the set of (non-singular) curves is isomorphism, or a space which parametrises all curves of genus <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g">. (The definition also holds if we take some other type of algebraic geometric object apart from a curve). We might typically denote this space with <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BM%7D_%7Bg%7D"> where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> is the genus or algebraic genus of the curves, bearing in mind that the space will become more complicated as the genus gets larger. A moduli space is often what is called an algebraic stack, to the point where we might refer to the more fundamental moduli stack <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BM%7D_%7Bg%7D">. I will not give the definition of a stack as it is technical and you can look into it elsewhere. In some cases, it is easier to consider moduli spaces of curves with several points which are marked or ‘labelled’: this is denoted by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BM%7D_%7Bg%3Bn%7D">, where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n"> is the number of marked points. As a trivial example, the moduli space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BM%7D_%7B0%3B3%7D"> is a single point, because two points on the complex projective line given be mapped to each other by an automorphism of the line. In the case of elliptic curves with one marked point, things are already more complicated. The corresponding moduli space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BM%7D_%7B1%3B1%7D"> is the quotient of the upper half-plane by a discrete <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSL%7D%5Cleft%28%202%2C%5Cmathbb%7BZ%7D%20%5Cright%29"> group action, but it can also be represented as the quotient of a different moduli space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BM%7D_%7B0%3B4%7D"> by an action of a permutation group.</p>
<p>The general theory behind construction of moduli spaces of curves is difficult, but we can say a few general things about such spaces. The space must have a topology and a complex structure. There is some sense which can be made precise in which a projective curve which has an equation which is a small perturbation of the equation for another curve must give a point in the moduli space which is a small perturbation away from the other point. In the case with no marked points and genus greater than or equal to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3">, the moduli space of curves has to have a universal curve. More explicitly, the moduli space has to come with a complex variety <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BC%7D_%7Bg%3B0%7D"> and a map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi%3A%5C%20%5Cmathcal%7BC%7D_%7Bg%3B0%7D%20%5Crightarrow%20%5Cmathcal%7BM%7D_%7Bg%3B0%7D"> such that the fibre over a point <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%20c%20%5Cright%29"> is a complex curve whose equivalence class coincides with the corresponding point in the moduli space. A moduli space has to contain all possible families of curves. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p"> is a holomorphic map between two complex varieties <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> such that the fibre <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%20b%20%5Cright%29"> over every point <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?b"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> is the quotient of a complex curve modded out by its automorphism group, then there must exist maps from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BC%7D_%7Bg%3B0%7D"> and from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BM%7D_%7Bg%3B0%7D"> such that one can form a commutative diagram.</p>
<p>In general, one has to distinguish between the fine and the coarse moduli problem. A coarse moduli space of algebraic curves with genus <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> is a triple consisting of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BC%7D_%7Bg%3B0%7D">, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BM%7D_%7Bg%3B0%7D"> and a map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi"> between them such that every curve of genus <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> appears once as a fibre of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi"> and such that for every holomorphic family <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p%3AE%20%5Crightarrow%20B"> whose fibres are quotients as above there exist holomorphic maps which allow us to form a commutative diagram as above<a href="#fn207" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref207" role="doc-noteref"><sup>207</sup></a>. One of the simplest non-trivial examples of a moduli space is a Hilbert scheme. This a moduli space of subvarieties (subschemes, more generally) with a Hilbert polynomial in some projective space. If we take a subvariety in a complex projective space and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I_%7BX%7D%5E%7B%5Cleft%28%20n%20%5Cright%29%7D%20%5Csubset%20%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%5E%7B%5Cleft%28%20n%20%5Cright%29%7D%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2Cx_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> the space of polynomials which vanish on the variety <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">, then for large <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n"> the dimension of the quotient is a polynomial in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n"> called the Hilbert polynomial. In the special case of points in the complex plane, the Hilbert scheme is a smooth irreducible variety:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BHilb%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%5E%7B2%7D%2Cn%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5C%7B%20%5Ctext%7Bideals%5C%20%7DI%5Cmathbb%7B%5Csubset%20C%7D%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20x_%7B2%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%5Ctext%7B%5C%20of%5C%20codimension%5C%20%7Dn%20%5Cright%5C%7D.%5C%20"><br></p></center>
<p>A collection of points in the complex plane is specified by an ideal in the coordinate ring</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I_%7BP%7D%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5C%7B%20f%5Cleft%28%20p_%7B1%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cldots%20%3D%20f%5Cleft%28%20p_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%200%20%5Cright%5C%7D%5Cmathbb%7B%5Csubset%20C%7D%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20x_%7B2%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>and the codimension of this ideal is always <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">, so this is the type of ideal which is being considered in the definition. The map from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I_%7BP%7D"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?P"> extends to a map</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi_%7B%5Ctext%7BHilb%7D%7D%3A%5C%20%5Ctext%7BHilb%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%5E%7B2%7D%2Cn%20%5Cright%29%20%5Crightarrow%20%5Cfrac%7B%5Cleft%28%20%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%5E%7B2%7D%20%5Cright%29%5E%7Bn%7D%7D%7BS%5Cleft%28%20n%20%5Cright%29%7D%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>which is what is known as a resolution of singularities of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%5E%7B2%7D%20%5Cright%29%5E%7Bn%7D%2FS%5Cleft%28%20n%20%5Cright%29">. This makes this particular Hilbert scheme into a type of variety known as an equivariant symplectic resolution<a href="#fn208" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref208" role="doc-noteref"><sup>208</sup></a>.</p>
<p>Symplectic geometry is a branch of differential geometry which studies the properties of symplectic manifolds which are invariant under symplectomorphisms, where a symplectic manifold is a smooth manifold equipped with a smooth, closed, nondegenerate <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2">-form <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega">. A choice of symplectic form on a manifold is also known as a symplectric structure and a diffeomorphism between two symplectic manifolds is called a symplectomorphism if the symplectic form on one manifold is equal to the form pulled back to the other manifold by that diffeomorphism:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F%5E%7B%2A%7D%5Cwidetilde%7B%5Comega%7D%20%3D%20%5Comega."><br></p></center>
<p>In the linear algebraic setting (known as linear symplectic geometry), a nondegenerate alternating <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2">-tensor is called a symplectic tensor and a vector space with a symplectic structure is called a symplectic vector space. A <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2">-tensor is said to be nondegenerate if the equation <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega%5Cleft%28%20X%2CY%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%200"> implies that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X%20%3D%200"> for any <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y"> in the vector space. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega"> is a symplectic tensor on a vector space, it can be shown by an induction argument that the vector space must have even dimension and that there must exist a basis called the symplectic basis such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega"> can be written as</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega%20%3D%20%5Csum_%7Bi%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%5Calpha%5E%7Bi%7D%5Cbigwedge%5Ctext%7B%5C%20%CE%B2%7D%5E%7Bi%7D%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>where the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Calpha%5E%7Bi%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7B%5C%20%CE%B2%7D%5E%7Bi%7D"> are elements of the dual basis for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V%5E%7B%2A%7D">. This implies that every symplectic manifold has even dimension. The most obvious example of a symplectic vector space is the Euclidean space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B2n%7D"> equipped with a bilinear form<a href="#fn209" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref209" role="doc-noteref"><sup>209</sup></a></p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega%20%3D%20%5Csum_%7Bi%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%7Bdx%7D_%7Bi%7D%5C%20%5Cbigwedge%5C%20%7Bdy%7D_%7Bi%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>We have mentioned that moduli spaces are often constructed by quotienting out varieties by an action of a linear group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">: for simplicity, we can take a linear group to be a semi-direct product of a unipotent group with a reductive group. A very important way of developing quotients in this way for moduli spaces is Mumford’s geometric invariant theory. The basic aim of general invariant theory is to study group actions on a variety or scheme <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">. In geometric invariant theory, one studies these actions with a view to forming a geometric quotient of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> which is itself a nice scheme. To be more technical, if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is a normal quasi-projective <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">-variety, then there exists a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">-linearization for the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">-action on that variety. The goal is then to look for a natural variety which parametrises <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">-orbits in an affine or projective variety using invariant functions in the coordinate ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20X%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">. As a specific example, there is a theorem which states that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is an affine scheme over a field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> is a reductive algebraic group and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma%3AG%20%5Ctimes%20X%20%5Crightarrow%20X"> is a group action of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">, then a uniform categorical quotient <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20Y%2C%5Cphi%20%5Cright%29"> of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> exists and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y"> is an affine scheme. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is algebraic, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y"> is algebraic over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">. If the field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> has characteristic zero, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20Y%2C%5Cphi%20%5Cright%29"> is a universal categorical quotient, and if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is Noetherian, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y"> is Noetherian<a href="#fn210" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref210" role="doc-noteref"><sup>210</sup></a>.</p>
<p>We did not fully define a scheme previously, so we will make a quick digression and do this here. Start with a topological space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">. A sheaf of abelian groups on the base space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is a topological space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BO%7D"> along with a map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi"> from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BO%7D"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi"> is a local homeomorphism, the fibres <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%20p%20%5Cright%29"> are abelian groups (the definition can easily be adjusted to allow for fibres which are rings or modules), and the group operations on each fibre are continuous in the relative topology from the space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BO%7D">. The fibres of the sheaf are known as stalks in the jargon. Now, take a ringed space (ie. a sheaf of rings). If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BO%7D_%7BX%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BO%7D_%7BY%7D"> are two ringed spaces, a morphism of ringed spaces is defined to be a continuous function <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma%3AX%20%5Crightarrow%20Y"> and a collection <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpsi"> of homomorphisms <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpsi_%7BU%7D"> from<img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5C%20%5Cmathcal%7BO%7D_%7BY%7D%5Cleft%28%20U%20%5Cright%29"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BO%7D_%7BX%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Csigma%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%20U%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%29"> which respect composition with the relevant restriction maps for the ringed spaces. An affine scheme can be defined as a ringed space which is isomorphic to the structure sheaf <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20%5Ctext%7BSpec%5C%20%7DA%2C%5C%20%5Cmathcal%7BO%7D%20%5Cright%29"> of some ring. One can define morphisms of affine schemes which are locally morphisms of the schemes as ringed spaces. A general scheme is a ringed space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20X%2C%5C%20%5Cmathcal%7BO%7D%20%5Cright%29"> such that every point of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> has an open neighbourhood so that the restriction to that neighbourhood is an affine scheme modulo appropriate isomorphisms. Schemes along with their morphisms form a category, as you might expect. The definition for a scheme may seem strange, but you will have to believe me that they are the only tool which allows mathematicians to formulate problems and prove results in algebraic geometry without being in some way tied down to their geometric intuition (this was the problem which ultimately derailed the great Italian school of classical algebraic geometry). The other algebraic objects such as varieties which you might be more familiar with can be recovered from schemes<a href="#fn211" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref211" role="doc-noteref"><sup>211</sup></a>.</p>
<p>We have seen that much of the motivation for geometric invariant theory comes from the construction of moduli spaces, but it is also a developed theory in its own right, which in more algebraic terms studies the actions of reductive algebraic groups. An algebraic group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> over a field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> is a group pre-scheme <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G%2Fk"> which is also an algebraic scheme. Kirwan has recently studied the problem of generalizing geometric invariant theory to non-reductive group actions. This is a necessary step, since many of the interesting problems in the theories of moduli spaces and classical invariants to which we would we like to apply geometric invariant theory involve this type of action. There are some key aspects of reductive actions which make them much nicer to work with. For example, take an affine variety <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> with a reductive <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">-action. If there are two disjoint <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">-invariant closed subvarieties <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y_%7B1%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y_%7B2%7D"> contained in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">, then there exists an invariant <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> in the ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%7Bk%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20X%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%7D%5E%7BG%7D"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%5Cleft%28%20Y_%7B1%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%200"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%5Cleft%28%20Y_%7B2%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%201">. As another example, if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">-invariant affine subvariety of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BA%7D%5E%7Bn%7D"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> acts reductively on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BA%7D%5E%7Bn%7D">, then given <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%20%5Cin%20%7Bk%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20X%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%7D%5E%7BG%7D">, this <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> can be extended to an invariant <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F"> on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BA%7D%5E%7Bn%7D"> and if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I_%7BX%7D"> is the defining ideal of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">, then</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%7Bk%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20X%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%7D%5E%7BG%7D%20%3D%20%5Cfrac%7B%7Bk%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20%5Cmathbb%7BA%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%7D%5E%7BG%7D%7D%7BI_%7BX%7D%20%5Ccap%20%5Cmathbb%7BA%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>As another example, if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> is a reductive algebraic group acting on an affine variety <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X%20%3D%20%5Ctext%7BSpec%7D%5Cleft%28%20A%20%5Cright%29">, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A%5E%7BG%7D"> is a finitely generated <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">-algebra. For a final example, take <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> to be a reductive group as above acting on an affine or projective variety. In this case, the quotient map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?q%3A%5Ctext%7BSpec%7D%5Cleft%28%20A%20%5Cright%29%20%5Crightarrow%20%5Ctext%7BSpec%7D%5Cleft%28%20A%5E%7BG%7D%20%5Cright%29"> is a surjection, but it is possible to construct a counter-example where one has the quotient of an affine variety by a non-reductive group action which is not itself an affine variety (the above statement is effectively saying that in the reductive case the quotient would also have to be affine).</p>
<p>Most importantly, reductive geometric invariant theory often allows one to compute the open subsets made up of stable and semi-stable points, since in the case of a reductive group action on an affine variety, there always exists an invariant function which separates two disjoint closed <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">-invariant subvarieties of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">. Although many properties fail for non-reductive actions, unipotent groups do have some nice features which give motivation for non-reductive geometric invariant theory apart from greater generality and applicability to a wider range of problems (unipotent can be read here as non-reductive). As examples, every orbit for a unipotent group action on a quasi-affine variety is closed, which is useful since the theory we discuss here does not distinguish between an orbit contained inside another one. Also, every homogeneous space of a unipotent group is isomorphic to an affine space and a connected unipotent group over a field of characteristic zero has no proper finite subgroups<a href="#fn212" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref212" role="doc-noteref"><sup>212</sup></a>.</p>
<p>Kirwan has also studied the links between geometric invariant theory and so-called moment maps in symplectic geometry. The theory of classical geometric invariants in the case of complex algebraic geometry is related to reduction in symplectic geometry. <p>We will mention a few other results. If we take a compact Lie group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> and fix an inner product on the Lie algebra <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bg%7D">, then given a Hamiltonian action of the group on a symplectic manifold with an associated moment map, the norm squared of that map defines a certain Morse stratification of the manifold by locally closed symplectic submanifolds such that the stratum to which any point in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> belongs is determined by the limiting behaviour of its trajectory under the gradient flow with respect to a compatible metric. Kirwan showed that one can construct symplectic quotients in a natural way for the action of the group on the unstable strata<a href="#fn216" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref216" role="doc-noteref"><sup>216</sup></a>. With Jeffrey, Kirwan proved a residue formula which enables one to evaluate elements of the cohomology ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H%5E%7B%2A%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Cfrac%7B%5Cmu%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%200%20%5Cright%29%7D%7BG%7D%20%5Cright%29"> whose degree is the dimension of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmu%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%200%20%5Cright%29%2FG">, provided that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0"> is a regular value of the moment map. Techniques used in the proof of this formula enabled a new proof of Witten’s nonabelian localization formula for Hamiltonian actions of compact groups on symplectic manifolds<a href="#fn217" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref217" role="doc-noteref"><sup>217</sup></a>.</p>
