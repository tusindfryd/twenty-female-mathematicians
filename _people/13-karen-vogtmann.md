<p>Vogtmann is a mathematician who works primarily in geometric group theory. She is known for the introduction of Culler-Vogtmann Outer space, often shortened simply to Outer space. The idea that group theory can be applied to geometry is fairly embedded at this point, both in geometry and in mathematical physics. This began with Klein’s so-called Erlangen programme in the nineteenth century, which planned to use group theoretic methods to study geometry. As an example, the symmetries of spherical geometry in one dimension form a group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?O%5Cleft%28%202%20%5Cright%29"> formed of rotation and reflection matrices:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cbegin%7Bbmatrix%7D%0A%5Ccos%5Ctheta%20%26%20-%20%5Csin%5Ctheta%20%5C%5C%0A%5Csin%5Ctheta%20%26%20%5Ccos%5Ctheta%20%5C%5C%0A%5Cend%7Bbmatrix%7D%2C%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5Cbegin%7Bbmatrix%7D%0A%5Ccos%5Ctheta%20%26%20%5Csin%5Ctheta%20%5C%5C%0A%5Csin%5Ctheta%20%26%20-%20%5Ccos%5Ctheta%20%5C%5C%0A%5Cend%7Bbmatrix%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>Is it possible to do the reverse and apply geometric methods in order to prove things and study objects in group theory? It is, and this is what is known as geometric group theory. Gromov is usually credited with the introduction of geometric group theory as a distinct field, although there are many isolated examples in the twentieth and even the nineteenth century where mathematicians used methods which could loosely be labelled as ‘geometric group methods’: for example, when Dehn applied hyperbolic geometry backwards to solve the word problem for a surface group<a href="#fn164" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref164" role="doc-noteref"><sup>164</sup></a>.</p>
<p>The basic tool which one starts with is a Cayley graph. This is a graph in every sense of the word (a geometric object), but it also encodes information about a finitely generated group and so allows us to view a finitely generated group as a geometric object. A group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> is finitely generated if it has a finite generating set <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> and a group is generated by a finite subset within it if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5Clangle%20A%20%5Cright%5Crangle">, where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5Clangle%20A%20%5Cright%5Crangle"> is the intersection of all the subgroups of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> which contain <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A">. It is possible to view a graph <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma"> as a metric space, where the graph is thought of as a set of vertices <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> along with a set of edges <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> (by metric space, I just mean that there is a notion of distance, roughly speaking). The way of doing this is to realise <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> as a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">-complex, by viewing each edge of the graph as a copy of the unit interval where the endpoints are vertices. You can think of a cell complex as a space which is constructed by beginning with a set of points (the vertices, in this case) and then attaching cells of increasing dimension, where each cell is a space which is homeomorphic to a ball in Euclidean space. In our case, we stop with cells of dimension 1 when we construct our spaces, as we are just adding edges to vertices<a href="#fn165" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref165" role="doc-noteref"><sup>165</sup></a>.</p>
<p>The <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">-complex can now be viewed as a set, on which we define a notion of distance by parametrising each edge and so defining length for every interval in an edge. This means that a path (that is, a combinatorial sequence of edges or vertices) has a well-defined finite, non-negative length <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?l">. Given two points on the graph, we can define the distance between them to be the smallest length for any path which connects them together. The set of lengths of all such paths is a discrete set, so this minimum has to be achieved at some point. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> is a connected graph, then it follows that the set derived from the realisation of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> as a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">-complex equipped with the metric which we have defined together make up a metric space, which induces a topology on the set if we assume that the graph is locally finite. However, one can also associate a graph to a group. Start with a group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> and a subset of that group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S">: we are looking to construct a graph <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CDelta%5Cleft%28%20G%3BS%20%5Cright%29">. Let the set of vertices of this graph <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V%5Cleft%28%20%5CDelta%20%5Cright%29"> be equal to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">. For every <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> in the group and every <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?a"> in the subset, there is a directed edge from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> to the composition of elements <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7Bga%7D"> which can be labelled by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?a"> (or <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?a%5E%7B-%201%7D"> if you go in the opposite direction). The group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> acts on the vertex set by left multiplication, which extends to a free action on the graph <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CDelta">. It is also possible to start with an element of the group and get a path from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> to the composition <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7Bgp%7D%5Cleft%28%20w%20%5Cright%29">, where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p"> maps a word from the set of words in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> to the group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">. A word is simply a finite sequence of elements of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> (think of the way that you can take an alphabet for a language and form any word by making a sequence from some of the elements in that alphabet).</p>
<p>This path is the image of a path from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p%5Cleft%28%20w%20%5Cright%29"> acted on by the element <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> in the group action we have already specified. Since <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5Clangle%20S%20%5Cright%5Crangle"> is the set of elements which can be expressed as a word in the alphabet <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A">, it follows by considering the above construction that the group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> equals <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5Clangle%20S%20%5Cright%5Crangle"> if and only if the graph <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CDelta%5Cleft%28%20G%3BS%20%5Cright%29"> is connected. It means that a finitely generated group acts freely on a locally connected finite graph. In particular, if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S"> is a generating set for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CDelta"> is called the Cayley graph of the group with respect to that generating set. As a simple example, the integers <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BZ%7D"> can be generated by one single generator such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BZ%20%3D%7D%5Cleft%5Clangle%20a%20%5Cright%5Crangle">. In this case, the Cayley graph is just the line of real numbers. <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BZ%7D"> acts freely on the Cayley graph via translation. The quotient graph for the action of the group on the set of vertices always consists of a single vertex and a number of loops equal to the number of generators in the generating set, so in this case the quotient graph is one vertex and one loop ie. a circle<a href="#fn166" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref166" role="doc-noteref"><sup>166</sup></a>.</p>
<p>The main group which we are interested in modelling and studying is the free group with <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n"> generators and no relations:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F_%7Bn%7D%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5Clangle%20a_%7B1%7D%2C%5Cldots%2Ca_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%5Crangle."><br></p></center>
<p>We then consider the group of automorphisms of the free group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BAut%7D%5Cleft%28%20F_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29">, where a group automorphism is just an isomorphism which takes a group ‘back to itself’. In linear algebra, the corresponding linear transformation is an endomorphism or ‘linear self-map’ from a vector space back to that same vector space. In the case of automorphisms, the transformation must be invertible, but this is clear from the fact that the automorphism group must have an inverse to be defined as a group. An example of an automorphism of the free group might be inversion, where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?a_%7Bi%7D"> is taken to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?a_%7Bi%7D%5E%7B-%201%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?a_%7Bj%7D"> is left untouched, such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?a_%7Bi%7D%20%5Cneq"> <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?a_%7Bj%7D">. Another example might be the map which multiplies the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?i">-th generator by the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?j">-th generator from the right or the left and leaves the other group elements untouched: this is an isomorphism, as one can write down the inverse. (We can multiply from the left or the right as we are in a free group). The automorphisms of the free group can be divided further into inner and outer automorphisms. The inner automorphisms of the free group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BInn%7D%5Cleft%28%20F_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29"> form a normal subgroup of the automorphism group, where an inner automorphism is a group automorphism given by conjugation of the group by one of its elements. More importantly, we can also form the group of outer automorphisms by taking the quotient of the automorphism group with the group of inner automorphisms.</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BOut%7D%5Cleft%28%20F_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cfrac%7B%5Ctext%7BAut%7D%5Cleft%28%20F_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29%7D%7B%5Ctext%7BInn%7D%5Cleft%28%20F_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>The groups form what is called a short exact sequence.</p>

<p>Bear in mind that the group of inner automorphisms of the free group is isomorphic to the free group itself. It is also necessary to study a group which you can think of as combining elements from the free group and its automorphism group. For any <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">, we obtain a group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A_%7Bn%2Ck%20%2B%201%7D"></p>

<p>The goal in general is to try to learn more about <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BAut%7D%5Cleft%28%20F_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BOut%7D%5Cleft%28%20F_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29">.</p>
<p>Generally speaking, less is known about automorphism groups than other groups and you can learn a lot about a mathematical object by studying its automorphisms, whatever they might be. Automorphism groups are usually complicated and can be studied at several levels. For example, to study the group of automorphisms <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> of a Riemann surface <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> of genus <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> requires information about the character <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cchi"> of the action of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> on the space of holomorphic abelian differentials on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">, the signature of the Fuchsian group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma"> of the surface, and a surface kernel epimorphism <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CPhi%3A%5C%20%5CGamma%20%5Crightarrow%20G">. Recall that a Riemann surface is just a connected topological <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2">-manifold equipped with a smooth structure such that the relevant transition maps between charts are holomorphic, conformal homeomorphisms. The different types of information are related: for example, the numbers <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%7C%20%5Ctext%7BFix%7D_%7BX%2Cu%7D%5Cleft%28%20h%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%7C"> determine the character of the Riemann surface by the Eichler trace formula. Under some mild assumptions on the relevant groups and taking a compact Riemann surface with genus <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g%20%5Cgeq%202">, where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?h"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G%5E%7BX%7D"> is of order <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?m">, these numbers are defined as</p>

<p>An important geometric model for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BOut%7D%5Cleft%28%20F_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29"> introduced by Culler and Vogtmann is known as Outer space<a href="#fn168" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref168" role="doc-noteref"><sup>168</sup></a>. The development of Outer space was inspired partly by Teichm<img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%C3%BC">ller spaces which turn up in the theory of surfaces. They also have some applications in theoretical physics, appearing in string theory scattering and in some superconformal field theories, where they are associated with spaces of exactly marginal deformations. In more technical terms, it is analogous to the Teichm<img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%C3%BC">ller space associated with the mapping class group of a surface. The mapping class group of a topological surface <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F"> is the quotient group</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cfrac%7B%5Ctext%7BOut%7D_%7Bt%7D%5E%7B%2B%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Cpi_%7B1%7D%5Cleft%28%20F%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%29%7D%7B%5Ctext%7BInn%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Cpi_%7B1%7D%5Cleft%28%20F%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%29%7D%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi_%7B1%7D%5Cleft%28%20F%20%5Cright%29"> is the fundamental group for the surface. The subgroup which fixes the parabolic conjugacy classes of the group and fixes the punctures in the surface is known as the pure mapping class group. A moduli space of Riemann surfaces can be formed by quotienting the Teichm<img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%C3%BC">ller space through by the associated pure mapping class group</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BM%7D_%7Bg%2Cn%7D%20%3D%20%5Cfrac%7B%5Cmathcal%7BT%7D_%7Bg%2Cn%7D%7D%7B%5Ctext%7BPMCG%7D%7D%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> is the genus of the surface and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n"> is the number of punctures. An important subset of this moduli space is the set of Riemann surfaces such that the length of the shortest closed geodesic is at least some positive number <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cepsilon">: this subset is denoted by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BM%7D_%7Bg%2Cn%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Cepsilon%20%5Cright%29"><a href="#fn169" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref169" role="doc-noteref"><sup>169</sup></a><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?."> Mumford’s compactness theorem states that the space of Riemann surfaces <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BM%7D_%7Bg%2Cn%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Cepsilon%20%5Cright%29"> with length of closed geodesics at least <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cepsilon"> is compact. Furthermore, a sequence of representations with minimum lengths of closed geodesics bounded from below has a convergent subsequence of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BPMCG%7D">-translates<a href="#fn170" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref170" role="doc-noteref"><sup>170</sup></a>. From Mumford compactness and so-called thick-thin decomposition, it follows that studying the compactification of the moduli space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BM%7D_%7Bg%2Cn%7D"> is equivalent to studying the way that lengths of geodesics tend to zero.</p>
<p>The mapping class group acts properly discontinuously on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BT%7D_%7Bg%2Cn%7D">, and by analogy Outer space is a contractible space on which the group of outer automorphisms of the free group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BOut%7D%5Cleft%28%20F_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29"> acts properly discontinuously. An action of a group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma"> on a proper length space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is said to be properly discontinuous if for all <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?r%20%5Cgeq%200"> and all <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%20%5Cin%20X">, the set of group elements <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?d%5Cleft%28%20x%2Cgx%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cleq%20r"> is finite. The definition can also be stated without a metric by saying that it is the set of group elements <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?gK%20%5Ccap%20K%20%5Cneq%20%5Cvarnothing"> is finite for all compact <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> (since a compact set can only meet finitely many subsets of itself). As mentioned previously, the natural metric on a finite graph is as an assignment of lengths to edges. A marking of a graph is a homotopy equivalence <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> which maps the rose <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R_%7Bn%7D"> (the graph with <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1"> vertex and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n"> edges) to the graph in question. This allows for an identification between the fundamental group of the graph <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi_%7B1%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5CGamma%20%5Cright%29"> and the free group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F_%7Bn%7D"> and two markings <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%27"> are equivalent are equivalent if that is a homeomorphism <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cphi"> mapping <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma%27"> such that there <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7B%CF%95f%7D"> is homotopic to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%27">. (If you are not familiar with some of these topological definitions, just think of a homotopy as taking a function and deforming it continuously into another one). Consider two triples <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20%5CGamma%2Cl%2Cf%20%5Cright%29"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20%5CGamma%27%2Cl%27%2Cf%27%20%5Cright%29"> such that the valence of every vertex of the graphs is at least 3 and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?l"> is a metric with unit volume, an isometry <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cphi"> from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma%27"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7B%CF%95f%7D"> is homotopic to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%27"> is an equivalence. This allows for an alternative definition for Outer space</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cchi_%7Bn%7D%20%3D%20%5Cfrac%7B%5Cleft%5C%7B%20%5Cleft%28%20%5CGamma%2Cl%2Cf%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5C%7D%7D%7B%5Csim%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>as the set of equivalence classes of finite marked metric graphs with vertices of valence at least 3 and unit volume. It is more common to leave out the equivalence class notation and to simply think of Outer space as a space of marked metric graphs<a href="#fn171" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref171" role="doc-noteref"><sup>171</sup></a>. Probably the most succinct definition is to define Outer space as the space of free minimal actions of the free group by isometries of metric simplicial trees.</p>
<p>Outer space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cchi_%7Bn%7D"> has its own rich geometry and topology and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cchi_%7Bn%7D"> decomposes into open simplices. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma"> is a graph and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> is a marking, the set of possible metrics on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma"> is an open simplex:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7B%20%5Cleft%28%20l_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20l_%7BE%7D%20%5Cright%29%5C%20%3A%5C%20l_%7Bi%7D%20%3E%200%2C%5C%20%5Csum_%7Bi%7D%5E%7B%5C%20%7D%7Bl_%7Bi%7D%20%3D%201%7D%20%5Cright%5C%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>The dimension of the simplex is one below the number of edges <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E">. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"> is a forest (a disjoint union of trees) in the graph and another graph <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma%27"> is obtained by identifying all the edges of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"> with points, then the set of metrics on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma%27"> can be identified with the open face of the set of metrics on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma"> for which the coordinates of edges in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"> are <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0">. This can be stated equivalently by saying that one has collapsed a forest to get <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma%27"> from the graph <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma">, or that one has blown up a forest to get <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma"> from the graph <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma%27">. The union of the set of metrics with all of these open faces as <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"> cycles through all the possible forests in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma"> is a simplex with missing faces which can be obtained from the corresponding closed simplex by removing the appropriate open faces: this union is denoted by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CSigma%5Cleft%28%20n%20%5Cright%29">, and its smallest possible dimension is always <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n%20-%201">.</p>
<p>In topological terms, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cchi_%7Bn%7D"> is a simplicial complex of simplices with missing faces and a simplicial topology can be defined on the space analogous to that of any other simplicial complex. If one restores the missing faces, one gets a simplicial complex which is isomorphic to a sphere complex. Without going into too many details, a sphere complex <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S_%7Bn%2Ck%7D"> is a simplicial complex with vertices which are isotopy classes of spheres and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">-simplices which are compatible collections of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k%20%2B%201"> isotopy classes <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20%5CDelta_%7B0%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20%5CDelta_%7Bk%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">. A compatible collection has distinct isotopy classes and a sphere system has isotopy classes <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20s_%7B0%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20s_%7Bk%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">. Outer space is a subspace of a sphere complex <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5Cleft%28%20M_%7Bn%2Cs%7D%20%5Cright%29"> consisting of open simplices <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma%5Cleft%28%20s_%7B0%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20s_%7Bk%7D%20%5Cright%29">, where the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?s_%7Bi%7D"> form a complete sphere system and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M_%7Bn%2Cs%7D"> is defined as</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M_%7Bn%2Cs%7D%20%3D%20%5C%23%20S%5E%7B1%7D%20%5Ctimes%20S%5E%7B1%7D%5C%20%5C%20%5Cbackslash%5Ccoprod_%7Bs%7D%5E%7B%7DB%5E%7B3%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>Another basic fact about Outer space is that it is contractible. One can prove this using sphere systems. The idea of the proof is to fix a maximal sphere system and then construct paths from an arbitrary point to a simplex <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CSigma"> in the sphere complex and so contract <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cchi_%7Bn%7D"> along those paths<a href="#fn172" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref172" role="doc-noteref"><sup>172</sup></a>. During the proof, one can employ Hatcher’s normal form theorem. This states that a sphere system <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S"> is isotopic to a system which intersects <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CSigma"> transversally in circles and that in each piece <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p"> of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M_%7Bn%2Cs%7D"> (technically a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3">-punctured sphere), this intersection is always a union of discs, cylinders and pairs of pants such that different boundary components are joined by cylinders or such that there is a boundary piece of each of the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3">- spheres. This in turn can be proved using Laudenbach’s lightbulb trick: homotopic embedded spheres are isotopic<a href="#fn173" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref173" role="doc-noteref"><sup>173</sup></a>.</p>

<p>Finally, we will mention that there is a natural right action of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BOut%7D%5Cleft%28%20F_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29"> on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cchi_%7Bn%7D"> which is obtained by precomposition of the marking. An element <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CPhi"> of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BOut%7D%5Cleft%28%20F_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29"> can be considered as a homotopy equivalence from the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-rose to the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-rose. In terms of the equivalence classes we defined before, the action is then</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20%5Cleft%28%20%5CGamma%2Cl%2Cf%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%20%5Cbullet%20%5CPhi%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20%5Cleft%28%20%5CGamma%2Cl%2Cf%5CPhi%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5Crbrack."><br></p></center>
<p>This action is proper and it acts properly and cocompactly (ie. the quotient by the action is compact) on the thick part and on the spine <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K_%7Bn%2Cs%7D">, where the spine is a specific deformation retract of Outer space. In fact, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K_%7Bn%2Cs%7D"> is a contractible cube complex whose vertices are complete sphere systems in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M_%7Bn%2Cs%7D"><a href="#fn174" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref174" role="doc-noteref"><sup>174</sup></a>.</p>
<p>We have mentioned surface groups and free groups. Another important class of groups are the right-angled Artin groups. As with surface groups and many other groups, the right-angled Artin groups have finite presentations. Whether a free group has a finite presentation depends on whether or not the group is finitely generated. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma"> is a finite graph with a vertex set <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> and an edge set <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> consisting of edges, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7B%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x_%7Bi%7D%2Cx_%7Bj%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%20%5Cright%5C%7D">, the right-angled Artin group is defined by the following finite presentation</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A_%7B%5CGamma%7D%20%E2%89%94%20%5Cleft%5Clangle%20V%3A%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x_%7Bi%7D%2Cx_%7Bj%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%2C%5C%20%5C%20%5Ctext%7B%5C%20when%5C%20%7D%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x_%7Bi%7D%2Cx_%7Bj%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%20%5Cin%20E%5C%20%20%5Cright%5Crangle."><br></p></center>
<p>The first set of square brackets in the presentation is meant to indicate that all the relations are commutator relations, whereas the second set denotes an edge between two vertices. As an example, if the graph <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma"> contains no edges, then the right-angled Artin group is the free group on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n"> generators<a href="#fn175" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref175" role="doc-noteref"><sup>175</sup></a>. The outer automorphisms of the right-angled Artin groups have also been studied, and it has been shown that for a connected, triangle-free graph <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma">, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BOut%7D%5Cleft%28%20A_%7B%5CGamma%7D%20%5Cright%29"> can be defined in terms of maximal join subgraphs in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CGamma">. In fact, it is also possible to form the corresponding analogue of an Outer space for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BOut%7D%5Cleft%28%20A_%7B%5CGamma%7D%20%5Cright%29"> and show that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BOut%7D%5Cleft%28%20A_%7B%5CGamma%7D%20%5Cright%29"> acts properly on this outer space and that the outer space is contractible with a spine. Using the fact that the Tits alternative is known for outer automorphism groups of free groups, one can also show that it holds for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BOut%7D%5Cleft%28%20A_%7B%5CGamma%7D%20%5Cright%29">. The Tits alternative states that for a finitely generated linear group over a field, either the group contains a solvable subgroup of finite index, or it contains a non-abelian free group (given the name in a Russian article by Roman’kov)<a href="#fn176" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref176" role="doc-noteref"><sup>176</sup></a>.</p>
<p>An intriguing recent possibility is that of applying Outer space to QFT, although these ideas are quite far from publication at the time of writing and I will not go into too much detail as this is primarily a book about mathematics, rather than physics. For example, it has been observed that the so-called Cutkosky rules can be related to Outer space<a href="#fn177" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref177" role="doc-noteref"><sup>177</sup></a>. The Cutkosky rules are rules for dealing with certain Feynman diagrams, so I will say a few words about QFT to set the scene. If we start from non-relativistic quantum mechanics (we might be considering an electron which is moving at speed well below light speed, for example) and it is being scattered by something, we would like to study the scattering amplitude of the outgoing wave compared to the incoming wave which is incident on the thing which is doing the scattering (everything is in terms of wave functions which solve the Schrodinger 'wave' equation, hence viewing things as waves).&nbsp; The amplitude could be imaginary, but you always square the modulus and so the probability ends up being a real number.&nbsp; The collision can be elastic or inelastic (kinetic energy may or may not be conserved), but what you can do is consider the scattering amplitude as a function of energy where energy is a complex variable. Since it is a function of a complex variable, it can have branch points in the complex plane.</p>
<p>This scattering amplitude has a branch point at <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E%20%3D%200"> which extends into a branch cut along the entire positive real axis, meaning that the imaginary part of the scattering amplitude is a discontinuity across this cut.&nbsp; This essentially means that the classical optical theorem can be stated for quantum mechanics as well as for classical physics. The optical theorem states that the imaginary part of the scattering amplitude for scattering straight forwards with angle <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0"> is proportional to the cross section of the scattering object (not necessarily the same as its geometric area)<a href="#fn178" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref178" role="doc-noteref"><sup>178</sup></a>.&nbsp; The optical theorem can be extended to field theory as you might expect. The easiest way to prove the optical theorem is to use an operator called the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S">-matrix, or scattering matrix, which gives the amplitude for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n"> particles all with their own wave functions to scatter and turn into <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?m"> particles with their own new wave functions (defined in an asymptotic way by assuming they are coming in from a long way out and going back out a long way to infinity).&nbsp; The theorem is then a consequence of the fact that the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S">-matrix is a unitary operator and it can be stated equivalently as an identity for the imaginary part of an S-matrix element.&nbsp; In QFT, the number of particles going in does not have to equal the number going out because of Einstein’s equation <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E%20%3D%20mc%5E%7B2%7D."></p>
<p>The question is, how does this imaginary part of an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S">-matrix element arise when you do a Feynman diagram expansion?&nbsp; If you have a quantum field theory with allows for interaction between fields (unlike a free field theory where things travel along and never 'see' each other), then you might be able to do a perturbation expansion.&nbsp; A field theory has to have a functional integral (ie. a Feynman path integral), so if you expand the interaction part as a Taylor series you end up with a number of terms which are basically the same and which can be collected together with a Feynman diagram<a href="#fn179" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref179" role="doc-noteref"><sup>179</sup></a>. Consider, for example, if we take a functional defined as</p>

<p>Each term in this dual expansion can be seen to produce many expressions which are algebraically identical and which are organised with Feynman diagrams.</p>
<p>A tree level diagram with no loops corresponds to zero order, one loop is first order, and so on.&nbsp; The Feynman rules are rules for perturbation theory and the ordinary assumptions which are made for applications of perturbation theory are exactly the same ie. the parameter multiplying the non-quadratic part of the potential (a physical 'coupling constant' in this case) has to be sufficiently small.&nbsp; The coupling constant in quantum electrodynamics is a constant equal to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1%2F137">, so QED is very amenable to perturbation theory.&nbsp; As you might know already, if you go too far in the expansion, you will quickly find that the integrals always diverge and so the diagrams are infinite, hence the need for renormalization to somehow 'get rid' of the infinities and 'subtract them out of existence'.</p>
<p>It turns out that a Feynman diagram only contributes an imaginary part to a matrix element when virtual particles are on-shell. On-shell means that they obey the relativistic energy-momentum relation from special relativity: since they are virtual and 'flitter' in and out of existence during a process such as a collision, they don't have to obey this relation.&nbsp; In mathematical terms, as you might guess, there will not be an imaginary part to the matrix element unless there is a branch cut.&nbsp; The branch cut is along the real axis as before but now it starts at a threshold energy <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?s_%7B0%7D"> (again, we are considering the matrix element as a function of energy as a complex variable, or as a function of momentum where there is a threshold momentum).&nbsp; It might not be obvious, but if you write out a Feynman diagram and then construct the expression for it, there could be a branch cut along the real axis (this might well be from a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Clog"> term, which has a well-known branch cut). This means you can compute the imaginary part which a Feynman diagram contributes to a matrix element by computing the discontinuity of the Feynman diagram across its branch cut.&nbsp; What Cutkosky showed is that you can do this for any order and that you can always do it with a simple set of rules<a href="#fn180" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref180" role="doc-noteref"><sup>180</sup></a>.</p>
<p>For the first step, you draw the Feynman diagram and cut through it in every way such that the cut propagators can be put on-shell at the same time (for example, if you have a diagram with a loop in the middle). Both sides of the loop contribute their own propagators, since a line corresponds to a propagator in a Feynman diagram (a propagator being the amplitude for a particle to travel from one place to another, but you can also view it as a Green's function if preferable).&nbsp; Each of those contributes a propagator term when you write out the expression for the diagram using the Feynman rules, so it's a question of whether both of the integrals can be written in such a way that the momenta in the expressions obey the on-shell condition: it might depend on the region of momentum integration as to whether or not this is possible.&nbsp;</p>

<p>In the second step, for each cut propagator you replace the propagator term with a Dirac delta function multiplied by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2%5Cpi%20i">.&nbsp; This essentially comes from the Cauchy residue theorem, as you are somehow closing the contour of integration around the poles which are present and picking up the residues.&nbsp; You integrate over the delta functions for each cut. Finally, you sum all the contributions from every cut and you have computed the discontinuity of any Feynman diagram, and that in turn gives you its imaginary contribution to an S-matrix element, the S-matrix elements being the things with direct physical interpretation which can be measured. Because we can do this, we can prove the optical theorem in QFT to all orders and generalise the theorem to all Feynman diagrams, which means that you can use them to evaluate other amplitudes which can be defined in terms of Feynman diagrams, even if they are not <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S">-matrix elements: this is significant for studying unstable particles.&nbsp; Unstable particles and 'resonances' are studied in non-relativistic quantum mechanics, but we would also like to be able to deal with them in field theory, especially in high-energy physics where most things you might discover in a collider experiment are unstable.</p>
<p>Another related idea is that the analytic structure of Feynman amplitudes viewed as functions into the complex plane can be connected to Outer space<a href="#fn181" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref181" role="doc-noteref"><sup>181</sup></a>. One can study functions which select a Feynman diagram from a Hopf algebra of diagrams and evaluate them with renormalized Feynman rules to map them into <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D">, depending on the rules which in turn depend on the field theory. One then has a multi-valued amplitude whose properties can be studied using techniques from complex analysis and algebraic geometry, an approach going back to Landau or earlier. The evaluation of a Feynman diagram is a loop integral and it is possible to always regard this amplitude as an iterated integral built up from amplitudes for one-loop graphs such as tadpoles. It is not possible to define the function which evaluates a diagram with rules as a unique function if you define it as an iterated integral, but you can define an equivalence relation on principal sheets. The iteration of the integral then gives the function a structure which is closely linked to that of the correct coloured Outer space.</p>
<p>As a Feynman amplitude is a many-valued function with branch points, there exists a group generated by the branch cycles known as the ‘monodromy’ of the graph, in the sense that the monodromy group encodes the extent to which an amplitude fails to be single-valued as it goes around a branch point with non-trivial monodromy. The generators of the monodromy group for an amplitude can be found by computing the variation of the amplitude with Cutkosky’s theorem. The wider importance of this is that the generators of the fundamental group for the graph (a simple object) can be mapped to the generators of the monodromy group of the amplitude, so, for example, a single loop like the triangle graph has one generator for its fundamental group, meaning that there is just one generator for the monodromy group, which then can be used to define the multi-valued functions which are assigned to the amplitude.&nbsp;</p>
<p>These different multi-valued functions are needed to study the sheet structure of the amplitude. The functions have representations in terms of coloured graphs (for example, there will be <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3"> coloured graphs in the case of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3"> functions) and the functions are needed for the simplex or cell which is being integrated over. In the case of what is called the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3">-edge banana graph <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?b_%7B3%7D">, the simplex is a triangle. As the corners of the triangle are not in Outer space, they must be changed to arcs, so there are <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3"> functions associated to the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3"> arcs and then equivalence relations to change between the arcs<a href="#fn182" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref182" role="doc-noteref"><sup>182</sup></a>. For the example of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?b_%7B3%7D">, one can take the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3"> equivalent markings for the graph and each marking determines a multi-loop integral for the amplitude. However, there are two generators for the monodromy group of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?b_%7B3%7D">, so essentially there is a connection between the number of markings for the appropriate subgraph (the two-edge banana graph <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?b_%7B2%7D"> which has <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2"> markings) and the generators of the monodromy group of the graph itself, with the choice of edges determining the integral. The ultimate aim is to get information on the sheet structure of the associated amplitude and to evaluate a parametric Feynman integral by assigning an open simplex from Outer space to the Feynman diagram (for example, a tetrahedron for the dunce’s cap graph). One then computes the Feynman integral by integrating over the volume of that simplex.</p>
<p>In a more general setting, Vogtmann and other mathematicians have constructed a geometric space of trees which models the set of all phylogenetic trees. These trees are diagrams familiar from evolutionary biology which detail the relationships between species: the trees generally have a distinguished vertex called the root which indicates the common descendant of all the species from which the diagram grows<a href="#fn183" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref183" role="doc-noteref"><sup>183</sup></a>. The diagrams will also have a number <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n"> of ‘final’ nodes of degree 1 which are called leaves in the formal terminology of the space of trees. Each space of trees will consist only of trees with the same number of leaves. The space has a metric of non-positive curvature, giving a formal way of measuring distances between different phylogenetic trees or finding trees which are a fixed distance from other trees. This model also allows rigorous justification for the practice of discarding sections of a set of trees which agree with each other under comparison<a href="#fn184" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref184" role="doc-noteref"><sup>184</sup></a>.</p>
