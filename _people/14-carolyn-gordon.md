<p>Gordon is a fellow of the American Mathematical Society and the American Association for the Advancement of Science. She specializes in the study of homogeneous spaces and isospectral geometry, and is best known for providing a negative answer (with Webb and Wolpert) to the question ‘Can you hear the shape of the drum?’ (first asked by Kac). This was done by constructing two subsets of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B2%7D"> which have different shapes but the same eigenvalues<a href="#fn185" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref185" role="doc-noteref"><sup>185</sup></a>. To be more technical, they found two domains which have different shapes and yet the spectrum of their Laplacians has the same frequency. In fact, the theory of the Laplacian on a Riemannian manifold is very deep. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20M%2Cg%20%5Cright%29"> is a compact Riemannian manifold, there has to be a Laplace operator <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathrm%7B%5CDelta%7D"> on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> which acts on smooth functions (<img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0">-forms) on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M">:</p>

<p>The spectrum of a linear operator is a generalization of the concept of a set of eigenvalues of a matrix which you are probably familiar with from linear algebra. Depending on the choice of basis for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"> can be represented by a matrix. In that case, the spectral theory just becomes the elementary theory of eigenvalues for matrices. For a given square matrix <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A">, eigenvalues and eigenvectors are defined in terms of an equation</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Ax%20%3D%20%5Clambda%20x."><br></p></center>
<p>An eigenvalue of such a square matrix is a number <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Clambda"> such the above equation has a non-trivial solution and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x"> is an eigenvector of the matrix corresponding to that eigenvalue. I will not describe the spectral theory for square matrices any further as it can be looked up in any textbook on finite dimensional linear algebra.</p>
<p>In infinite dimensions, things become more complicated. It is important to allow infinite dimensional spaces for physical applications, since the state spaces of quantum mechanics must be allowed to have infinite dimension. Historically, quantum mechanics provided much of the impetus for the development of functional analysis. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is infinite dimensional, it is possible to have a linear operator <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"> which has values in its spectrum which are not eigenvalues of that operator. For example, take the space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?l%5E%7B2%7D"> and define a linear operator called the right-shift operator which takes us back into <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?l%5E%7B2%7D">.</p>

However, a lot of the relevant theory does carry over if we stick to compact operators: the spectral properties of compact operators on Hilbert spaces are very close to those of operators on finite dimensional spaces. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y"> are two normed spaces, an operator <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%3AX%20%5Crightarrow%20Y"> is compact if for every bounded subset <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> the closure of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%5Cleft%28%20M%20%5Cright%29"> is also compact<a href="#fn186" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref186" role="doc-noteref"><sup>186</sup></a>.</p>
<p>In particular, the eigenvector decomposition of the normed space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> goes through the same if we take <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> an infinite dimensional Hilbert space and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"> a compact operator. Again, the Hilbert space has an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors for the operator and the eigenspaces are finite. The linear operator is also bounded by definition. A linear operator on an infinite dimensional space might be unbounded even if the range is finite dimensional. Maybe the most important unbounded linear operator is the differentiation operator which might be familiar from quantum mechanics:</p>

<p>Two Riemannian manifolds are isospectral if the spectra of their Laplacians coincide, so the question arises as to whether two isospectral Riemannian manifolds must also be isometric. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> has nonempty boundary, one can consider the Dirichlet spectrum, which is the spectrum of the Laplacian acting on smooth functions which vanish at the boundary (so-named because of the corresponding Dirichlet boundary condition). For a region of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B2%7D">, the Dirichlet eigenvalues of the Laplacian are basically the frequencies which a drumhead under tension would produce if it were shaped like that region, hence the formulation in terms of vibrating drums. Gordon, Webb and Wolpert answered the question in the negative by constructing two simply connected plane regions which are non-isometric and whose Dirichlet and Neumann spectra coincide. In fact, Milnor disposed of the question for higher dimensional manifolds quite early on by constructing a pair of isospectral <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?16">-dimensional tori which were non-isometric, but the corresponding problem in the plane remained open for several decades afterwards. One notes that simply being in lower dimension does not necessarily make a conjecture easier to prove or disprove, as it always depends on the nature of the problem at hand.</p>
<p>A key tool in the proof was Sunada’s theorem. This states that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> is a Riemannian manifold on which a finite group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> acts by isometries, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> are subgroups of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> which act freely and there is a bijection <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%3AH%20%5Crightarrow%20K"> taking every element of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H"> to an element <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%5Cleft%28%20h%20%5Cright%29"> which is conjugate to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?h"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">, then the quotient manifolds <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H%5Cbackslash%20M"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K%5Cbackslash%20M"> are isospectral. Gordon, Webb and Wolpert used Sunada’s method to construct a pair of isospectral of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2">-orbifolds with boundary. However, the Neumann spectra of the orbifolds are the same as the Neumann spectra of the underlying manifolds, but the underlying spaces are simply connected plane regions. A slightly more complicated argument can be used to deduce the Dirichlet isospectrality of the underlying spaces<a href="#fn188" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref188" role="doc-noteref"><sup>188</sup></a>.</p>
<p>Gordon has continued to do wide-ranging research on various aspects of isospectral Riemannian manifolds (much of this research being motivated by the idea that geometric and topological information about compact manifolds can be encoded in the spectra of natural operators on those manifolds). In particular, the overarching question is: to what extent does the spectrum of the Laplacian on a Riemannian manifold actually determine the geometry of the manifold? Low-dimensional round spheres are certainly determined by their spectra in a unique way, but there are other cases where geometry is not controlled by the spectrum. For dimension <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n%20%5Cgeq%208"> there are isospectral deformations of Riemannian metrics on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7Bn%7D"> which can be chosen to be arbitrarily close to the round metric, and for dimension <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n%20%5Cgeq%209"> there are isospectral deformations of Riemannian metrics on the ball in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%7D"> which can be chosen to be arbitrarily close to the flat metric<a href="#fn189" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref189" role="doc-noteref"><sup>189</sup></a>.</p>
<p>Some of Gordon’s earlier work focussed on isometry groups of homogeneous manifolds. Homogenous spaces in some sense look the same wherever you are on the space, and so they play a key role in modelling the Universe in cosmology, where it is assumed that the Universe looks the same everywhere on the large scale. A Riemannian manifold <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20M%2Cg%20%5Cright%29"> will have a group of Riemannian isometries <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BIso%7D%5Cleft%28%20M%2Cg%20%5Cright%29"> which map the manifold back to itself. Informally speaking, the metric measures lengths of tangent vectors and so it can be used to measure lengths of curves.</p>
<p>The manifold is homogeneous if the isometry group acts transitively such that there is always an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F%20%5Cin%20%5Ctext%7BIso%7D%5Cleft%28%20M%2Cg%20%5Cright%29"> with the property that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F%5Cleft%28%20p%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20q"> for two points <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p%2C%5C%20q%20%5Cin%20M">. The isotropy group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BIso%7D_%7Bp%7D%5Cleft%28%20M%2Cg%20%5Cright%29"> at the point <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p"> is the set of isometries in the group such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F%5Cleft%28%20p%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20p."> For a homogeneous space such as Euclidean space, one has that the space is isomorphic to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Iso%2F%5Ctext%7BIso%7D_%7Bp%7D"> for any point <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p">. As an example, if we take the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-sphere equipped with the canonical metric, the Riemannian isometry group is as follows:</p>

<p>which is also the isotropy group of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%20%2B%201%7D%2C%5Ctext%7Bcan%7D%20%5Cright%29"> at the point <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0">. <p>are both smooth) and trivialise the tangent space using left or right translations on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">:</p>

<p>The left and right translation maps are self-maps of the Lie group defined by</p>

A tangent vector in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T_%7Be%7DG"> can be extended to a left (or right) invariant vector field by left (or right) translation to other parts of the manifold, hence <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T_%7Be%7DG"> can be identified with the space of left invariant vector fields. <p>However, the space of all smooth left-invariant vector fields on a Lie group is an example of a Lie algebra, which is referred to as the Lie algebra <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bg%7D"> of the group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">. A Lie algebra in general is simply a real vector space equipped with a map from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bg%20%5Ctimes%20g%7D"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bg%7D"> called the bracket which satisfies the Jacobi identity along with bilinearity and antisymmetry. One can also show that the connected component of the Lie group which contains the identity element is the only connected open subgroup of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> and that every connected component of the group is diffeomorphic to this component.</p>
<p>Because of the above, the canonical inner product on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T_%7Be%7DG"> induces a Riemannian metric on the Lie group, so left translations are Riemannian isometries. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H"> is a closed normal Lie subgroup of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G">, the quotient <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G%2FH"> is a smooth manifold by the quotient manifold theorem, and it follows that it also a Lie group and that the quotient map is a Lie group homomorphism. In fact, if we equip <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> with a metric for which all right translations are Riemannian isometries, then there is a unique Riemannian metric on the quotient group such that the quotient map is a submersion. If the metric is left invariant, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> acts as an isometry group on the quotient, making <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G%2FH"> into a homogeneous manifold<a href="#fn191" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref191" role="doc-noteref"><sup>191</sup></a>. If we start with the complex space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%5E%7Bn%20%2B%201%7D">, this has <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B2n%20%2B%201%7D"> as a subset. The Lie group acts as an isometry group on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%5E%7Bn%20%2B%201%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B2n%20%2B%201%7D"> and the quotient map</p>

<p>Since <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B1%7D"> acts smoothly as a Lie group by isometries of the round metric, the metric on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B2n%20%2B%201%7D"> induces a homogeneous, isotropic metric on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7B%5Ctext%7BCP%7D%7D%5E%7Bn%7D"> such that the map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5E%7B2n%20%2B%201%7D%20%5Crightarrow%20%5Cmathbb%7B%5Ctext%7BCP%7D%7D%5E%7Bn%7D"> is a Riemannian submersion: this metric is called the Fubini-Study metric<a href="#fn192" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref192" role="doc-noteref"><sup>192</sup></a>.</p>

<p>Spinors are defined via representations of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSL%7D%5Cleft%28%202%2C%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%20%5Cright%29">. Space spinors could be used for problems where one is working on Riemannian <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3">-manifolds (assuming existence of a spinor structure equipped with a Hermitian product)<a href="#fn194" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref194" role="doc-noteref"><sup>194</sup></a>. <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSL%7D%5Cleft%28%202%2C%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%20%5Cright%29"> is the universal covering group of the Lorentz group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSO%7D%5Cleft%28%203%2C1%20%5Cright%29">. (If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> is a connected Lie group, then there exists a simply connected Lie group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G%27"> called the universal covering group and a smooth covering map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi%3AG%20%5Crightarrow%20G%27"> which is a homomorphism of Lie groups: see a textbook on algebraic topology or smooth manifolds if you need to revise elementary covering space theory). If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> is the fundamental representation of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSL%7D%5Cleft%28%202%2C%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%20%5Cright%29"> on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%5E%7B2%7D">, this representation is equipped with an invariant symplectic form, but there is no hermitian structure on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D%5E%7B2%7D">, as the size of vectors does not change.</p>
<p>Take a ball of radius <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi"> and consider the map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B%5E%7B3%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Cpi%20%5Cright%29%20%5Crightarrow%20SO%5Cleft%28%203%20%5Cright%29"> which takes the pair <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20r%2C%5Ctheta%20%5Cright%29"> to a rotation by the angle <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?r"> with the axis along <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctheta">. This is a surjective map, as one can always find a point by picking an axis and taking any rotation. Rotations can only be the same if we rotate by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%E2%80%93%5Cpi">, so only antipodal points can be identified. This means that</p>

Covering maps are themselves local Riemannian isometries.</p>
<p>An important and useful class of homogeneous spaces are the symmetric spaces (introduced and studied by Cartan). A Riemannian manifold <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20M%2Cg%20%5Cright%29"> is a symmetric space if for every point in the manifold, the isotropy group for that point <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BIso%7D_%7Bp%7D"> contains an isometry <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A_%7Bp%7D"> such that the differential <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?DA_%7Bp%7D%3A%5C%20T_%7Bp%7DM%20%5Crightarrow%20T_%7Bp%7DM"> is the antipodal map. The fact that isometries preserve geodesics can be used to show that such a space is homogeneous. Symmetric spaces are geodesically complete, meaning that any geodesic can be extended indefinitely in both directions. This can be proved using the fact that an involution of a symmetric space switches geodesics through <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p">. A Riemannian manifold is symmetric if for every point in the manifold there is an isometry <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma_%7Bp%7D"> called an involution such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma_%7Bp%7D%5Cleft%28%20p%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20p%2C"><br></p></center>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?D%5Csigma_%7Bp%7D%5Cleft%28%20p%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20-%20Id."><br></p></center>
<p><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7B%5Ctext%7BCP%7D%7D%5E%7Bn%7D"> equipped with the Fubini-Study metric is an example of a symmetric space. In a symmetric space, any two points can be connected by a geodesic. Any Lie group with a bi-invariant metric is a symmetric space, since the inverse operation forms the required symmetry around the identity element. These include some of the well-known Lie groups: <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSU%7D%5Cleft%28%20n%20%2B%201%20%5Cright%29">, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSO%7D%5Cleft%28%202n%20%2B%201%20%5Cright%29">, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSp%7D%5Cleft%28%20n%20%5Cright%29"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSO%7D%5Cleft%28%202n%20%5Cright%29">. Interestingly, the symmetric spaces come in pairs composed of a compact and a non-compact space. <p>Gordon has recently worked on a particular type of complete Riemannian manifold known as a geodesic orbit manifold: <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20M%2Cg%20%5Cright%29"> is a geodesic orbit manifold if every geodesic is an orbit of a one-parameter group of isometries. With Nikonorov, Gordon showed that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20M%2Cg%20%5Cright%29"> is an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-dimensional, simply connected, geodesically orbit manifold, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20M%2Cg%20%5Cright%29"> is isometric to a non-compact symmetric space, a simply connected, geodesically orbit Riemannian nilmanifold, or the total space of a Riemannian submersion with totally geodesic fibres, where the base space is a non-compact symmetric space and the fibres are isometric to a simply connected, geodesically orbit Riemannian nilmanifold. <p>In connection with the spectral theory of the Laplacian and bi-invariant metrics on Lie groups, Gordon, Schueth and Sutton showed that a bi-invariant metric on a compact connected Lie group is spectrally isolated in the class of left-invariant metrics. The term ‘spectral isolation’ refers to the question of whether special types of Riemannian manifold such as symmetric spaces have spectra which can be distinguished from those of other Riemannian manifolds. Aside from manifolds of constant curvature, there are not any examples of metrics which are spectrally isolated from other arbitrary Riemannian metrics, but one can restrict to classes of metrics: for example, any set of mutually isospectral compact symmetric spaces is finite. Gordon considered the question as to whether symmetric spaces can be spectrally distinguished from other homogeneous Riemannian manifolds: more specifically, if we restrict to compact symmetric spaces which are given by bi-invariant Riemannian metrics on compact Lie groups, is such a metric spectrally isolated if we stay in the class of left-invariant Riemannian metrics? The answer is yes, and Gordon obtained a stronger quantitative result: given a bi-invariant metric <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> there is a positive integer <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n"> such that in a neighbourhood of the metric in the class of left-invariant metrics with the same volume or less, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"> is uniquely determined by the first <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n"> distinct non-zero eigenvalues of the Laplacian<a href="#fn198" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref198" role="doc-noteref"><sup>198</sup></a>.</p>

<p>This system describes the dynamics of a charged particle moving in the magnetic field, and could presumably be used to model the dynamics of plasma inside a tokamak<a href="#fn199" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref199" role="doc-noteref"><sup>199</sup></a>. The idea of applying Hamiltonian dynamics to plasma confinement is not restricted to tokamaks, and is also being considered for devices known as stellarators. In this case, one is looking to have dynamics for the guiding-centre which is integrable or almost integrable to maximise confinement and no or small toroidal current. The condition on the toroidal current requires deviation from axisymmetry. Integrability is guaranteed in the case of quasisymmetry, but it is believed that the only true quasisymmetries are axisymmetries, so scientists will have to develop ways of testing the deviation from integrability which will necessarily result in the design of stellarator devices.</p>
<p>In general, magnetic confinement uses electromagnetic fields to confine a hot plasma of deuterium and tritium as it circulates in a region. A major practical difficulty involved in controlled fusion is that the fuel must be confined over a long time at extreme temperatures, with the fields being necessary to keep the fuel away from the walls of the tokamak. The fields can only confine the plasma for a short period of time and the plasma is prone to instabilities due to the temperatures and the strong magnetic fields. The tokamak itself must also be able to withstand the electromagnetic forces which are being generated. To assess these, one assumes the fusion device to be a three-dimensional conducting structure <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V_%7Bc%7D"> which surrounds a plasma torus. <p>The model for the conducting structure can be combined with a model for the plasma, which would typically be modelled in the form of single-fluid magnetohydrodynamics equations defined between the plasma and the walls of the device. The plasma model would need to be discretized using similar methods and then combined with the differential equation for the potentials. As mentioned earlier, there is always the possibility of instabilities developing in the plasma, even when it is in its equilibrium configuration: these perturbations can die down or they can grow. In particular, there is interest in so-called resistive wall modes. These are unstable modes which develop when the plasma requires a perfectly conducting wall to stabilize its external kink modes. A vacuum chamber surrounding a plasma is a good approximation to a perfectly conducting wall, but its conductivity is finite. Working through the eigenvalue problem, it can be shown that finite wall resistivity destroys kink stability and so resistive wall modes are inherently unstable. The modes can be computed using inverse iteration algorithms, but this becomes very computationally intense, and there would probably need to be some development of parallel computing techniques before monitoring of instabilities in tokamaks becomes feasible<a href="#fn200" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref200" role="doc-noteref"><sup>200</sup></a>. Another possibility for a magnetic confinement device is the Reversed Field Pinch, which has similar magnetic field strength in both the toroidal and the polar direction, whereas the tokamak has a stronger field in the toroidal direction.</p>
