<p>Mirzakhani was known for her profound work on symplectic geometry, hyperbolic geometry and moduli spaces of Riemann surfaces. She began publishing mathematical articles on a variety of topics from an early age and was the first woman and the first Iranian to win the prestigious Fields medal for achievements in mathematics. We mentioned Teichmüller spaces earlier in connection with the mapping class group, but did not really define them. A compact Riemann surface with a finite number of punctures is said to be of finite type. If you fix a smooth compact reference surface <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F"> with genus <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g">, every diffeomorphism <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F"> into a Riemann surface <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> can be pulled back to a conformal structure on the reference surface (denote the set of all the conformal structures on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F"> by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S%5Cleft%28%20F%20%5Cright%29">). If one denotes the group of orientation-preserving diffeomorphisms as usual by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BDiff%7D%5E%7B%2B%7D%5Cleft%28%20F%20%5Cright%29">, then the quotient <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BM%7D%5Cleft%28%20F%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20S%5Cleft%28%20F%20%5Cright%29%2F%5Ctext%7BDiff%7D%5E%7B%2B%7D%5Cleft%28%20F%20%5Cright%29"> is the space of conformal structures on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F"> with <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n"> labelled, distinguished points (this is simply the moduli space of Riemann surfaces of type <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F">. If one quotients through instead by the normal subgroup of diffeomorphisms which are homotopic to the identity map, one obtains the space of marked conformal structures on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F">:</p>

<p>This is the Teichmüller space of surfaces of type <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F">. If one quotients thorough the first diffeomorphism group by the second group, one obtains the pure mapping class group which we mentioned before.</p>
<p>We will state another definition for something you may have heard mentioned before: a train track for a hyperbolic surface <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> with cusps is an embedded family of smooth curves with a finite set of vertices called switches and edges called branches. There is a unique tangent at each switch such that curves meet at the switch with the same tangent line and the neighbourhood of a switch is a union of disjoint smoothly embedded arcs. There are some further restrictions on the form that a component of the complement of the train track can take. As with many concepts in modern geometry, the concept of the train track was introduced by Thurston, who needed something which would give a piecewise linear symplectic structure for the space of measured geodesic laminations <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7B%5Ctext%7BMGL%7D%7D">. A lamination is a foliation of a closed subset<a href="#fn245" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref245" role="doc-noteref"><sup>245</sup></a>.</p>
<p>Recall that a foliation of dimension <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> on an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-manifold is a collection of disjoint, connected, immersed <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">-dimensional submanifolds of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> such that the union is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> and in a neighbourhood of a point in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> there is a smooth chart <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20U%2C%5Cvarphi%20%5Cright%29">, where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cvarphi%5Cleft%28%20U%20%5Cright%29"> is a product of connected open subsets <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U%27%20%5Ctimes%20U%27%27%20%5Csubset%20%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bk%7D%20%5Ctimes%20%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%20-%20k%7D">. Also, each leaf of the foliation intersects <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U"> on a countably large union of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">-dimensional slices. A chart of this kind is called a foliated chart. The immersed submanifolds are actually equivalence classes, known as the leaves of the foliation, where foliation is an equivalence relation. These criteria are met trivially in the case where we take the set of all <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">-dimensional affine subspaces of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%7D"> parallel to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bk%7D">. As another example, if one takes the set of connected components of the curves in the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20y%2Cz%20%5Cright%29">-plane given by the following equations</p>

A lamination <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BG%7D"> is geodesic if its leaves are complete geodesics. A transverse measure <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmu"> for a geodesic lamination is an assignment for each smooth transverse arc <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctau"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BG%7D"> a non-negative measure whose support is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctau%20%5Ccap%20%5Cmathcal%7BG%7D">. A measured geodesic lamination is the union of a finite number of simple closed geodesics and a perhaps uncountable number of open complete geodesics and the complement of a measured geodesic lamination is a finite union of convex subsurfaces with geodesic boundaries.</p>
<p>A geodesic lamination is carried by a train track <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctau"> provided that there is a differentiable map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> of the hyperbolic surface which is homotopic to the identity map and such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%5Cleft%28%20%5Cmu%20%5Cright%29"> is contained in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctau"> and the differential does not vanish on a leaf. Given a marked hyperbolic surface, the mapping class group acts on the associated space of measured geodesic laminations. A fundamental result is that the action of the mapping class group on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7B%5Ctext%7BMGL%7D%7D"> is ergodic<a href="#fn247" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref247" role="doc-noteref"><sup>247</sup></a>. This terminology may strange if you have studied some ergodic theory, but remember that we have defined a measure on the space. In that case, we actually have the group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BMCG%7D"> acting on a measure space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20%5Cmathcal%7BMGL%2CA%2C%5C%20%7D%5Cmu%20%5Cright%29">, where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BA%7D"> is a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma">-algebra. A <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma">-algebra on a set <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is a family of subsets which contains <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> and is closed under taking of complements, under formation of countable unions, and under formation of countable intersections.</p>
<p>As a trivial example, take the collection of all the subsets of a set <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">. This is a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma">-algebra, but if we take the collection of all the finite subsets of an infinite set <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">, then this collection cannot contain <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">. It is therefore not closed under taking of complements, and it cannot be a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma">-algebra (or even an algebra) on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">. As always, we are also interested in constructing new <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma">-algebras from old ones, and there are various ways of doing this. The intersection of a collection of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma">-algebras on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is another <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma">-algebra, for example. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is a set and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BA%7D"> is a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma">-algebra on the set, a function <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmu"> whose domain is the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma">-algebra and whose values belong to the extended half-line <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5Clbrack%200%2C%20%2B%20%5Cinfty%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> with <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cinfty"> is included as a point is countably additive if</p>

A measure is a function of this kind such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmu%5Cleft%28%20%5Cvarnothing%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%200"> and a triple <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20X%2C%5Cmathcal%7BA%2C%5C%20%7D%5Cmu%20%5Cright%29"> is called a measure space<a href="#fn248" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref248" role="doc-noteref"><sup>248</sup></a>. <p>This identity also provides a way of evaluating the WP volume integral <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cint_%7B%5Ctau%2F%5Ctext%7BMCG%7D%7D%5E%7B%5C%20%7D%5Ctext%7BdV%7D">. Mirzakhani realised that the length identity and the Fenchel-Nielsen construction of hyperbolic surfaces lead to a recursion for WP volume integrals known as Mirzakhani volume recursion. Fenchel-Nielsen coordinates are coordinates on Teichmüller space. A collection of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3g%20-%203%20%2B%20n"> disjoint simple closed geodesics can be used to cut a surface up into <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2g%20-%202%20%2B%20n"> pairs of pants, such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n"> of the pants boundaries are cusps with zero length and not geodesics of positive length. The Fenchel-Nielsen coordinates for a point of the Teichmüller space of a surface are given by two sets of parameters which can be used to sew the surface together from the pieces of the decomposition. The first parameter is the boundary length <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?l_%7Bj%7D"> and the second is the Fenchel-Nielsen twist <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctau_%7Bj%7D">, which measures the hyperbolic displacement between two boundaries. As you might intuitively guess, a Fenchel-Nielsen twist around a simple geodesic is the action of the cutting the surface open along the geodesic, rotating the boundaries of the two resulting circles relative to each other, and then gluing back together. The lengths of the coordinates are simply the lengths of geodesics which are homotopic to the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3g%20-%203%20%2B%20n"> disjoint simple closed geodesics. It can be shown that the mapping of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BT%7D"> to parameters in this way</p>

<p>The Weil-Petersson volume is a volume of a moduli space and volumes of this kind can be computed by using covers for intermediate moduli spaces and considering a decomposition of a surface into different configurations of subsurfaces: this is what eventually leads to the volume recursion theorem. There are finite symmetry considerations to take into account when cutting open a surface in this way, but we will skip ahead slightly to Mirzakhani’s volume recursion. <p>The first proofs of the theorem by Hadamard and de la Valleé Poisson were analytic, having now been cleaned up and streamlined into a standard ‘proof via complex analysis’. The proof is intricate in its argument, but quite pleasing, as it uses all the basic tools such as contour integrals and the Cauchy residue theorem which you might be familiar with from a course on elementary complex analysis. There is also an elementary proof by Selberg and Erdős, but it is harder to follow than the analytic proof and only elementary in the sense that it does not require complex analysis.
