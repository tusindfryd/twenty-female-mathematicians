<p>McDuff has received many prizes for her contributions to symplectic geometry and topology. She is also a Fellow of the Royal Society. We have already mentioned symplectic geometry a few times, so we should probably explain the topological side of things a bit more. Symplectic topology studies the global aspects of symplectic geometry, whereas the local structures of a symplectic manifold are equivalent to Euclidean structure. This follows from Darboux’s theorem, which states that every symplectic form <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega"> on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> is locally diffeomorphic to the standard form <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega_%7B0%7D"> on Euclidean <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B2n%7D">. To prove this, one first needs a lemma. Take a smooth manifold <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M%5E%7B2n%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Q%20%5Csubset%20M"> a compact submanifold. Suppose that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega_%7B1%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega_%7B2%7D"> are closed <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2">-forms such that at every point <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?q%20%5Cin%20Q"> the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega_%7B1%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Comega_%7B2%7D"> are equal and non-degenerate on the tangent space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T_%7Bq%7DM">. If this is the case, then there exist open neighbourhoods <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U_%7B1%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U_%7B2%7D"> of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Q"> and a diffeomorphism <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpsi%3A%5C%20U_%7B1%7D%20%5Crightarrow%20U_%7B2%7D"> such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpsi%20%3D%20Id%2C%5Ctext%7B%5C%20%5C%20%7D%5Cpsi%5E%7B%2A%7D%5C%20%5Comega_%7B2%7D%20%3D%20%5Comega_%7B1%7D%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>where for the first equation we have restricted to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Q">. To prove this, it is sufficient to show that there exists a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">-form <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma%20%5Cin%20%5COmega%5E%7B1%7D%5Cleft%28%20U_%7B1%7D%20%5Cright%29"> such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csigma%20%3D%200%2C%5C%20%5C%20d%5Csigma%20%3D%20%5Comega_%7B2%7D%20-%20%5Comega_%7B1%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>One proves this by restricting the exponential map to the normal bundle <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BTQ%7D%5E%7B%5Cbot%7D"> of the submanifold <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Q"> with respect to any Riemannian metric <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?g"><a href="#fn147" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref147" role="doc-noteref"><sup>147</sup></a>.</p>
<p>The normal bundle is an example of a vector bundle which is relatively easy to visualise. To be clear, a smooth <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-dimensional vector bundle is a pair of smooth manifolds <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> (known as the total space) and a base space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M">, along with a surjective map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi%3AE%20%5Crightarrow%20M"> called the projection map, such that every fibre <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%20p%20%5Cright%29"> of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> over a point <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p"> is equipped with the structure of a vector space. In addition for every point in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> there is a neighbourhood <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U"> of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p"> and a diffeomorphism</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cvarphi%3A%5C%20%5Cpi%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%20U%20%5Cright%29%20%5Crightarrow%20U%20%5Ctimes%20%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>called a local trivialisation of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> such that one has a commutative diagram when one includes the projection onto the first factor in the product</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi_%7B1%7D%3A%5C%20U%20%5Ctimes%20%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%20%5Crightarrow%20U."><br></p></center>
<p>The restriction of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cvarphi"> to a fibre <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cvarphi%3A%5C%20%5Cpi%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%20p%20%5Cright%29%20%5Crightarrow%20%5Cleft%5C%7B%20p%20%5Cright%5C%7D%20%5Ctimes%20%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%7D"> is a linear isomorphism. You see that we are essentially back in linear algebra again, as this construction has essentially glued together a set of vector spaces into a union, but remember that this bundle is also a manifold.</p>
<p>The prototype example is the tangent bundle <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BTM%7D">, which is the disjoint union of the tangent spaces which one gets at every point. If you take the cotangent spaces instead, you construct a different bundle called the cotangent bundle <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%5E%7B%2A%7DM">. Also, consider a bundle such that the fibre at every point is the orthogonal complement of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T_%7Bp%7DM"> for a submanifold of Euclidean space: this is called the normal bundle to the submanifold<a href="#fn148" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref148" role="doc-noteref"><sup>148</sup></a>. It is a result of Whitney (the embedding theorem) that every smooth <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-manifold has a proper smooth embedding into <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B2n%20%2B%201%7D">. It is a corollary of this theorem that every smooth <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-manifold is diffeomorphic to a closed embedded submanifold of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B2n%20%2B%201%7D">. This follows because we already know that there is a proper smooth embedding, and the image of that has to be an embedded submanifold which is closed in the ambient space because proper continuous maps are closed. As a corollary of the previous statement, every smooth manifold has a distance function whose metric topology is equivalent to the induced topology. This follows because any subspace of a metric space has to inherit a metric which controls the topology of the subspace.</p>
<p>The Whitney embedding theorem answered one of those obvious-sounding questions which no-one seemed able to answer for a long time: are there smooth manifolds which are not diffeomorphic to submanifolds of Euclidean space? A similar result called the Whitney immersion theorem states that every smooth <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-manifold has an immersion into <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B2n%7D">. There are also stronger versions of both these theorems, which state that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n%20%3E%201">, then every smooth <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-manifold has an immersion into <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B2n%20-%201%7D">, and if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n%20%3E%200">, then every smooth <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-manifold has a smooth embedding into <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B2n%7D">. Nash’s embedding theorem is a similar result for Riemannian manifolds<a href="#fn149" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref149" role="doc-noteref"><sup>149</sup></a>. <p>In symplectic topology, we study the global structure of a symplectic manifold and the non-local behaviour of symplectomorphisms which are far from the identity. A number of new constructions have appeared in the development of these global studies. For example, Lalonde and McDuff discovered a method for constructing symplectic embeddings of balls, with the method being use to prove the non-squeezing theorem for arbitrary symplectic manifolds. <p>The Weinstein conjecture relates to a particular type of hypersurface called a contact hypersurface (the hypersurface must also be compact). The specific statement of the conjecture is that the Reeb vector field of such a hypersurface must have at least one periodic orbit. Contact geometry has been described as the version of symplectic geometry for odd dimension. <p>Hypersurfaces of contact type are characterised by the existence of a vector field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> close to the level surface of the Hamiltonian such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BL%7D_%7BX%7D%5Comega_%7B0%7D%20%3D%20%5Comega_%7B0%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>Weinstein proposed his conjecture for all hypersurfaces of contact type and it was subsequently proved by Viterbo that every hypersurface of contact type in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7B2n%20%2B%201%7D"> has a closed characteristic. The general conjecture for contact manifolds which are not embedded contact hypersurfaces in Euclidean space is still open, however. The conjecture was proved for all closed <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?3">-dimensional manifolds by Taubes using a version of Seiberg-Witten Floer homology<a href="#fn156" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref156" role="doc-noteref"><sup>156</sup></a>. Another basic problem in symplectic topology which marks the transition from local to global is that of rigidity. Suppose we take a sequence of symplectomorphisms which converge on a compact set to a diffeomorphism. This limit preserves volume, but it is not clear that the symplectric structure is preserved, and if this structure is not preserved, then we can no longer guarantee that we are even studying symplectic topology. Put another way, we would like to know the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5E%7B0%7D">-closure of the group of symplectic diffeomorphisms. It turns out that the key to characterizing symplectomorphisms is to use the non-squeezing theorem to define a symplectic invariant which is continuous with respect to the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5E%7B0%7D"> topology. Symplectic diffeomorphisms are then simply the diffeomorphisms which preserve this symplectic invariant, hence we see that the four basic strands of the subject influence each other.</p>
<p>The final basic problem in symplectic topology is that of counting the fixed points of symplectic diffeomorphisms. Again, there is a conjecture which encapsulates this problem, known as Arnold’s conjecture. The conjecture relates the minimum number of fixed points of a Hamiltonian symplectomorphism on a closed manifold to Morse theory<a href="#fn157" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref157" role="doc-noteref"><sup>157</sup></a>. We will not go into Morse theory here, but the starting point is to take a compact Riemannian manifold along with a function <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F%20%5Cin%20C%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D%5Cleft%28%20M%20%5Cright%29">. Morse theory begins by studying the behaviour of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F"> near every critical point (assuming that there is a finite number of these points) and studies the possible topological changes which the level sets of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F"> can undergo<a href="#fn158" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref158" role="doc-noteref"><sup>158</sup></a>. The minimum number of critical points for a function defined on a compact manifold is actually a topological invariant of the manifold. This minimum number obviously has to be at least <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?2"> from elementary calculus, because any function will have a distinct local maximum and local minimum. One way of phrasing the conjecture is that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cphi"> is the time-<img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1"> map of a time-dependent Hamiltonian flow on a compact symplectic manifold, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cphi"> has a number of distinct fixed points which is at least the minimum number of critical points on a compact manifold, taken over all smooth functions <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f">. The Arnold conjecture can be viewed as a global version of the local statement that a function defined on a manifold has to have more critical points then there are zeroes for a vector field or convector field defined on that manifold. The exact details of the interplay between local symplectic structure and global properties (and how the former manifests itself in the latter) is at the core of symplectic topology<a href="#fn159" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref159" role="doc-noteref"><sup>159</sup></a>.</p>
