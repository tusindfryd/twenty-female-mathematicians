<p>Tomczak-Jaegermann researches geometric functional analysis, especially the theory of Banach spaces. She has received the CRM-Fields-PIMS prize for her research, previously awarded to Coxeter and Tutte. The basic definition for a Banach space is that it is a complete normed vector space (where by complete, we mean complete in the metric induced by the norm). A norm on a real or complex vector space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is a real valued function on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> whose value at a point in the space is denoted by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C">. The norm has to satisfy the following four properties:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C%20%5Cgeq%200%2C"><br></p></center>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C%20%3D%200%20%5CLeftrightarrow%20x%20%3D%200%2C"><br></p></center>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20%5Ctext%7B%CE%B1x%7D%20%5Cright%5C%7C%20%3D%20%5Cleft%7C%20%5Calpha%20%5Cright%7C%5C%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C%2C"><br></p></center>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%2B%20y%20%5Cright%5C%7C%20%5Cleq%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C%20%2B%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20y%20%5Cright%5C%7C%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y"> are any two vectors in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Calpha"> is any scalar. A norm on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> defines a metric <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?d"> on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> given by</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?d%5Cleft%28%20x%2Cy%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20-%20y%20%5Cright%5C%7C."><br></p></center>
<p>This metric is called the induced metric. It can be checked easily that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?d"> does define a metric space, and so normed spaces and Banach spaces are metric spaces.</p>
<p>An example of a norm on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5Cleft%5Clbrack%200%2C1%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> is the supremum norm:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20f%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B%5Cinfty%7D%20%3D%20%5Csup_%7Bt%20%5Cin%20%5Cleft%5Clbrack%200%2C1%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%7D%5Cleft%7C%20f%5Cleft%28%20t%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%7C."><br></p></center>
<p>Other common examples are the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5E%7B1%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5E%7B2%7D"> norms.</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20f%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7BL%5E%7B1%7D%7D%20%3D%20%5Cint_%7B0%7D%5E%7B1%7D%7B%5Cleft%7C%20f%5Cleft%28%20t%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%7C%5Ctext%7B%5C%20dt%7D%7D%2C"><br></p></center>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20f%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7BL%5E%7B2%7D%7D%20%3D%20%5Cleft%28%20%5Cint_%7B0%7D%5E%7B1%7D%5Cleft%7C%20f%5Cleft%28%20t%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%7C%5E%7B2%7D%5Ctext%7Bdt%7D%20%5Cright%29%5E%7B1%2F2%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>Different norms can be introduced on the same vector space, so we would like an equivalence relation which enables us to compare two norms. We say that two norms <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20%5Cbullet%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B1%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20%5Cbullet%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B2%7D"> on a vector space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> are equivalent if there are positive constants such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?c_%7B1%7D%5C%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B1%7D%20%5Cleq%20%5C%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B2%7D%20%5Cleq%20c_%7B2%7D%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20"><br></p></center>
<p>for all <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%20%5Cin%20V">. We can then write</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20%5Cbullet%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B1%7D%5C%20%5Csim%5C%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20%5Cbullet%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B2%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>Any two norms on a Euclidean space are equivalent. As an example of two norms which are not equivalent, one can consider the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5E%7B1%7D"> and the supremum norm on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5Cleft%5Clbrack%200%2C1%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">.</p>
<p>If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?W"> are two normed spaces, a linear map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L"> from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?W"> such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20L%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5C%7C%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C"><br></p></center>
<p>is called a linear isometry. Two normed spaces are isometrically isomorphic if there exists an invertible linear isometry between them. A norm can be pulled back by a linear invertible map, since if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> is a space equipped with a norm, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?W"> is a vector space, and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L"> is a linear isomorphism, then one can define a norm on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?W"> as</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7BW%7D%20%E2%89%94%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20L%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7BV%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>You can check that this does satisfy the requirements for a norm, including the triangle inequality. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> is a finite dimensional vector space and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n%20%3D%20%5Cdim%20V">, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> is linearly isomorphic to the space of tuples with entries from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D"> or <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D">. However, since a norm can be pulled back by a linear invertible map, this implies that any finite dimensional vector space can be equipped with a norm and any normed space of dimension <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n"> is isometrically isomorphic to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%7D"> with a suitable norm. Since two norms on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%7D"> are equivalent, we also have that all norms on a finite dimensional vector space are equivalent.</p>
<p>We would now like to consider complete normed spaces. Recall that a sequence <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20x_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29_%7Bn%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D"> in a normed space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> is Cauchy if for any positive non-zero <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cvarepsilon"> there exists an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?N"> such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x_%7Bn%7D%20-%20x_%7Bm%7D%20%5Cright%5C%7C%20%3C%20%5Cvarepsilon"><br></p></center>
<p>for all <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?m%2Cn%20%3E%20N">. A sequence of real numbers converges if and only if it as Cauchy sequence. One can also prove that any convergent sequence is Cauchy, and that any Cauchy sequence is bounded. To prove the former for a metric space, realise that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x_%7Bn%7D"> converges to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x"> in the limit, then for every <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cvarepsilon%20%3E%200"> there must be some <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?N"> depending only on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cvarepsilon"> such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?d%5Cleft%28%20x_%7Bn%7D%2Cx%20%5Cright%29%20%3C%20%5Cfrac%7B%5Cvarepsilon%7D%7B2%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n%20%3E%20N">. By the triangle inequality we find that for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?m%2Cn%20%3E%20N"></p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?d%5Cleft%28%20x_%7Bm%7D%2Cx_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cleq%20d%5Cleft%28%20x_%7Bm%7D%2Cx%20%5Cright%29%20%2B%20d%5Cleft%28%20x%2Cx_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%3C%20%5Cfrac%7B%5Cvarepsilon%7D%7B2%7D%20%2B%20%5Cfrac%7B%5Cvarepsilon%7D%7B2%7D%20%3D%20%5Cvarepsilon."><br></p></center>
<p>However, you can see from the definition that this implies that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20x_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29"> is a Cauchy sequence. Since a sequence of real numbers converges only if it is Cauchy, one has that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D"> is complete, and by extension <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D">. Every finite dimensional normed space can be seen to be complete by considering basis vectors.</p>
<p>A good example of a Banach space is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7Bl%7D%5E%7Bp%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%20%5Cright%29"> equipped with the usual <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7Bl%7D%5E%7Bp%7D"> norm. To begin with the proof of completeness, just take a tuple of sequences in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7Bl%7D%5E%7Bp%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%20%5Cright%29">, assume it to be Cauchy, and show that the sum which one obtains implies that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%7C%20x_%7Bj%7D%5E%7B%5Cleft%28%20m%20%5Cright%29%7D%20-%20x_%7Bj%7D%5E%7B%5Cleft%28%20n%20%5Cright%29%7D%20%5Cright%7C%20%3C%20%5Cvarepsilon%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>since all the terms in the sum will be positive. From there you will need to show that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x_%7B%5C%20%7D%5E%7B%5Cleft%28%20m%20%5Cright%29%7D"> converges to a tuple of real constants as <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?m"> tends to infinity, which establishes that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7Bl%7D%5E%7Bp%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%20%5Cright%29"> is complete. The argument also goes through the same if we replace real with complex numbers. As another example, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5Cleft%5Clbrack%200%2C1%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> equipped with the supremum norm is complete. The proof uses a basic fact from analysis that the uniform limit of a sequence of continuous functions is also continuous. It is possible to have a space with is complete when equipped with one norm, but not complete when equipped with another one. For example, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%5Cleft%5Clbrack%200%2C1%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> equipped with the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5E%7B1%7D"> norm is not complete. This can be proved by taking a sequence of continuous functions as follows:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f_%7Bn%7D%5Cleft%28%20t%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5C%7B%20%5Cbegin%7Bmatrix%7D%0A%5Cleft%28%202t%20%5Cright%29%5E%7Bn%7D%2C%5C%20%5C%20%5Ctext%7Bfor%5C%20%7D0%20%5Cleq%20t%20%5Cleq%201%2F2%2C%20%5C%5C%0A1%2C%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5Ctext%7Bfor%5C%20%7D1%2F2%20%5Cleq%20t%20%5Cleq%201.%20%5C%5C%0A%5Cend%7Bmatrix%7D%20%5Cright.%5C%20"><br></p></center>
<p>It can then be shown that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f_%7Bn%7D"> does not converge to a continuous function in the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5E%7B1%7D"> norm via an argument by contradiction whereby one assumes that the limit does exist and calls it <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f">. Another example of an incomplete metric space is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D"> equipped with the usual metric. A normed space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> can be viewed as a dense subset of a larger Banach space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BX%7D"> called a completion of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">. A natural question to ask is whether an incomplete normed vector space can always be completed in this way. (In fact, we have given the game away with this question, as we stated near the beginning of the book that a normed space can always be completed by equipping with an inner product and taking the closure) <a href="#fn137" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref137" role="doc-noteref"><sup>137</sup></a>.</p>
<p>A subset <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X%20%5Csubset%20V"> is dense in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V"> if for any <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?v%20%5Cin%20V"> and any <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cvarepsilon%20%3E%200"> there is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%20%5Cin%20X"> such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20-%20v%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7BV%7D%20%3C%20%5Cvarepsilon."><br></p></center>
<p>An obvious example is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D">, which is dense as a subset of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D">. The real numbers can be obtained from this subset by completing <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D"> with Dedekind cuts<a href="#fn138" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref138" role="doc-noteref"><sup>138</sup></a>. Similarly, the space of tuples with rational entries <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BQ%7D%5E%7Bn%7D"> is a dense subset of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BR%7D%5E%7Bn%7D">. If we take two normed spaces and a linear map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5C%20%3AV%20%5Crightarrow%20W">, this map is called a linear isometry if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L"> is linear, bijective, and</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20L%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7BW%7D%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7BV%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>for all <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%20%5Cin%20V">. We say that the two spaces are linearly isometric. Any normed vector space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is linearly isometric to a dense subset of a Banach space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BX%7D"> equipped with a norm <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20%5Cbullet%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B%5Cmathcal%7BX%7D%7D">. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is already Banach, then the linear isometry is just the identity. The proof of the completion theorem is quite long, but essentially you are just looking to construct <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BX%7D"> using a suitable equivalence relation between Cauchy sequences, show that is a normed vector space, and then show that it is complete and that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is always isometric to a subset of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BX%7D"> such that the subset is dense.</p>
<p>Obviously, at this point, we do not know if the completion is unique, but it can be shown with less work than the proof of the previous theorem that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BX%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cwidetilde%7B%5Cmathcal%7BX%7D%7D"> are Banach spaces where the isometries <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?i%5C%20%3AX%20%5Crightarrow%20%5Cmathcal%7BX%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cwidetilde%7Bi%7D%5C%20%3AX%20%5Crightarrow%20%5Cwidetilde%7B%5Cmathcal%7BX%7D%7D"> are such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7BX%7D%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20i%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B%5Cmathcal%7BX%7D%7D%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20%5Cwidetilde%7Bi%7D%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B%5Cwidetilde%7B%5Cmathcal%7BX%7D%7D%7D%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>then if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?i%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29"> is a dense subset of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BX%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cwidetilde%7Bi%7D%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29"> is a dense subset of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cwidetilde%7B%5Cmathcal%7BX%7D%7D">, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7BX%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cwidetilde%7B%5Cmathcal%7BX%7D%7D"> are linearly isometric ie. all completions of a space are the same modulo linear isometries. There are also more advanced theorems relating to linear maps between Banach spaces. We will start by recalling the Baire category theorem. This starts that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7B%20G_%7Bi%7D%20%5Cright%5C%7D_%7Bi%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D"> is a countable family of dense open subsets of a complete metric space, then the intersection</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G%20%3D%20%5Cbigcap_%7Bi%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7DG_%7Bi%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>is dense in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">. In words, every countable collection of dense open subsets has a dense intersection. It is also possible to formulate this theorem with reference to a locally compact Hausdorff space, and this is one of the main properties that such spaces share with complete metric spaces. A topological space for which every countable union of dense open subsets is dense is said to be a Baire space. The Baire property is topological so any space which is homeomorphic to a complete metric space must be a Baire space. As a corollary, every meagre subset of a Baire space has a dense complement, where a subset of a topological space is said to be meagre if it can be expressed in the form of a union of a countable number of nowhere dense subsets.</p>
<p>The first big theorem we would like to state for linear maps between Banach spaces is the principle of uniform boundedness. Start with a Banach space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> and a normed space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y">. Take <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S"> to be a subset of bounded linear maps from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y"> such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csup_%7BT%20%5Cin%20s%7D%7B%5Cleft%5C%7C%20T%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7BY%7D%20%3C%20%5Cinfty%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>for every <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%20%5Cin%20X">. If these assumptions hold, then</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csup_%7BT%20%5Cin%20s%7D%7B%5Cleft%5C%7C%20T%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B%5C%20%7D%20%3C%20%5Cinfty%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>This a very useful theorem in applications. As a corollary, take <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T_%7Bn%7D"> in the space of bounded linear maps and suppose that the norm <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20T_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%5C%7C"> is unbounded. There must exist <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%20%5Cin%20X"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20T_%7Bn%7Dx%20%5Cright%5C%7C"> is unbounded. This can be proved by a contradiction: assuming that this <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x"> does not exist implies that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20T_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%5C%7C"> is bounded. As another corollary, suppose that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is a Banach space, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y"> a normed space and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T_%7Bn%7D"> a bounded linear map as before. Suppose that the following limit exists for every <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%20%5Cin%20X">:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%E2%89%94%20%5Clim_%7Bn%20%5Crightarrow%20%5Cinfty%7D%7BT_%7Bn%7D%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>If this is the case, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%20%5Cin%20B%28X%2CY%29">. This can be proved by checking that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"> is both linear and bounded. The latter follows from the principle of uniform boundedness.</p>
<p>Our next big theorem is the open mapping theorem. Be warned that the statement and proof of this result in some textbooks of functional analysis can be difficult to understand. We can state the theorem as follows: if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%3AX%20%5Crightarrow%20Y"> a bounded surjective linear map from a Banach space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> to another Banach space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y">, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"> maps open sets in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> to open sets in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y">. The proof uses the Baire category theorem. As a corollary, one obtains the inverse mapping theorem. This says that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%3AX%20%5Crightarrow%20Y"> is a bounded bijective linear map from a Banach space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> onto a Banach space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y">, then the inverse <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%5E%7B-%201%7D"> is bounded. We already know that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"> has an inverse. This inverse is linear because</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%5Cleft%28%20%5Calpha%20T%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%20y_%7B1%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%2B%20%5Cbeta%20T%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%20y_%7B2%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Calpha%20y_%7B1%7D%20%2B%20%5Cbeta%20y_%7B2%7D%20%3D%20T%5Cleft%28%20T%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Calpha%20y_%7B1%7D%20%2B%20%5Cbeta%20y_%7B2%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%29"><br></p></center>
<p>which implies that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5Calpha%20y_%7B1%7D%20%2B%20%5Cbeta%20y_%7B2%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Calpha%20T%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%20y_%7B1%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%2B%20%5Cbeta%20T%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%20y_%7B2%7D%20%5Cright%29."><br></p></center>
<p>The inverse is also unique, and the result then follows from the open mapping theorem. From standard spectral theory, we know that in infinite dimension, the spectrum of a bounded linear self-map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"> consists of the set of all <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Clambda"> such that the operator <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%20-%20%5Clambda%20I"> does not have a bounded inverse. The inverse mapping theorem then tells us that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"> is a bounded linear self-map and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%20-%20%5Clambda%20I"> is a bijection, then the inverse <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20T%20-%20%5Clambda%20I%5C%20%20%5Cright%29%5E%7B-%201%7D"> is bounded. This implies <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Clambda"> does not belong to the spectrum of the operator. A corollary of the inverse mapping theorem states that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is a Banach space which is complete with respect to different norms <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20%5Cbullet%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B1%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20%5Cbullet%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B2%7D"> such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B2%7D%20%5Cleq%20C%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7B1%7D%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>then the two norms are equivalent.</p>
<p>Our final theorem is the closed graph theorem. This states that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%3AX%20%5Crightarrow%20Y"> is a linear map between Banach spaces and if the graph of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"></p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G%5C%20%3A%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5C%7B%20%5Cleft%28%20x%2CT%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cin%20X%20%5Ctimes%20Y%5C%20%3Ax%20%5Cin%20X%20%5Cright%5C%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>is a closed subset of the product <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X%20%5Ctimes%20Y"> with an appropriately defined norm, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"> is a bounded operator. The graph <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?G"> is a closed linear subspace of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X%20%5Ctimes%20Y">, so it must be a Banach space which equipped with the product norm which we just mentioned. Consider the projector map which takes the graph to one of the halves of the product, defined as</p>
<p><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cpi_%7BX%7D%5Cleft%28%20x%2Cy%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20x."></p>
<p>This map is a linear, bounded bijection. By the inverse mapping theorem, we know that the inverse is also bounded, so</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20%5Cpi_%7BX%7D%5E%7B-%201%7D%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7BX%20%5Ctimes%20Y%7D%20%3D%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7BX%7D%20%2B%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20T%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7BY%7D%20%5Cleq%20M%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C."><br></p></center>
<p>This implies that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20T%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7BY%7D%20%5Cleq%20M%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>which completes the proof. The theorem can be used to prove an important result known as the Hellinger-Toeplitz theorem, which states that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?H"> is a Hilbert space and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T%5C%20%3AH%20%5Crightarrow%20H"> is a linear operator which satisfies</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20T%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%2Cy%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cleft%28%20x%2CT%5Cleft%28%20y%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%29%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?T"> is bounded. In words, a symmetric operator which is defined everywhere on a Hilbert space is bounded. This has important consequences, as it says that an unbounded linear operator which satisfies the above cannot be defined everywhere on the Hilbert space. However, unbounded operators arise naturally in quantum mechanics (the operator corresponding to the energy observable, for example), but we will not go any further into the unbounded theory here.</p>
<p>There is also a body of more advanced theory relating to Banach spaces. The starting point for this theory is to start thinking about bases in Banach spaces. In common with the usual linear algebra theory, suppose that a Banach space has a countable basis <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7B%20e_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%5C%7D"> such that any element of the space can be written uniquely as a decomposition:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%20%3D%20%5Csum_%7Bj%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D%7Bx_%7Bj%7De_%7Bj%7D%7D%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>where the sum converges in the Banach space. If we consider the following finite expansions up to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n"> terms</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?P_%7Bn%7D%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Csum_%7Bj%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%7Bx_%7Bj%7De_%7Bj%7D%7D%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>a question can we ask is whether there exists a constant such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20P_%7Bn%7D%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5C%7C%20%5Cleq%20C%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>for every <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">?</p>
<p>To prove this we need to show that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?P_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cin%20B%5Cleft%28%20X%2CX%20%5Cright%29"> for every <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">. For this, we need some fairly involved arguments which show that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20e_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29"> is a countable sequence in a Banach space such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20e_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%5C%7C%20%3D%201"> and such that the closed linear span is the whole of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">, then</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5C%20%5Cleft%7C%20%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C%5C%20%20%5Cright%7C%20%3D%20%5Csup_%7Bn%7D%5Cleft%5C%7C%20%5Csum_%7Bj%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%7Ba_%7Bj%7De_%7Bj%7D%7D%20%5Cright%5C%7C_%7BX%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>defines a norm on the Banach space such that the space is complete with respect to this norm. A corollary is that if a sequence <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20e_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29"> satisfies the previous conditions, then there is a constant <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C"> such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20%5Csum_%7Bj%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%7Bx_%7Bj%7De_%7Bj%7D%7D%20%5Cright%5C%7C%20%5Cleq%20C%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20%5Cright%5C%7C%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n%5Cmathbb%7B%5Cin%20N%7D"><a href="#fn139" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref139" role="doc-noteref"><sup>139</sup></a>.</p>
<p>It is of course not clear that infinite dimensional Banach spaces can have a basis, since the concept of a basis originates in finite dimensional linear algebra. If a normed space contains a sequence of vectors <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20e_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29"> with the property that there always exists a unique sequence of scalars <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20%5Calpha_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29"> such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7C%20x%20-%20%5Cleft%28%20%5Calpha_%7B1%7De_%7B1%7D%20%2B%20%5Cldots%20%2B%20%5Calpha_%7Bn%7De_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%5Cright%5C%7C%20%5Clongrightarrow%200%5C%20%5Ctext%7Bas%5C%20%7Dn%20%5Clongrightarrow%20%5Cinfty%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20e_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29"> is called a Schauder basis for the Banach space. We then have as before the expansion of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x"> with respect to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20e_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29">:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%20%3D%20%5Csum_%7Bk%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D%7B%5Calpha_%7Bk%7De_%7Bk%7D%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>In particular, this means that the following sequence</p>

<p>must converge to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x"> in the norm topology of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">. <p>and so on. It can be shown that a normed space with a Schauder basis is separable. Banach asked whether the converse is true: that is, whether every separable Banach space has to have a Schauder basis. Enflo proved that the answer is negative by constructing a counterexample<a href="#fn140" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref140" role="doc-noteref"><sup>140</sup></a>.</p>
<p>The concept of a basis in an infinite dimensional Banach space is distinct from the concept of a Hamel basis, which is a vector space basis, being defined merely as a linearly independent subset of the space which spans <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?V">. More concretely, a Hamel basis for a vector space is a collection of linearly independent vectors such that every element of the space can be represented uniquely as a finite linear combination of the members of the basis. One can prove with the axiom of choice or Zorn’s lemma that every vector space has a Hamel basis. Also, if a vector space has a finite Hamel basis, then every Hamel basis for the space has the same number of elements. This is essentially just a linear algebra argument. The Baire category theorem can be used to deduce that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20e_%7Bi%7D%20%5Cright%29"> is a Hamel basis for an infinite dimensional Banach space, then it must be uncountable. The closed graph theorem can be used to show that a sequence in a separable Banach space is only a Schauder basis if it is equivalent to the type of basis we are already familiar with. The proof is elegant, but the result is not as useful as it sounds, as it is rare that you would know that something was a basis without already knowing that it was a Schauder basis.</p>

<p>is the basis constant, where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S_%7Bn%7D"> form a sequence of projections associated with the elements of the Schauder basis. One also has the corresponding notion of a basic sequence. A sequence <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20e_%7Bk%7D%20%5Cright%29_%7Bk%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D"> in a Banach space is called a basic sequence if it is a basis for the closed linear span of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20e_%7Bk%7D%20%5Cright%29_%7Bk%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7B%5Cinfty%7D">. There is also some very interesting theory on the nonlinear geometry of Banach spaces. Since a Banach space is also a metric space by definition, the question of nonlinear geometry is to what extent does the metric structure of a Banach space determine its linear structure. All the theory which we have discussed so far has been regarding linear operators between Banach spaces, but there are many natural maps between these spaces which are non-linear (though they might still have good regularity properties). Investigating these maps is a relatively new field of research in functional analysis.</p>
<p>If we have very little information about the metric structure of a Banach space, we might ask how much we can know about the space if we only know the homeomorphic class of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> as a topological space. In effect, we would like to know when two separable Banach spaces are homeomorphic as topological spaces. This problem was resolved a few decades after it was posed by Fréchet: all separable infinite dimensional Banach spaces are homeomorphic. In the opposite case we assume that the map between the Banach spaces is an isometry, meaning that the map essentially carries all the information we might wish to know about the metric structure of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">. In this situation it was shown that one can obtain complete information about the real linear structure. Specifically, if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y"> are real normed spaces and if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CPhi"> is an isometry which maps <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?Y"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0">, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5CPhi"> is a bounded linear operator. If two real Banach spaces are isometric as Banach spaces, then they are also linearly isometric.</p>
<p>In general, we will find ourselves between these two extremes, so the goal is to find out how much of the information about the structures on the first Banach space is transferred to the second space under some map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f">. For example, if it can be proved that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> is a uniform homeomorphism between two normed spaces, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> is a coarse surjective Lipschitz embedding. A basic question in the area is whether the fact that there is a Lipschitz map between Banach spaces with some desirable property such as being a linear isometry establishes the existence of a corresponding linear map which also has that same property<a href="#fn141" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref141" role="doc-noteref"><sup>141</sup></a>. There is no room to go any further into the theory of Banach spaces, but hopefully this has been good motivation. We might also mention that the work of Tomczak-Jaegermann was used in an essential way by Gowers in his solution to Banach’s homogeneous space problem. A Banach space is said to be homogeneous if it is isomorphic to all of its infinite dimensional Banach spaces. The homogeneous space problem asks if every homogeneous Banach space isomorphic to a separable Hilbert space. The specific result of Tomczak-Jaegermann was the fact that a Banach space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> with cotype <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?q"> for some finitie <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?q"> either has a subspace without an unconditional basis or it has a subspace which is isomorphic to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7Bl%7D_%7B2%7D">. In addition, if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is a homogeneous Banach space, then either it is isomorphic to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathcal%7Bl%7D_%7B2%7D"> or it fails to have an unconditional basis<a href="#fn142" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref142" role="doc-noteref"><sup>142</sup></a>.</p>
<p>Tomczak-Jaegermann is also interested in probability theory, especially the theory of random matrices and random graphs. Random matrices are often more useful than regular matrices because in the limit of large matrix dimension, the statistical correlations for the spectra of an ensemble of random matrices do not depend on the probability distribution which defines the ensemble, and only depend on the invariant properties of the distribution. This is known as the universal property of random matrices. Techniques from random matrix theory also enable analytic computations which is often impossible to achieve otherwise. This concept of universality means that a matrix does not need to have independent Gaussian random variables as matrix entries. One could theoretically create a random matrix by filling each slot with <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1"> or <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?-%201"> with a <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?50%2F50"> probability for each. Depending on the overall symmetry of the matrix, one can end up with the common eigenvalue statistics of the Gaussian random matrix ensembles even though we have different rules for creating the matrix elements.</p>
<p>However, we need to be slightly careful with statements such as these, as it is only the local eigenvalue statistics (those that operate over a few mean spacings, perhaps) which are universal in this way, and there are other global properties which can depend on the distribution from which the elements are drawn. If you compute the density of the eigenphases on the unit circle, the density will only be uniform for the Gaussian elements, and increasing the size of the matrix will not change this, whereas local properties (nearest neighbour statistics) will become universal in the limit of a large matrix. Filling a matrix with random elements will not give universality for these global properties unless the random number generator samples from a Gaussian distribution. As we are interested in eigenvalues, we consider random unitary matrices. The concept of a random unitary matrix is usually referring to the Haar measure on the group of unitary matrices. One takes the Lie group of matrices <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U%28n%29"> and then makes it into a probability space by assigning the Haar measure, which is the unique measure invariant under multiplication in the Lie group. There are numerical methods for generating random unitary matrices which sample from either of the classical Lie groups <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?U%28n%29"> or <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?O%5Cleft%28%20n%20%5Cright%29">, where the sampling is uniform since it is done with respect to the uniform Haar measure<a href="#fn143" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref143" role="doc-noteref"><sup>143</sup></a>.</p>
