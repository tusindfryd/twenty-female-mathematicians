<p>Noether was one of the most important and influential mathematicians of the nineteenth century, especially known for her contributions to algebra and mathematical physics. Despite her brilliance, she faced many obstacles in her academic life due to the fact that she was a woman, and had to work without pay for several years. She did much to shape the face of abstract algebra when it was relatively under-developed, including the basic theories of rings and fields which are now so well-known to undergraduate mathematics students. If you consider the state which abstract algebra was in at the time, the difficulty of this task cannot be overestimated. Workers in other fields such as fluid dynamics, mathematical physics, or differential geometry, usually have hints to work with, or analogies and simple pictures to fall back on. Even with something like the fundamental group in algebraic topology, there is a simple geometric idea at the bottom of the abstract concept: that of counting the number of holes encircled by a closed loop. These hints tend to vanish when one studies pure abstract algebra, especially for the beginner.</p>
<p>Noether laid the foundations of much of the theory of commutative rings, known as commutative algebra. This is still a basic and important branch of algebra, and one has to know a fair amount of commutative algebra before even beginning to consider studying problems in algebraic geometry. A ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> in general is a set equipped with addition and multiplication such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> forms an abelian group with respect to addition (<img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> contains a zero element and every element of the ring has an additive inverse which can be added to it to obtain the zero element). Furthermore, multiplication is associative</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20%5Ctext%7Bxy%7D%20%5Cright%29z%20%3D%20x%5Cleft%28%20%5Ctext%7Byz%7D%20%5Cright%29%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>and distributive over addition</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%5Cleft%28%20y%20%2B%20z%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20xy%20%2B%20xz%2C%5C%20%5C%20%5Cleft%28%20y%20%2B%20z%20%5Cright%29x%20%3D%20yx%20%2B%20zx%2C"><br></p></center>
<p>for any <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%2C%5C%20y%2C%5C%20z%20%5Cin%20R">. One can also require that the ring has an identity element denoted by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1"> and that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?xy%20%3D%20yx">. The latter requirement means that the ring is commutative, and the former is also often assumed for simplicity. A ring homomorphism is a map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> taking a ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> into another ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%5Cleft%28%20x%20%2B%20y%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20f%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%2B%20f%5Cleft%28%20y%20%5Cright%29%2C"><br></p></center>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%5Cleft%28%20%5Ctext%7Bxy%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20f%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29f%5Cleft%28%20y%20%5Cright%29%2C"><br></p></center>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%5Cleft%28%201%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%201."><br></p></center>
<p>The first criterion means <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f"> is a group homomorphism of abelian groups. As you might expect, you can form a subset of a ring which is then a subring if the product of sum of two elements is also an element of the set and if the set contains the identity element for the original ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R">. A composition of ring homomorphisms is also a ring homomorphism.</p>
<p>Another basic definition which one needs is that of an ideal: these are simple objects, but they were employed to great effect by Noether. An ideal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Br%7D"> of a ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> is a subset of the ring such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Br%7D"> is an additive subgroup (ie. a linear sum of elements of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Br%7D"> with coefficients taken from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> is still in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Br%7D">). One might write <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20x%2Cy%20%5Cright%29"> for the ideal generated in this way by two elements of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R">. An ideal is also defined such that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R%5Cmathfrak%7Br%20%5Csubseteq%20r.%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Br%20%5Csubset%7DR"> is an ideal, then the quotient group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R%2F%5Cmathfrak%7Br%7D"> inherits multiplication from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> such it becomes a ring in itself, known as the quotient ring. There exists a surjective ring homomorphism <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cphi"> from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R%2F%5Cmathfrak%7Br%7D"> which takes an element of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> to its coset and the quotient ring along with the quotient homomorphism are defined uniquely modulo isomorphisms. It is an important and useful fact that the map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cphi%5E%7B-%201%7D"> which takes ideals of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R%2F%5Cmathfrak%7Br%7D"> to ideals of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> which contain <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Br%7D"> is a one-to-one order-preserving correspondence. As an example of a fact which uses rings and ideals, take a ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> which is not the zero ring. This is the ring whose only element is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0">, which can only occur if we allow the identity element to equal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0">, since this means that</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%20%3D%20x1%20%3D%20x0%20%3D%200."><br></p></center>
<p>Excluding this possibility, the following three statements are equivalent: <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> is a field (that is, every non-zero element of the ring is a unit), the only ideals which the ring contains are the ideals generated by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">, and every homomorphism of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> into a non-zero ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> is an injection. Bear in mind that whereas the ideal generated by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1"> is denoted by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%201%20%5Cright%29">, the ideal generated by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0"> is usually denoted just by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0"> (as is the zero ring). Perhaps the hardest part to prove in this chain is that the third statement implies the first statement. By definition, every ideal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Br%7D"> is the kernel of the quotient homomorphism from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R%2F%5Cmathfrak%7Br%7D">. We have assumed that the homomorphism is injective, but that implies that the only proper ideal is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%200%20%5Cright%29">, where a proper ideal is an ideal which is smaller than the ring. For any proper ideal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29">, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%20%3D%200">. This implies that every non-zero <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x"> is invertible, which means that the ring is a field. Alternatively, we could prove much the same thing by a contradiction argument. Assume that the third statement is true, but that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> is not a field. This means that there has to be an element of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> which is not a unit. If it is not a unit, the ideal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29"> does not contain <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1">, so <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29"> is a proper ideal of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R">. If we take the quotient homomorphism <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cphi">, this homomorphism is not an injection by definition because <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cphi%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%200"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x"> is not <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0">. <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29"> is not a proper ideal, so the quotient ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R%2F%28x%29"> is not the zero ring (since the only way to get the zero ring from a quotient is via the quotient <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R%2FR"> and we just said that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20x%20%5Cright%29"> is smaller than <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R">). This is a contradiction, which proves the claim<a href="#fn20" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref20" role="doc-noteref"><sup>20</sup></a>.</p>
<p>We are not quite finished with the preliminary definitions, as we still need to define modules. The definition of modules might seem a bit strange at first, but they are essential to modern commutative algebra and they help to unify many concepts which you may have seen before in linear algebra without really knowing what was going on or why the computations were producing the correct answers. Start with a commutative ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R">. An <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R">-module is an abelian group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> on which <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> acts via linear transformations. More formally, it is an abelian group <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> along with a map from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M%20%5Ctimes%20R"> to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> such that for elements <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%2Cy"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?a%2Cb"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R">, the following axioms hold:</p>

<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%5Cleft%28%20a%20%2B%20b%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20xa%20%2B%20xb%2C"><br></p></center>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%5Cleft%28%20%5Ctext%7Bab%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cleft%28%20%5Ctext%7Bxa%7D%20%5Cright%29b%2C"><br></p></center>

<p>You might see these axioms written such that the operators occur in the opposite order: this results in a left <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R">-module, whereas we have stated the axioms for a right <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R">-module. The classic example of a module which you might have seen is a vector space over a field: this vector space is an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R">-module, where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> is a field. As another example, if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R%5Cmathbb%7B%3D%20Z%7D">, then the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R">-module is an abelian group.</p>
<p>In order to obtain more meaningful results about modules and rings, one has to make use of finiteness conditions for partially ordered sets called chain conditions<a href="#fn21" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref21" role="doc-noteref"><sup>21</sup></a>. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S"> is a set equipped with a partial order for the relation <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleq"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%20%5Cleq%20y"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y%20%5Cleq%20x"> implies that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y%20%3D%20x">, then the following conditions are equivalent: firstly, for an increasing sequence in the set,</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x_%7B1%7D%20%5Cleq%20x_%7B2%7D%20%5Cleq%20%5Cldots%5C%20"><br></p></center>
<p>there exists <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n_%7B0%7D"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x_%7Bm%7D%20%3D%20x_%7Bn%7D"> for all <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n%2C%5C%20m%20%5Cgeq%20n_%7Bo%7D">. Secondly, every non-empty subset of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S"> contains a maximal element. The first condition is known as the ascending chain condition, and the second is the maximum condition. The particular application we have in mind is when <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S"> is the set of submodules of a module and the partial ordering relation is given by inclusion of closed subsets. A module which satisfies either of the above conditions is Noetherian. If we order the set via the opposite inclusion, the conditions become the descending chain condition and the minimum condition and a module which satisfies either is Artinian. If we take a set in which the maximum condition holds, the principle of Noetherian induction says that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is a subset of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S"> which contains an element <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?s"> of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?S"> whenever it contains all elements <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%20%5Cin%20S"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%20%3E%20s">, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X%20%3D%20S">. Technically, this is based on the fact that the set of all submodules forms a modular lattice under partial ordering by inclusion, and it is this lattice which then satisfies one of the conditions<a href="#fn22" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref22" role="doc-noteref"><sup>22</sup></a>. Noetherian modules are more useful than Artinian ones, since the Noetherian condition is the one which is required for many theorems and propositions. For example, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> being a Noetherian <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R">-module implies that every submodule of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> is finitely generated.</p>
<p>In many cases, however, the basic properties of Noetherian and Artinian modules are the same. If we take a short exact sequence of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R">-modules,</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0%20%5Crightarrow%20M%27%20%5Crightarrow%20M%20%5Crightarrow%20M%27%27%20%5Crightarrow%200"><br></p></center>
<p><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M"> being Noetherian or Artinian implies that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M%27"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M%27%27"> are also of this type. If a set of modules <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M_%7Bi%7D"> are all Noetherian or Artinian, then so is the direct sum. This can be proved using a similar short exact sequence.</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0%20%5Crightarrow%20M_%7Bn%7D%20%5Crightarrow%20%7B%5Cprod_%7Bi%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7Bn%7DM_%7Bi%7D%7D_%7B%5C%20%7D%20%5Crightarrow%20%5Cprod_%7Bi%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7Bn%20-%201%7DM_%7Bi%7D%20%5Crightarrow%200."><br></p></center>
<p>We can take a ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> and regard it as a right <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R">-module in itself, and by the same terminology the ring is said to be a Noetherian or an Artinian ring, where the maximum and minimum conditions are defined with respect to right ideals. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> is a right Noetherian ring, then a finitely generated <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R">-module is also Noetherian (or Artinian, respectively). Most rings which one uses when applying commutative algebra are Noetherian: in particular, the rings which are used in algebraic geometry when one wishes to study the links between rings and geometric objects are Noetherian. It might be worth mentioning that the ascending chain condition also has applications in topology: a topological space <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is Noetherian if the open subsets of the space satisfy the ascending chain or maximum condition. Since closed subsets are complements of open subsets (and vice versa), it is an equivalent requirement that the closed subsets of the space satisfy the descending chain or minimum condition. One can show that every subspace of a Noetherian topological space is also Noetherian, and that the space is compact.</p>
<p>There are three equivalent conditions for a ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> to be Noetherian: we already established their equivalence in the setting of modules. Firstly, every non-empty set of ideals in the ring contains a maximal element. Secondly, every ascending chain of ideals in the ring is stationary: that is, the chain of ideals</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%7D_%7B1%7D%20%5Csubset%20%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%7D_%7B2%7D%20%5Csubset%20%5Cldots%5C%20%20%5Csubset%20%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%7D_%7Bk%7D%20%5Csubset%20%5Cldots%5C%20"><br></p></center>
<p>stops at some point, so we end up with <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%7D_%7Bk%7D%20%3D%20%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%7D_%7Bk%20%2B%201%7D"> for some <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">. Thirdly, every ideal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%20%5Cin%7DA"> is finitely generated. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> is Noetherian and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cphi"> is a ring homomorphism from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> onto another ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B">, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> is also Noetherian. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> is a subring of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> is Noetherian and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> is finitely generated as an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A">-module, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> is a Noetherian ring. A trivial example of a ring which is not Noetherian is the polynomial ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20X_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20X_%7Bn%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%5C%20%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> formed from the set of polynomials in an infinite number of indeterminates, where the coefficients are drawn from the field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> (more generally, another ring).</p>
<p>Many examples of rings which <em>are</em> Noetherian can be found using the Hilbert basis theorem. This states that the polynomial ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> is Noetherian if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> is Noetherian. One can prove this by defining auxiliary sets which form an ascending chain of ideals and showing that any ideal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%20%5Csubset%7DA%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> is finitely generated. (The word ‘basis’ was used historically to refer to a generating set). As a direct corollary by induction on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">, if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> is Noetherian, so is <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">. Also, if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> is a Noetherian ring and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> is a finitely generated <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B">-algebra, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B"> is Noetherian. Every finitely generated ring, and every finitely generated algebra over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BZ%7D"> or a field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">, is Noetherian. One can follow similar lines to the proof of the Hilbert basis theorem to show that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> being a Noetherian ring implies that the formal power series ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A%5Cleft.%20%E2%9F%A6x%20%5Cright.%E2%9F%A7"> is Noetherian<a href="#fn23" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref23" role="doc-noteref"><sup>23</sup></a>. We can also state that if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> is a field and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> is a finitely generated <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">-algebra such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> is also a field, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?E"> is a finite algebraic extension of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">. A field extension is a monomorphism <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?i%3Ak%20%5Crightarrow%20l%2C"> where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?l"> are both subfields of a larger field (often taken to be <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BC%7D">). We might say that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> is the small field and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?l"> is the large field. There are many interesting results for field extensions and one studies this type of extension in some detail in Galois theory: specifically, the group of field automorphisms of the extension. The famous Tower Law, for example, is a result about degrees of extensions where there is a chain of subfields (the degree of a field extension being the dimension of the large field considered as a vector space over the small field)<a href="#fn24" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref24" role="doc-noteref"><sup>24</sup></a>.</p>
<p>A finite extension is one with finite degree and an extension (denoted as <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?l%5C%20%3Ak">) is algebraic if every element of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?l"> is algebraic over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">. Every finite extension is algebraic, but the converse is not always true. An element is algebraic over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> if it satisfies a dependence relation for an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">-th degree polynomial in the polynomial ring over the field:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%5Cleft%28%20y%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20a_%7Bn%7Dy%5E%7Bn%7D%20%2B%20%5Cldots%20%2B%20a_%7B1%7Dy%20%2B%20a_%7B0%7D%20%3D%200."><br></p></center>
<p>One can divide through by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?a_%7Bn%7D"> when working in a field, so we can assume that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?a_%7Bn%7D%20%3D%201">: that is, the polynomial is monic. However, a polynomial ring does not have to be over a field. In the case when it is over a ring, the relation</p>
<p><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%5Cleft%28%20y%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20a_%7Bn%7Dy%5E%7Bn%7D%20%2B%20%5Cldots%20%2B%20a_%7B1%7Dy%20%2B%20a_%7B0%7D%20%3D%200%2C"></p>
<p>where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?a_%7Bn%7D%20%3D%201"> is known as an integral dependence relation for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y">. There is a useful relation between integral dependence and finiteness conditions in a ring extension: as an example, if one knows that two elements of a ring extension <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B%20%5Csubset%20A"> are integral over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A">, one can use finiteness conditions to show that the sum and the product of the two elements are integral over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> without needing to compute their integral dependence relations. One can start with an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A">-algebra <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cvarphi%3AA%20%5Crightarrow%20B"> and an element <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y%20%5Cin%20B"> and show that the following statements are all equivalent: <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y"> is integral over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A">, the polynomial subring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A%5E%7B%27%7D%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20y%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%20%5Csubset%20B"> generated by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A%5E%7B%27%7D%20%3D"> <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cvarphi%5Cleft%28%20A%20%5Cright%29"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y"> is finite over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A">, and there exists an <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A">-subalgebra <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C%20%5Csubset%20B"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A%5E%7B%27%7D%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20y%20%5Cright%5Crbrack%20%5Csubset%20C"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?C"> is finite over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A">. One can prove the final equivalence using the determinant trick<a href="#fn25" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref25" role="doc-noteref"><sup>25</sup></a>.</p>
<p>These finiteness conditions lead us to an important lemma, the Noether normalization lemma (really a theorem). For this lemma one takes a field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> and a finitely generated <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">-algebra <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A">. In this case, there exist elements <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bn%7D"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bn%7D"> are algebraically independent over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> is independent over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?B%20%3D%20k%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">. A <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">-algebra is said to be finitely generated over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A%20%3D%20k%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> for some finite set <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x_%7B1%7D">, … , <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x_%7Bn%7D">. In more detail for the case where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> is an integral domain, the result says that for an infinite field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K%20%3D%20k%5Cleft%28%20x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29"> the field of fractions of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">, there exists a nonnegative integer <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?d"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?d"> linear combinations <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20y_%7Bd%7D"> of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bn%7D"> with coefficients of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20y_%7Bd%7D"> are algebraically independent over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> and such that every element of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> is integral over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20y_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20y_%7Bd%7D%5C%20%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> is separably generated over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">, then the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y_%7Bi%7D"> can be chosen such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> is a separable extension of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k%5Cleft%28%20y_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20y_%7Bd%7D%20%5Cright%29">. The field of fractions of the ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> is a field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> containing a subring isomorphic to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> such that every element of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> can be expressed as <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?r%2Fs">, where <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?s"> is non-zero and both <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?r"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?s"> belong to the subring. Noether introduced this lemma in the different context of a paper on the theory of invariants of finite groups<a href="#fn26" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref26" role="doc-noteref"><sup>26</sup></a>.</p>
<p>One important use of the lemma is to establish a result known as Hilbert’s Nullstellensatz (or the weak form, at least). This states if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> is a field and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> is a finitely generated <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">-algebra, then <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> is algebraic over <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k"> is a finite field extension. Another way of stating this is that if one takes an algebraically closed field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">, each maximal ideal in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R%20%3D%20k%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> has the form</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bm%20%3D%7D%5Cleft%28%20x_%7B1%7D%20-%20%5Calpha_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bn%7D%20-%20%5Calpha_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29."><br></p></center>
<p>In any case, the proof starts off by using the Noether normalisation lemma, since one uses the fact that one immediately has a set of elements in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> which are algebraically independent, plus the fact that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?K"> is finite over the polynomial ring in the indeterminates formed by those elements where the coefficients are drawn from <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">. The full form of the Nullstellensatz is somewhat drawn out, but it can be stated in a few lines using the concept of the radical of an ideal. If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I"> is an ideal contained in commutative ring with an identity, the radical of the ideal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Csqrt%7BI%7D"> is the set of elements in the ring such that the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">-th power is in the ideal for some <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k%20%5Cgeq%201">. Using this concept, start with <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R%20%3D%20k%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">, a polynomial ring over an algebraically closed field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">. It follows that for any ideal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I%20%5Csubset%20R%2C"></p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BIV%7D%5Cleft%28%20I%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Csqrt%7BI%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>Although it might not be obvious from the statement using radicals, this theorem marks the beginning of modern algebraic geometry (the study of geometric properties defined by algebraic equations), since it states the basic result that a family of polynomials over an algebraically closed field has a common solution when the ideal they generate in the polynomial ring is proper. In general, a proper ideal does not necessarily need to have a non-empty zero locus, so the theorem in part is making the non-trivial statement that a proper ideal does have a non-empty zero locus if we assume that the field is algebraically closed. If we think of a system of polynomial equations which we are looking to solve simultaneously in the appropriate field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?F_%7Bi%7D%5Cleft%28%20x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%29">, the set of solutions is the zero locus for the system. The key is that increasing the size of the system does not affect the zero locus, so we can take everything to be a member of an ideal in the polynomial ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">. The Hilbert basis theorem already tells us that an ideal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I%20%5Csubset%20k%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> is finitely generated, so studying the zero locus of an ideal is the same as studying the zero locus of a set of polynomials.</p>
<p>Another important area of commutative algebra which Noether contributed to is the theory of decompositions of ideals in Noetherian rings, where the decompositions are primary since they break ideals up into primary ideals. Recall that integers can be factorised into products of prime numbers and powers of prime numbers. This concept of factorisation generalises to ideals, since one can have prime ideals and ideals which are analogous to products of prime powers. A ideal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bp%20%5Csubset%7DA"> is prime if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bp%20%5Cneq%7D%5Cleft%28%201%20%5Cright%29"> and if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7Bxy%7D"> belonging to the ideal implies that either <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x"> or <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y"> by themselves belongs to the ideal. <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bp%7D"> is prime if and only if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A%2F%5Cmathfrak%7B%5Ctext%7Bp%5C%20%7D%7D">is an integral domain. One might be able to guess this equivalence, since a ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?R"> is an integral domain if for two elements <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y"> of the ring, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?xy%20%3D%200"> implies that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%20%3D%200"> or <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y%20%3D%200">. A classic example is the ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BZ%7D"> (also obviously a commutative ring), since the product of two integers coming out as zero implies that one of those integers has to be zero. Another example is the polynomial ring <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathbb%7BZ%7D%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20t%20%5Cright%5Crbrack">: one can show by a contradiction that one cannot have <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?fg%20%3D%200"> such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?f%2Cg%20%5Cneq%200">. Another way of stating this is that the domain has no zero-divisors, since one cannot find a non-zero element which can be multiplied with another non-zero element to get zero. As well as prime ideals, one can have primary ideals. An ideal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I%20%5Csubset%20A"> is primary if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I%20%5Cneq%20A"> and if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7Bxy%7D"> belonging to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A%2FI"> implies that either <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x"> is in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I"> or <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?y%5E%7Bn%7D"> is in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I"> for some positive <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?n">.</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?I%20%3D%20%5Cbigcap_%7Bi%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%5Cmathfrak%7Bq%7D_%7Bi%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>Not all ideals have primary decompositions, but Noether proved the basic existence theorem that every ideal contained in a Noetherian ring has a primary decomposition. The proof is an application of an argument by Noetherian induction and also makes use of the concept of an indecomposable ideal. An ideal is indecomposable if it cannot be written as the intersection of two larger ideals, so a prime ideal is obviously indecomposable. There are also several options for uniqueness or partial uniqueness theorems. For example, take a decomposable ideal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%7D"> and write out a minimal primary decomposition for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%7D">:</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%20%3D%7D%5Cbigcap_%7Bi%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%5Cmathfrak%7Bq%7D_%7Bi%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>A decomposition is said to be minimal if the radicals <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?r%5Cleft%28%20%5Cmathfrak%7Bq%7D_%7Bi%7D%20%5Cright%29"> are all distinct and if the intersection <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cbigcap_%7Bj%20%5Cneq%20i%7D%5E%7B%5C%20%7D%5Cmathfrak%7Bq%7D_%7Bi%7D"> is not properly contained in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bq%7D_%7Bi%7D">. Any primary decomposition can be reduced to a minimal decomposition. Take <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bp%7D_%7Bi%7D%20%3D%20r%5Cleft%28%20%5Cmathfrak%7Bq%7D_%7Bi%7D%20%5Cright%29">, then the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bp%7D_%7Bi%7D"> are the prime ideals in the set of ideals <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?r%5Cleft%28%20%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%3A%7Dx%20%5Cright%29"> and so do not depend on the decomposition which is chosen for <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%7D">. Recall that a famous uniqueness result exists for the integers known as the fundamental theorem of arithmetic (first proved by Euclid): every integer is either a prime number or can be decomposed into a product of prime numbers which is unique modulo ordering. The fundamental theorem was used by Gauss to prove another basic result, the quadratic reciprocity law. This states that for any odd prime number <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?q">, the following relation holds</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20%5Cfrac%7Bp%7D%7Bq%7D%20%5Cright%29%5Cleft%28%20%5Cfrac%7Bq%7D%7Bp%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cleft%28%20-%201%20%5Cright%29%5E%7B%5Cfrac%7Bp%20-%201%7D%7B2%7D%5C%20%5Cfrac%7Bq%20-%201%7D%7B2%7D%7D"><br></p></center>
<p>where the Legendre symbol <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%28%20%5Cfrac%7Bq%7D%7Bp%7D%20%5Cright%29"> is defined as <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?0"> if <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?q"> divides <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?p">, <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?1"> if the modular equation <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?x%5E%7B2%7D%20%5Cequiv%20q%5C%20%5Cleft%28%20%5Ctext%7Bmod%5C%20%7Dp%20%5Cright%29"> has a solution, and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?-%201"> otherwise. The last two possibilities are equivalent to <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?q"> being a quadratic residue<a href="#fn27" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref27" role="doc-noteref"><sup>27</sup></a>.</p>
<p>For another uniqueness theorem, take a decomposable ideal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%7D">. Let <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cbigcap_%7Bi%20%3D%201%7D%5E%7Bn%7D%5Cmathfrak%7Bq%7D_%7Bi%7D"> be a minimal primary decomposition of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%7D"> and <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7B%20%5Cmathfrak%7Bp%7D_%7Bi_%7B1%7D%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20%5Cmathfrak%7Bp%7D_%7Bi_%7Bn%7D%7D%20%5Cright%5C%7D"> be a set of prime ideals of <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%7D">. It follows that the intersection <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bq%7D_%7Bi_%7B1%7D%7D%20%5Ccap%20%5Cldots%5C%20%20%5Ccap%20q_%7Bi_%7Bm%7D%7D"> does not depend on the decomposition. As a corollary, the primary components <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bq%7D_%7Bi%7D"> corresponding to minimal prime ideals are uniquely determined by <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%7D">. The term ‘isolated’ originates in algebraic geometry, as does the Spec notation, referring to a type of topological space known as a ‘prime spectrum of a ring’ where the topology is the Zariski topology. In algebraic geometry, one typically considers objects known as varieties, where a variety is a geometric object cut out by a set of homogenous polynomial equations in one or more variable</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cleft%5C%7B%20P_%7B1%7D%5Cleft%28%20x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bd%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%20%5Cldots%20%3D%20P_%7Bk%7D%5Cleft%28%20x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bd%7D%20%5Cright%29%20%3D%200%20%5Cright%5C%7D."><br></p></center>
<p>If <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A%20%3D%20k%5Cleft%5Clbrack%20x_%7B1%7D%2C%5C%20%5Cldots%2C%5C%20x_%7Bn%7D%20%5Cright%5Crbrack"> for a field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?k">, the ideal <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Ba%7D"> cuts out a variety <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X">. The minimal prime components <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cmathfrak%7Bp%7D_%7Bi%7D"> correspond to the components of the variety which are irreducible, whereas the embedded primes correspond to sub-varieties which are embedded in the irreducible components. Although we have said that the isolated primary components are determined uniquely by the ideal, this is not generally true of the embedded primary components<a href="#fn28" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref28" role="doc-noteref"><sup>28</sup></a>.</p>
<p>As with the example of Noetherian topological spaces, there are other ‘Noetherian’ objects which follow the type of finiteness condition associated with Noether. For example, in algebraic geometry a scheme <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> is said to be Noetherian if it has a finite cover by affine set</p>
<center><p><br><img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X%20%3D%20%5Cbigcup_%7Bi%7D%5E%7B%5C%20%7DU_%7Bi%7D%2C%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5C%20%5Ctext%7Bwith%5C%20%7DU_%7Bi%7D%20%3D%20Spec%5C%20A_%7Bi%7D%2C%5C%20"><br></p></center>
<p>such that the <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A_%7Bi%7D"> are Noetherian rings. This is equivalent to a scheme having a kind of finiteness condition on its dimensionality. If the affine scheme <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BSpec%5C%20%7DA"> is Noetherian, then it can be proved that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?A"> is a Noetherian ring using one of those standard arguments with a chain of ideals which you are hopefully getting used to hearing about<a href="#fn29" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref29" role="doc-noteref"><sup>29</sup></a>.</p>
<p>A famous theorem of Noether relates to invariants computed in variational problems: it is usually known as Noether’s theorem and it is one of the most important results in the history of mathematics and theoretical physics (modern physics would certainly not have developed in the same direction without this theorem)<a href="#fn30" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref30" role="doc-noteref"><sup>30</sup></a>. <p>such that <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?u_%7B%2A%7D"> is a minimizer for this functional and let this functional be invariant under transformations specified by the following maps</p>

Assume that there exists a majorant <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?h"> in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?L%5E%7Bp%7D%5Cleft%28%20%5COmega%20%5Cright%29"> such that</p>

<p>almost everywhere in <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5COmega">, where</p>

<p>are known as the Noether multipliers. This essentially means that invariances of a functional which can be differentiated give rise to conservation laws. In more quantitative terms, the invariances of the functional give rise to differential equations which a minimizer of the functional must satisfy<a href="#fn31" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref31" role="doc-noteref"><sup>31</sup></a>.</p>

Start with a one-parameter group of motions <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Cvarphi_%7Bs%7D%3AM%20%5Crightarrow%20M"> on <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?M">, which generates a vector field <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?X"> by</p>

<p>when the derivative is evaluated at <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?s%20%3D%200">. If this looks strange, recall that vector fields are operators on the space of smooth functions such that a vector field acting on a real-valued function is another real-valued function <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%5Ctext%7BXf%7D">. The tangent map <img style="vertical-align:middle" src="https://latex.codecogs.com/png.latex?%28%7B%5Cvarphi_%7Bs%7D%29%7D_%7B%2A%7D"> induces a local flow on the tangent bundle and the Lagrangian is invariant under the one-parameter group if</p>

The one-parameter group is usually known as a symmetry group for the system, hence there is a physical relationship between the conservation laws of a system and its mathematical symmetries<a href="#fn32" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref32" role="doc-noteref"><sup>32</sup></a>.</p>
<p>I should emphasise that this has been an extremely brief and selective survey from Noether’s body of work and given the space constraints I have chosen to focus on her most basic and important contributions to algebra and mathematical physics. Noether worked in many other areas besides commutative algebra and I have not touched upon her other contributions: see, for example, her work on early algebraic topology and representation theory of groups and algebras.</p>
